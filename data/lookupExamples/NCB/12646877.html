Nature Cell Biology 5, 261 - 263 (2003)
Published online: 01 March 2003 | doi:10.1038/ncb944

The spindle-associated transmembrane protein Axs identifies a membranous structure ensheathing the meiotic spindle

Joseph Kramer & R. Scott Hawley

Stowers Institute for Medical Research, Kansas City, MO 64110, USA

Correspondence should be addressed to R. Scott Hawley RSH@Stowers-Institute.org
Mutations in the aberrant X segragation (Axs) gene disrupt the segregation of achiasmate chromosomes during female meiosis in Drosophila melanogaster. We show that Axs encodes the founding member of an eukaryotic family of transmembrane proteins. Axs protein colocalizes with components of the endoplasmic reticulum and is present within a structure ensheathing the meiotic spindle. In both meiotic and mitotic cells, Axs is recruited to the microtubules of assembling spindles. We propose that Axs and the sheath represent novel mediators of meiotic spindle assembly and chromosome segregation.
Oocyte maturation is accompanied by the initiation of two parallel processes: resumption of the meiotic cell cycle and changes in the oocyte cytoplasm that prepare it for activation and fertilization1. Extensive restructuring of the endoplasmic reticulum occurs coincident with maturation in a wide range of organisms, including Xenopus laevis, mouse and sea urchin2, 3, 4. These changes are thought to correlate with changes in calcium physiology that are important for oocyte activation. However, relatively little is known about how this re-organization affects the meiotic cell cycle. Electron microscopic studies of spermatocytes from the wolf spider Lycosa have identified a membranous structure resembling endoplasmic reticulum that encases the meiotic spindle and chromosomes5, 6. Here we identify Axs as a protein component of a similar structure that ensheaths the female meiotic spindle of Drosophila and show that perturbation of this component results in defects in spindle assembly and chromosome segregation.

The mapping of the Axs gene by positional cloning was verified by the identification of mutational lesions of known alleles and by transgene rescue7 (see Supplementary Information, Methods). The Axs protein is predicted to contain eight strong transmembrane segments and identifies a new family of transmembrane proteins present in all major kingdoms except Monera. D. melanogaster has four such polypeptides and Homo sapiens possesses at least six. The Schizosaccharomyces pombe and Saccharomyces cerevisiae genomes also seem to possess an Axs-like gene. No significant homology is observed outside of the transmembrane core, possibly indicating diverse ligand and signalling potentials among different family members.

We identified the mutational lesion responsible for the AxsD mutation and engineered a cDNAconstruct under the control of the GAL4/UAS driver (see Methods). Expression of the AxsD cDNA in the germline resulted in high levels of achiasmate-specific chromosome non-disjunction that paralleled the defects observed in the original AxsD mutant7. However, expression of an Axs cDNA in somatic tissues of the adult female (for example, follicle cells) failed to elicit segregation defects. No such effect was observed when wild-type Axs was expressed in the germline or the soma (Fig. 1a).

Expression of Axs in the germline is also associated with defects in meiotic chromosome positioning and meiotic spindle structure (Fig. 1b). AxsD spindles are shorter than their wild-type counterparts (AxsD = 10.4 2.4 m (n = 47); wild-type = 14.9 2.8 m (n = 55)) and exhibit a barrel-shaped structure. Comparison of the ratio of spindle length and spindle width suggests that the spindle mid-zone in AxsD spindles does not undergo the tightening associated with late stages of spindle morphogenesis (AxsD = 2.2; wild-type = 4.9)8.

Overexpression of AxsD impairs the symmetric placement of the chromosomal complement, a cytological landmark for stable meiosis I metaphase arrest (Fig. 1b)9. Chromosome distribution was symmetrical in 79% of the wild-type figures examined (n = 71). However, in AxsD spindles, the entire chromosomal complement was only rarely arranged in a completely symmetrical manner (6% (n = 52)). Notably, the chiasmate (exchange) chromosomal mass was often observed to be symmetrically stretched across the metaphase plate, whereas achiasmate (nonexchange) chromosomes failed to be positioned properly.

Overexpression of AxsD also increased the incidence of anaphase in AxsD oocytes (wild-type = 2% (n = 98); AxsD = 14% (n = 86)). Such figures often contained 16 distinct chromosomal masses, each with a centromere, as assessed by labelling with the centromere antigen, Cid10 (Fig. 1b and data not shown). These figures probably reflect the release of sister chromatid cohesion at the metaphase–anaphase transition of meiosis II. Thus, AxsD spindles are apparently unable to achieve or maintain an arrested metaphase configuration.

Axs protein is associated with the nuclear surface of both the nurse cells and the germinal vesicle (oocyte nucleus) of late-stage egg chambers (Fig. 1c and data not shown). Double labelling with lamin, wheat germ agglutinin and Axs indicated that Axs is localized to the outer nuclear membrane of the germinal vesicle. Myc-tagged Axs and the resident endoplasmic reticulum protein Bip also colocalized throughout the syncytial cell cycles in embryos, with prominent accumulations appearing near the spindle poles during mitosis (Fig. 1d and data not shown). Thus, Axs is distributed within the endoplasmic reticulum of the germinal vesicle just before meiotic spindle assembly, positioning it nearby the assembling spindle during entry into prometaphase.

After germinal vesicle breakdown, Axs associated with the developing spindle through all stages of assembly. Before the formation of distinct spindle poles, Axs localized to the outer surface of the karyosome as microtubules emanated from the chromatin. Axs formed a sheath that encapsulated the spindle as poles were established and tapered into a fusiform shape. As spindles matured and achieved metaphase arrest, Axs remained associated with the central spindle and was often absent from the polar regions (Fig. 1e). This localization was maintained on entry into anaphase I (data not shown).

The localization of Axs to the meiotic spindle suggests that it had affinity for microtubules. In addition, the distribution of Axs during oogenesis and early embryogenesis supports this view. A punctate distribution of Axs was found associated with both the oocyte and nurse cell cortex (Fig. 2a). These particles were often found upon or in close association along microtubule bundles (Fig. 2a, arrowheads). We also examined early embryos expressing an Axs–green fluorescent protein (GFP) fusion. Simultaneous visualization of live Axs–GFP embryos injected with rhodamine–tubulin showed that Axs is recruited to the nuclear surface from cytoplasmic pools coincident with the changes in microtubule dynamics associated with prometaphase spindle assembly11 (Fig. 2b; t = 0, t = 300). Similar to the changes observed during meiotic metaphase I, Axs became tightly associated with the mature mitotic spindle (t = 495), remained associated with the spindle surface throughout anaphase and persisted through telophase, labelling the surface of mid-body remnants (Fig. 2b; t = 675, t = 765).

Finally, through the use of the microtubule-depolymerizing agent, colchicine, we examined the localization of Axs in the absence of detectable microtubules. Mock-treated embryos labelled with the centrosomal antigen, centrosomin12, showed discrete pericentrosomal accumulations of Axs and fully formed mitotic spindles (Fig. 2c and inset). Colchicine treatment resulted in the disassembly of the microtubules and disruption of the pericentrosomal Axs localization (Fig. 2c, arrowheads). Under these conditions, the peri-centrosomal accumulations were often observed to fuse and lose their apical orientation (Fig. 2c, arrow and data not shown). Together with the distribution of the protein during meiotic spindle assembly, these data suggest that Axs is re-organized coincident with changes in bulk microtubule distribution.

These data suggest that in addition to the cytoplasmic restructuring of endoplasmic reticulum thought to be important for oocyte activation, the endoplasmic reticulum may also be re-organized into structures that more directly impinge on the control of spindle assembly and meiotic progression.
