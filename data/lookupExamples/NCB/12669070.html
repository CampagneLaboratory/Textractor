Spontaneous cell polarization: undermining determinism

Roland Wedlich-Soldner & Rong Li

Roland Wedlich-Soldner and Rong Li are in the Department of Cell Biology, Harvard Medical School, 240 Longwood Avenue, Boston, MA 02115, USA

Correspondence should be addressed to Rong Li rong_li@hms.harvard.edu
It is widely observed that eukaryotic cells can polarize spontaneously in the absence of pre-established asymmetric cues. This phenomenon indicates that the principle of self-organization may be central to the establishment of cell polarity. Modelling work, as well as recent experimental data from several organisms, suggests that a combination of local positive feedback loops and global inhibitors could result in robust cell symmetry breaking through amplification of minute, stochastic variations.
In the past years, there has been notable progress in our understanding of the principles that guide the process of cell polarization. However, until recently the study of cell polarity has mostly emphasized the importance of asymmetric stimuli. A large amount of work has been devoted to the elucidation of the molecular links between regulators of cell polarity and the asymmetric cues that come from the environment or the cell's history. This has led to the unravelling of several signalling cascades consisting of conserved molecular constituents, such as heterotrimeric G proteins, Rho family GTPases and specific phospholipids1, 2, 3. These signalling cascades recognize pre-existing spatial asymmetries and modulate the dynamics of the actin and microtubule cytoskeletons, resulting in major structural reorganization of the cell. Although the molecular connections emphasize a stepwise development of cell polarity starting from recognition of pre-existing spatial cues, a competing view has now emerged that cells have an intrinsic ability to break symmetry that is perhaps dependent solely on their internal biochemical states4. This view is built on many observations that cells can polarize spontaneously, albeit in random orientations, in the absence of any obvious spatial cues. Experimental and modelling work has begun to focus on the mechanisms of spontaneous cell polarization that could be fundamental to cellular morphogenesis. In this article we will re-examine some of the known examples of spontaneous cell polarization (Fig. 1) and discuss the potential mechanisms behind them.

If presented with a uniform concentration of chemoattractant, both polarize and move in a random direction. A widely occurring phenomenon
Because in most physiological processes cell polarization occurs towards a defined orientation in response to spatial cues, spontaneous cell polarization can often be appreciated only when the cue is not spatially confined or when there is a deficiency in cue recognition (Fig. 1). A well-known example occurs during chemotropic responses. Chemotactic cells, such as neutrophils, the slime mold Dictyostelium discoideum or mating yeast cells are able to orientate themselves and move along or, in the case of yeast, grow towards a gradient of chemoattractant1, 3. The gradient provides an external chemical signal that is asymmetrically distributed and is thought to establish an asymmetric activation of heterotrimeric G proteins that are bound to chemoattractant receptors on the cell surface5. The G proteins in turn signal to a cascade of downstream targets, such as phospholipid kinases and Rho family small GTPases, resulting in asymmetric assembly of actin cytoskeleton structures required for motility or growth3, 6. However, if chemotactic cells are exposed to a uniform concentration of chemoattractant, which presumably results in uniform G-protein activation, cells are still able to polarize and initiate motility or form mating projections, albeit in random directions7. This observation suggests that a role for chemoattractant is to activate an intrinsic ability of cells to break symmetry.

Clearly, in processes such as chemotaxis or yeast mating, the directionality of polarization is vitally important. However, in certain developmental processes where establishing a single axis for cell fate specification is perhaps more important than the orientation of the axis, at least under laboratory conditions, spontaneous polarization may be an adequate default. The zygotes of fucuid algae polarize in response to unilateral light exposure, resulting in an asymmetric cell division that produces the rhizoid and thallus cells8. Relatively little is known about the molecular components that are required for polarization, although it is thought that calcium ions are of central importance and that components of the cell wall, the actin cytoskeleton and secretion machinery are also involved9, 10. If, however, no directional stimulus, such as light, is available for several hours after fertilization, polarization of fucuid zygotes occurs spontaneously, still resulting in specification of rhizoid and thallus cell types. Another classic example, involving not the actin but the microtubule cytoskeleton, is the cortical rotation of amphibian eggs. After fertilization of Xenopus laevis eggs, the egg cortex rotates relative to the cytoplasm, resulting in the formation of a dorsal-determining activity opposite the sperm entry point11. Normally, the site of sperm entry determines the direction of rotation; however, if eggs are activated by a uniform stimulus, cortical rotation occurs in a randomly chosen direction, a process that is adequate for establishing a normal dorsal–ventral polarity12.

Finally, a case of spontaneous cell polarization occurs during bud formation in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. At the G1–S phase transition of each cell cycle, yeast cells normally polarize to form a bud in a direction specified by a remnant from the previous round of budding, the bud scar. Haploid cells form new buds adjacent to the previous bud scar, resulting in an axial budding pattern. In contrast, diploid cells form new buds alternating between both cell poles, resulting in a bipolar budding pattern. Genetic analysis has revealed intricate signalling pathways that confer bud site selection13, 14. A central player of these pathways is a small GTPase termed Bud1. The current model is that Bud1 is activated near the presumptive bud site, recruiting a protein termed Cdc24, which functions as the guanine nucleotide-exchange factor (and thus an activator) for another small GTPase, Cdc42 (ref. 15). Cdc42 is required for cell polarization in many cell types and, once activated, it induces multiple downstream events, such as actin polymerization and polarized secretion16, 17. Interestingly, mutations that abolish bud-site selection, such as deletion of BUD1, do not inhibit budding per se, but only randomize the bud site18. More surprisingly, simply expressing a constitutively active form of Cdc42 in a non-polarized G1-arrested cell results in cell polarization in a random orientation19, 20, 21. These findings indicate that localized activation of Cdc42 directed by the spatial cue is not required for cell polarization and that a high level of activated Cdc42 may represent an internal biochemical state that favours spontaneous cell polarization.

Potential mechanisms
The examples of spontaneous polarization introduced above are mostly decades-old observations that have been somewhat overlooked because spontaneous processes are mechanistically less intuitive in this era of molecular pathways. Spontaneous cell polarization is an example of 'self organization', a concept that has broad implications in biology, ranging from molecular and subcellular processes to pattern formation during development or even to the organization of honey bee colonies or other state-forming insects22, 23. The classic theory of biological pattern formation first introduced by Meinhardt and Gierer in 1972 suggests that cells can perform an internal pattern-forming reaction using a combination of a self-enhancing local activator and a long-range inhibitor24, 25. The self-enhancing activator functions through positive feedback loops that could enhance stochastic local variations in a non-linear fashion, whereas the long-range inhibitor increases competition between different areas for activation. Recent work has begun to provide a molecular basis for the types of activators and inhibitors that could contribute to spontaneous cell symmetry breaking.

In two recent papers studying the self-polarization process in neutrophils, it was found that a positive feedback loop involving the lipid phosphatidylinositol-3,4,5-trisphosphate (PtdIns(3,4,5)P3) is sufficient to induce cell polarization and motility in the absence of chemoattractant26, 27. The experiments involved globally introducing PtdIns(3,4,5)P3 into cells by non-physiological means. PtdIns(3,4,5)P3, as detected by fluorescently tagged pleckstrin homology domains, first exhibited a uniform distribution in the plasma membrane, but within minutes became polarized to one end of the cell where F-actin-rich protrusive structures also appeared. The idea of a positive feedback loop involving PtdIns(3,4,5)P3 came from the observation that inhibition of phosphatidylinositol-3-OH kinase (PI(3)K), the enzyme required for the synthesis of PtdIns(3,4,5)P3, blocks the lipid-induced polarization. Thus, a positive feedback loop may be constructed between the lipid-dependent activation or recruitment of the lipid kinase and the synthesis of new lipids by this kinase (Fig. 2a)28, 29. This feedback loop could result in the enhancement of small stochastic variations in the initial distribution of PtdIns(3,4,5)P3. Interestingly, Rho-type GTPases and possibly F-actin are required for PtdIns(3,4,5)P3 polarization27, 30, suggesting that there is still much to learn about the details of this positive feedback loop.

Figure 2. Positive feedback loops that can drive spontaneous cell polarization.

a, A schematic representation of a positive feedback loop that is thought to operate in neutrophils27. PI(3)K synthesizes the signalling lipid PtdIns(3,4,5)P3, which in turn stimulates the activity of PI(3)K through pathways involving Rho-type small GTPases and possibly F-actin. b, Schematic representation of a positive feedback loop that allows polarization of G1-arrested budding yeast cells. GTP-bound Cdc42 on the plasma membrane stimulates actin cable formation through activation of formin-like proteins. The actin cables in turn allow Cdc42 to be delivered to the same site on the plasma membrane through type-V myosin. These two interdependent processes enable the cell to amplify minute stochastic asymmetries in the distribution of Cdc42 and establish a stable axis of polarity.

A potential candidate for a long-range inhibitor is the PI(3)-phosphatase PTEN, as demonstrated by experiments in Dictyostelium31. PTEN, which counteracts the activity of PI(3)K, is itself excluded from the leading edge, but is required for restricting PtdIns(3,4,5)P3 to this site. Additionally, activation of the myosin-II-dependent cortical contractile machinery away from the leading edge could also function as an inhibitor for establishing new protrusive structures. This possibility is suggested by the localization of myosin II, as well as the observation that inhibition of Rho-dependent kinase, which is required for activating myosin-II filament formation, also blocks the PtdIns(3,4,5)P3-induced cell polarization3, 26.

Rho-type GTPases and F-actin also seem to be key components of a self-polarization mechanism in yeast. As mentioned above, two reports suggest that simply overexpressing a constitutively active form of the Cdc42 GTPase in non-polarized G1 cells is sufficient to induce polarized distributions of Cdc42 itself and F-actin19, 20. Polarization in this assay is dependent on filamentous actin and formin-family proteins, which have been shown to nucleate actin cables in yeast and are dependent on Cdc42-GTP for activation32, 33. Recent work has followed up on this observation and demonstrated that formation of a polar Cdc42 cap is dependent on the actin cable and myosin-V-dependent transport of membrane-bound Cdc42-GTP. A mathematical simulation on the basis of this observation showed that a polarized distribution of Cdc42 could simply result from amplification of an initial stochastic variation of Cdc42 distribution on the plasma membrane, through a positive feedback loop between Cdc42-dependent actin cable formation and actin cable-based Cdc42 transport21 (Fig. 2b). The 'long-range inhibitor' could result from the modelling assumption that Cdc42 or actin is limiting. Additionally, recycling of Cdc42 from the plasma membrane through global endocytosis could also function as a long-range inhibitor.

Actin-dependent vesicular transport also seems to be a critical part of the mechanism underlying cell polarization in the zygotes of the fucuid algae. An important readout of cell polarization in this system is the creation of a cortical calcium gradient with its maximum on the shaded side of the zygote. It has been proposed that active transport of calcium channels along actin filaments is required for the establishment of the calcium gradient34. Experiments with brefeldin A, which blocks transport between the ER and the Golgi, indicated that secretion is also required for stabilization of the polarity axis. Spontaneous cell polarization in this system, therefore, could also be dependent on a feedback loop between actin polymerization and actin-dependent membrane transport. Finally, a recent report showed that the membrane fusion factor NSF (N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive fusion protein) is important for the maintenance of cell polarity in Dictyostelium cells35, further suggesting that the link between membrane trafficking and cell polarity may be conserved.

At first glance, the mechanism underlying the spontaneous cortical rotation of amphibian eggs that results in the establishment of the dorsal–ventral embryonic axis is very different from the ones discussed above, because this process is driven primarily by the microtubule cytoskeleton12, 36. Microtubule motor proteins of the kinesin family are also involved, either functioning directly at the cortex to produce rotational force, or indirectly by aligning microtubules into orientated arrays37, 38. The latter is suggested by in vitro studies showing that different microtubule structures can self-assemble using only tubulin and kinesins39, 40. Interestingly, 'dorsalizing' proteins, such as dishevelled have been shown to be transported along microtubules on vesicles41. Therefore, it is conceivable that, in analogy to the examples involving actin-dependent membrane trafficking, proteins transported along microtubules could in turn help to further increase microtubule assembly or attachment towards or at the site of their deposition.

Moving towards quantitative models
In the context of chemotaxis, the feedback loops and inhibitors such as those proposed by Meinhardt and Gierer have mainly been used to explain the exquisite sensitivity of cells towards gradients of chemoattractants7, 42. However, the same mechanisms that could amplify a minute difference of chemoattractant concentration created by a gradient could also amplify stochastic variations in the absence of a gradient. Thus, spontaneous cell polarization could simply reflect a powerful asymmetry amplification process, which, once initiated, renders the non-polarized state highly unstable. During normal physiological processes, the signals that induce polarization probably contain two elements: one to activate the spontaneous asymmetry amplification process and another to provide a spatial bias such that polarization occurs in a physiologically relevant orientation. These two functions must be coupled perfectly to ensure the fidelity of pattern formation. Although examples discussed above emphasize an involvement of cytoskeleton-dependent positive feedback loops, there may also be cytoskeleton-independent mechanisms that result in stochastic activation or enhancement of signalling pathways. In fact, efficient cell polarization may involve multiple interlocked positive feedback circuits. Further work is now required to elucidate the intrinsic cell-symmetry-breaking mechanisms at the molecular level, to elevate the present qualitative models to the level of quantitative assessment and to understand the dynamic coupling between cue-dependent signalling and the spontaneous polarization machinery.
