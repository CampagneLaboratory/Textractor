Ku70 suppresses the apoptotic translocation of Bax to mitochondria

Motoshi Sawada1, 2, 4, Weiyong Sun1, 2, 4, Paulette Hayes1, 2, Konstantin Leskov3, David A. Boothman3 & Shigemi Matsuyama1, 2

1 Blood Research Institute, The Blood Center of South Eastern Wisconsin, 8727 Watertown Plank Rd, Milwaukee, WI 53226, USA

2 Department of Biochemistry, Medical College of Wisconsin, 8727 Watertown Plank Rd, Milwaukee, WI 53226, USA

3 Departments of Radiation Oncology and Pharmacology, Case Western Reserve University and University Hospitals of Cleveland, BRB-326 East, 10900 Euclid Avenue, Cleveland, OH 44106-4942, USA

4 These authors contributed equally to this work.

Correspondence should be addressed to Shigemi Matsuyama smatsuyama@csew.edu
Bax induces mitochondrial-dependent cell death signals in mammalian cells. However, the mechanism of how Bax is kept inactive has remained unclear. Yeast-based functional screening of Bax inhibitors from mammalian cDNA libraries identified Ku70 as a new Bax suppressor. Bax-mediated apoptosis was suppressed by overexpression of Ku70 in mammalian cells, but enhanced by downregulation of Ku70. We found that Ku70 interacts with Bax, and that the carboxyl terminus of Ku70 and the amino terminus of Bax are required for this interaction. Bax is known to translocate from the cytosol to mitochondria when cells receive apoptotic stimuli. We found that Ku70 blocks the mitochondrial translocation of Bax. These results suggest that in addition to its previously recognized DNA repair activity in the nucleus, Ku70 has a cytoprotective function in the cytosol that controls the localization of Bax.
Bcl-2 family proteins regulate a distal step in an evolutionarily conserved pathway for apoptosis, with some members functioning as suppressors of apoptosis and others as promoters of cell death1, 2. In mammalian cells, Bcl-2 family proteins control mitochondria-dependent cell death cascades3, 4, 5. During apoptosis, mitochondria release apoptogenic factors such as cytochrome c into the cytosol, triggering Apaf-1-dependent caspase activation that results in cell death6, 7. Pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family proteins such as Bax promote release of cytochrome c from mitochondria8. In contrast, anti-apoptotic Bcl-2-family proteins such as Bcl-2 suppress cytochrome c release from mitochondria, thereby protecting cells from apoptotic stresses9, 10. Bax normally resides in the cytosol in a quiescent state. After apoptotic stimuli, however, Bax is translocated into mitochondria11 and promotes release of cytochrome c, possibly by forming a pore in the mitochondrial outer membrane12, 13.

Results
Ku70 inhibits Bax.
We used a yeast-based functional screening system14, 15 to search for Bax inhibitors and cloned human Ku70 as a potential Bax suppressor protein. Ku70 is the 70K subunit of Ku antigen, a heterodimeric complex composed of Ku70 and Ku80 (ref.16). Ku70 has been localized to both the cytosol and the nucleus17, is expressed ubiquitously in mammalian cells and is essential for non-homologous DNA double-strand break (DSB) repair16, 18. The heterodimerization domains between Ku80 and Ku70 have been localized to amino acids 1–115 and 430–482 in Ku70 (ref.19; Fig. 1a).

Full Figure and legend (71K)
We constructed yeast expression cDNA libraries using mRNA from HeLa cells and mouse brain tissue. Yeast-based functional screening of Bax inhibitors was performed as previously reported14, 15 and two individual clones were identified as Bax suppressors. These clones were found to encode amino acids 323–609 (clone 1; HeLa cell library) and 496–609 (clone 2; mouse brain library) of Ku70 (Fig. 1a). Human Ku70 mutant constructs, together with full-length human Ku70, were made in accordance with the mouse sequence of clone 2 and tested for their ability to inhibit Bax activity in mammalian cells (Fig. 1). Full-length Ku70 suppressed Bax-induced apoptosis (Fig. 1b, c; also see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1a; FACS analysis) and caspase activation (data not shown) in HEK293T cells as efficiently as X-linked inhibitor of apoptosis (XIAP), a potent cytoprotective protein that inhibits caspase activity20. Expression of full-length Ku70 also attenuated staurosporine (STS)-induced apoptosis (Fig. 2a) and caspase activation (Fig. 2b) in HeLa cells, and UVC-irradiation-induced cell death in HEK293T cells (Fig. 2c). Interestingly, the Ku70 mutant construct encoding amino acids 496–609 of Ku70 (Ku70496–609), which is equivalent to 'Bax-inhibitor clone 2' and lacks the Ku80-binding domain, retained anti-Bax activity in mammalian cells (Fig. 1d). These results suggest that Ku70–Ku80 complex formation is not required for the anti-Bax activity of Ku70. In fact, overexpression of Ku80 did not suppress Bax-induced apoptosis (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1b, c). Deletion of the C-terminal 74 amino acids of Ku70 (Ku701–535) resulted in a loss of Ku70's cytoprotective activity against Bax expression and STS treatment, whereas the C-terminal region alone (Ku70536–609) was sufficient to block Bax-, STS- or UVC-induced apoptosis (Figs 1d–f and 2a, c). These results suggest that the C-terminal region of Ku70 is required for its cytoprotective function. Expression of full-length Ku70 did not affect the expression level of endogenous Bax (data not shown), suggesting that Ku70 controls the Bax-mediated apoptotic signal rather than the cellular levels of Bax. Among the pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members, Bax and Bak are central to the induction of apoptosis, as evident by the fact that deletion of both these genes in mice results in resistance of derived cells to several cell death stimuli known to trigger mitochondria-dependent apoptosis21. In this regard, Ku70 could not suppress Bak-induced cell death in HEK293T cells (Fig. 1d), suggesting that Ku70 specifically controls Bax-mediated signalling. Cytochrome c release from mitochondria induced by Bax expression, STS treatment or UVC irradiation was attenuated by expression of Ku70 (Fig. 2d and data not shown). These results indicate that Ku70 suppresses cell death upstream of mitochondrial cytochrome c release.

Endogenous Ku70 has cytoprotective functions.
The expression of antisense Ku70 mRNA reduced levels of Ku70 in HEK293T and HeLa cells and induced hypersensitivity to Bax-mediated apoptosis induced by Bax expression (Fig. 3a), STS treatment (Fig. 3b) or UVC irradiation (Fig. 3c). Furthermore, mycoplasma-free SV40-transformed mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) derived from Ku70-deficient mice also showed increased sensitivities to apoptotic stimuli, such as STS treatment and UVC irradiation, in contrast to genetically matched Ku70-proficient MEFs (Fig. 3f–h). However, antisense Ku70 mRNA treatment did not change the sensitivities of HeLa cells to 'death-receptor-mediated apoptosis', such as Fas- (Fig. 3d) and TRAIL-induced apoptosis (Fig. 3e). This suggests that the hypersensitivity to Bax-mediated apoptosis induced by Ku70 deficiency (Fig. 3a–c) was not merely the result of non-specific cellular damage caused by the absence of the Ku70 gene. These results suggest that Ku70 has a physiological role as an inhibitor of Bax-mediated apoptosis.

Ku70 interacts with Bax.
We found that endogenous Ku70 and Bax co-immunoprecipitate each other (HEK293T cell, Figs 4a, b and see Supplementary Information, Fig. S2a–d; mouse brain tissue, see Supplementary Information, Fig. S3a, b; mouse fibroblasts, see Supplementary Information, Fig. S3c, d), suggesting that Ku70 interacts with Bax. We did not observe any significant interaction between Ku70 and Bak, or between Ku80 and Bax (data not shown). Co-immunoprecipitation experiments of endogenous Ku70 and Bax were performed in detergent-free buffer for the cytosol fraction (Fig. 4a, b; also see Supplementary Information, Figs S2a, b and S3a, b), and in Chaps buffer for total cell lysates (see Supplementary Information, Figs S2c, d and S3c, d), in accordance with a previous report showing the effects of detergents on immunoprecipitation22. This study shows that in co-immunoprecipitation analysis of Bcl-2-family proteins, the presence of certain detergents, with the exception of Chaps, caused artifactual interactions22. To determine if other proteins are involved in Ku70–Bax complex formation, we performed autoradiography of immunoprecipitated samples using radiolabelled (35S-methionine and 35S-cysteine) cell lysates prepared from healthy cells without apoptotic stimuli (Fig. 4c). Two proteins with relative molecular masses (Mr) of 21K and 70K were immunoprecipitated with an anti-Bax antibody, whereas three proteins of 21K, 70K and 86K were immunoprecipitated with an anti-Ku70 antibody. The molecular weight of Bax, Ku70, and Ku80 are 21K, 70K and 86K, respectively, suggesting that the bands most probably correspond to these proteins. Although further biochemical analysis is clearly needed, these observations suggest that Bax and Ku70 interact directly, and that Ku70 does not bind simultaneously to Bax and Ku80. The amount of Bax precipitated by anti-Bax antibody and anti-Ku70 antibody was almost equivalent (Fig. 4c; also see Supplementary Information, Fig. 3e), suggesting that a large proportion of the Bax population is associated with Ku70 in normal cells.

To identify the Ku70-binding domain of Bax, we examined the binding activities of several deletion mutants of Bax fused to green fluorescent protein (GFP; Fig. 4d). Endogenous Ku70 was pulled down by full-length Bax–GFP, as well as by deletion mutants of the second -helix (containing the 'death-inducing' BH3 domain)1, 2 and the ninth -helix (transmembrane domain, mitochondria-targeting domain)23, but not by mutants lacking N-terminal sequences (53 amino acids) before the second -helix (Bax-N). These results suggest that the N terminus of Bax is required for its interaction with Ku70. We also examined whether the C terminus of Ku70, which retains cytoprotective activity, can bind to Bax. Flag-tagged Ku70, Ku70496–609 and Ku70536–609, but not Ku701–535, bound to Bax, suggesting that the Bax-binding domain is localized in the C terminus of Ku70 (Fig. 4e). Furthermore, Ku70 did not suppress the apoptosis-inducing activity of Bax-N in DU145 cells, a Bax-deficient prostate cancer cell line24 (Fig. 5a). These results suggest that the N terminus of Bax and the C terminus of Ku70 are required for the interaction of Bax and Ku70.

The Ku70 C terminus does not suppress apoptosis in Bax-deficient cells.
To examine whether Ku70 inhibits apoptotic signals other than Bax-mediated signals, we examined the anti-apoptotic activity of Ku70 in DU145 cells24 (Fig. 5b, c). STS triggers the mitochondria-dependent cell death pathway through pro-apoptotic Bcl-2-family proteins such as Bax and Bak21. Thus, in Bax-deficient cells, STS is expected to induce apoptosis through pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family proteins such as Bak21, 24. Ku70, which protected Bax-expressing cells (HEK293T and HeLa cells) from the effects of STS (Figs 1 and 2), did not suppress STS-induced apoptosis in DU145 cells (Fig. 5b). Furthermore, downregulation of Ku70 levels did not induce hypersensitivity to STS in DU145 cells (Fig. 5c). In these experiments, the reduced levels of Ku70 induced by antisense Ku70 RNA in Bax-deficient cells were low enough to enhance Bax-induced apoptosis (Fig. 5c). In addition, overexpression of Ku70 in HeLa cells did not suppress apoptosis induced by 'extrinsic' cell death stimuli, such as Fas and TRAIL, which can induce apoptosis without mitochondrial cytochrome c release25 (Fig. 5d, e). These results support our hypothesis that Ku70 selectively inhibits Bax among several apoptotic signals.

Ku70 inhibits mitochondrial translocation of Bax.
Overexpression of Ku70 suppressed the mitochondrial translocation of Bax triggered by STS treatment (Fig. 6a) or UVC irradiation (Fig. 7). Ku70 was detected both in the cytosol and the nucleus under normal conditions, as previously reported17, but not in mitochondria-enriched fraction (heavy membrane (HM) fraction) (Figs 6 and 7). The elevation of Ku70 levels also suppressed the overexpression-induced accumulation of Bax in the mitochondrial fraction, whereas it increased the relative proportion of Bax in the cytosol (Fig. 6b). These results suggest that Ku70 has a role in sequestering Bax from mitochondria, thus protecting cells from death. Consistent with the hypothesis that Ku70 is a cytosol retention factor for Bax, an increased association of Bax protein with mitochondria was observed both in cells expressing Ku70 antisense RNA (Fig. 6d) and in Ku70-/- MEFs (Fig. 6e). Furthermore, mitochondrial translocation of Bax during STS-induced apoptosis occurred earlier in Ku70-/- MEFs (Fig. 6f). However, the dissociation of Ku70 from Bax may not allow mitochondrial Bax to reach levels sufficient to induce apoptosis, as a substantial amount of Bax remained in the cytosol in Ku70-deficient cells (Fig. 6d, e). In addition, the mitochondrial Bax in Ku70-deficient cells was not resistant to alkali (see Supplementary Information, Fig. 4a), suggesting that these Bax molecules are not activated26. These results suggest that there are multiple steps involved in activation of the Bax molecule (mitochondrial translocation and membrane insertion), in addition to the dissociation of Ku70.

Recently, a Bax mutant (BaxP13A) has been reported to show enhanced mitochondrial accumulation of Bax27. Overexpression of Ku70 did not suppress BaxP13A-induced apoptosis (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S4b) and Ku70 did not bind to BaxP13A (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S4c). These results suggest that Pro13 in the N terminus of Bax is at least required for binding of Ku70 to Bax and that BaxP13A may show enhanced mitochondrial translocation by escaping Ku70-mediated inhibition.

Ku70 levels are reduced during apoptosis.
Western blot analysis demonstrated that Ku70 levels decrease significantly during apoptosis (Figs 6a and 7a; a time course of Ku70 disappearance is shown in Supplementary Information, Fig. S4d). Importantly, only cytosolic Ku70 levels decreased, whereas nuclear Ku70 levels remained constant during apoptosis (Figs 6a and 7a), as previously observed in UV-induced apoptosis28. This change was unaffected by caspase inhibitor treatments (Figs 6c and 7a) that were effective in suppressing caspase substrates, such as PARP (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S4f). Fragments of Ku70 were not detected during UVC- or STS-induced apoptosis (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S4d, e). The disappearance of immunoreactive Ku70 on western blots may have been caused by caspase-independent proteolysis29 or post-translational modification of Ku70. As Ku70 binds and inhibits the mitochondrial translocation of Bax, the disappearance of immunoreactive Ku70 in the cytosol fraction may be one of the early caspase-independent events in apoptosis29 that causes the dissociation of Ku70 from Bax. Immunohistochemical experiments also confirmed that Ku70 inhibits the mitochondrial translocation of Bax (Fig. 7b).

Ku70 inhibits the apoptosis-induced conformational change of Bax.
It has been reported that exposure of the Bax N terminus occurs before mitochondrial translocation of the protein and that this phenomenon can be detected with the anti-Bax monoclonal antibody clone 6A7 (refs 23, 30). In fact, we confirmed that 6A7 antibody can immunoprecipitate Bax protein only in cells treated with apoptotic stimuli, such as UVC irradiation (Fig. 8a) and STS treatment (data not shown). We found that overexpression of Ku70 suppressed binding of the 6A7 antibody to Bax in the cells treated by UVC irradiation (Fig. 8a). In addition, the 6A7 antibody could not immunoprecipitate the Bax–Ku70 complex when Ku70 is overexpressed, whereas an anti-Bax polyclonal antibody (this antibody recognizes both inactive and active forms of Bax) could immunoprecipitate the complex (Fig. 8b). These results suggest that Ku70 suppresses exposure of the Bax N terminus. The epitope of 6A7 antibody is reported to be amino acids 13–24 of the N terminus (ref.30). Therefore, we thought that binding of Ku70 to Bax might explain why the N terminus of Bax is masked under normal conditions. However, this simple model failed to explain how Ku70 suppresses the conformational change of Bax because of the following observations. We tested whether a Ku70 deficiency can induce the exposure of the Bax N terminus by using Ku70-deficient MEFs. We found that Ku70 deficiency itself did not induce exposure of the Bax N terminus, but that absence of Ku70 enhanced this event after UVC irradiation (Fig. 8c). These results suggest that Ku70 can inhibit exposure of the Bax N terminus; however, the dissociation of Ku70 from Bax is not sufficient to activate Bax protein. These results also suggest the presence of factors other than Ku70 that prevent exposure of the Bax N terminus in normal cells. Thus, Ku70 deficiency alone is not sufficient for the apoptotic mitochondrial translocation of Bax (Fig. 6d, e).

Ku70 does not bind to activated Bax.
Next, we tested whether Ku70 can interact with activated Bax that has changed its conformation. As Ku70 levels decrease when cells are treated by apoptotic stimuli, this question cannot be examined in vivo. Therefore, we performed semi-in vitro experiments using cell lysates. Ku70 was overexpressed in DU145 cells and the cell lysates were prepared from these cells. Ku70-deficient MEFs were used to obtain Bax activated by UVC irradiation. The cell lysates prepared from Ku70-containing Bax-deficient cells and Bax-containing Ku70-deficient cells were mixed in vitro and the effect of apoptotic stimuli on the interaction of Bax and Ku70 was examined. Co-immunoprecipitation of Bax and Ku70 was performed with an anti-Bax polyclonal antibody that can immunoprecipitate the Ku70–Bax complex in normal cells. Apoptotic stress (UVC treatment) decreased the interaction of Bax and Ku70 (Fig. 8d, e). These results suggest that Ku70 cannot bind to the activated form of Bax that becomes detectable with the 6A7 antibody. In fact, the 6A7 antibody could not precipitate the Ku70–Bax complex under these conditions (data not shown). It has been reported that in vitro activation of Bax can be induced by using detergents such as NP40 (ref.22). Therefore, we also tested the effects of NP40 on the binding of Ku70 and Bax. The Ku70–Bax complex was immunoprecipitated in Chaps buffer with an anti-Bax polyclonal antibody and an anti-Ku70 monoclonal antibody, but not in NP40 buffer (Fig. 8f, g). In the NP40 buffer, Bax became detectable with the 6A7 antibody, even in the lysates of normal cells (Fig. 8h), as previously reported22. These results suggest that Ku70 cannot bind to the activated form of Bax. Combined with the observation that Ku70 can bind to the inactive form of Bax, our data suggest that Ku70 inhibits Bax-mediated apoptosis by preventing the activation process of Bax in the early phase of apoptosis. However, Ku70 cannot revert Bax-mediated death signals once Bax is activated.

Discussion
Ku70 has been recognized as a subunit of the Ku protein complex, which is important in non-homologous DNA DSB repair16, 18. The present study suggests that Ku70 has a Bax inhibitor activity that is independent from the previously recognized DNA damage repair function. Consistent with this hypothesis, it has been reported that fibroblasts derived from Ku70-deficient mice become hypersensitive to several anti-cancer drugs that do not induce DNA damage31. We also observed that Ku70-deficient cells showed increased sensitivity to STS treatment, which induces apoptosis regardless of DNA damage (Fig. 3). This phenotype of Ku70-deficient cells may be explained by the anti-Bax activity of Ku70. In addition, increased neuronal cell death in the developing brain of Ku70-deficient mice may be partly explained by the abnormal activation of Bax caused by the absence of Ku70, as Bax is a key component of neuronal apoptosis during development31, 32.

Previously, the presence of a cytosol retention signal in the N terminus of Bax has been suggested by in vitro experiments26. This report is consistent with our observation that binding of Bax to Ku70 is abolished by mutation or deletion of the Bax N terminus. Ku70 may be one of the cytosol retention factors that interacts with the N terminus of Bax. However, at present, we cannot exclude the possibility that an unexpected structural change caused by mutations in Bax protein induced dissociation of Ku70 from Bax. Further biochemical studies will be needed to examine whether the N terminus of Bax contains the domain for Ku70 binding. In this study, the binding of Ku70 and Bax was analysed by co-immunoprecipitation using cell lysates, and the present results suggest the possibility of a direct interaction between Bax and Ku70. However, as cell lysates contain several cellular components, we cannot completely exclude the possibility that factors other than Ku70 and Bax that cannot be detected in SDS–PAGE gels of 35S-labelled proteins are involved in complex formation. Future biochemical analysis using purified recombinant proteins should elucidate the molecular mechanism of this interaction.

The present data suggest that cytosolic Ku70 blocks the mitochondrial translocation of Bax by inhibiting exposure of the Bax N terminus (Figs 6 and 7). However, the absence of Ku70 was not sufficient for the apoptotic level of Bax translocation from the cytosol to mitochondria, when there are no apoptotic stresses (Fig. 6d, e). These results suggest the presence of other factors activated by apoptotic stimuli that enhances Bax relocation. One possibility is that Ku70 may not be the only cytosol retention factor of Bax. Dissociation of multiple cytosol retention factors from Bax may be required for the complete relocation of Bax from the cytosol to mitochondria. Another possibility is the presence of a factor that actively transports Bax into the mitochondrial membrane. In this regard, the C-terminal ninth -helix of Bax, which is not required for Ku70–Bax interaction, has been reported to be involved in mitochondrial targeting23. The combination of factors regulating cytosolic retention through the Bax N terminus and mitochondrial targeting through the Bax C terminus may set the threshold for apoptosis induction through Bax. In addition to this system, BH3-domain proteins such as Bid will also be critical; for example, in activation of Bax after its translocation to mitochondria21.

Bax levels in the nuclear fraction were increased by overexpression of Ku70 (Figs 6a and 7a), and were significantly reduced by lowering Ku70 levels (Fig. 6d, e). At present, we cannot exclude the possibility that changes in the association of cytosolic Bax with the nuclear membranes are the reason of this phenomenon. Although most of the studies examining the subcellular localization of Bax focus on the partitioning of Bax in the cytosol and mitochondria, it has been reported that Bax also exists in the nucleus33, 34, 35, 36. However, the biological activity of Bax in the nucleus is not understood. If Bax does exist in the nucleus, the possibility of a Bax–Ku70 interaction in the nucleus is an intriguing scenario.

In summary, we have found that Ku70 interacts with Bax and inhibits its mitochondrial translocation. Our data suggest that Ku70 has a physiological role in the regulation of Bax-mediated apoptosis, in addition to the previously known roles in DNA-damage repair. Several anti-cancer drugs are known to stimulate Bax-mediated apoptotic signals. Furthermore, irregular high expression levels of Ku70 in cancer cells have been reported37, 38. The elevated Ku70 levels may confer cancer cell resistance to anti-cancer drugs, triggering Bax-mediated apoptosis. Alternatively, rapid reduction of Ku70 levels occurs in the early phase of ischaemia-induced tissue damage39. Proteolysis of Ku70 may enhance Bax-mediated cell death in tissue damaged by ischaemia, and the regulation of Ku70 levels may alter the sensitivity of cells to the stresses that trigger intrinsic cell death signals.

Methods
Plasmids
The plasmids pGilda-Bax, pcDNA3-Bax (human), pcDNA3-Myc-XIAP, pcDNA3-Bcl-2 (human), and pcDNA3-Bcl-XL (human) have been described20, 40. Yeast expression plasmid libraries of cDNAs from HeLa cells (pJG4-5 vector; Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) and mouse brain (pYES vector, Invitrogen) were constructed using directional cDNA synthesis kit (Stratagene, Cedar Creek, TX) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The plasmid vectors pCMV-2B and pEGFP were purchased from Stratagene and Clontech (Palo Alto, CA), respectively, and human full-length Ku70 and the deletion mutants of Ku70 were subcloned into the BamH1 and Xho1 sites of pCMV-2B. The deletion mutants of Bax were subcloned into the EcoR1 and Sal1 sites of pEGFP. The full-length Ku70 cDNA was prepared by RT-PCR using HeLa cell cDNA. The mutant constructs of Ku70 and Bax described in this article were prepared by the second-step PCR mutagenesis method41.

Yeast
The yeast strain (EGY48) used for this study has been described previously40. Yeast-based functional screening of Bax-suppressors was performed using pGilda-Bax as the Bax expression plasmid in accordance with the previously described method14, 15.

Cell culture and detection of apoptosis
HEK293T and HeLa cells were cultured in MEM supplemented with 10% foetal bovine serum (FBS). MEFs and DU145 cells were cultured in DMEM containing 10% FBS. Transfection of plasmids was performed with SuperFect (Qiagen, Valencia, CA) in accordance with the manufacturer's instructions. Apoptosis was induced by transfection with pcDNA3-human Bax (1g for 106 cells), STS treatment (200nM), UVC irradiation (200J m-2 for HEK293T cells and 1200J m-2 for MEFs), anti-Fas treatment (CH11 antibody (1, 4, or 8 g ml-1 in Fig. 3d and 1g ml-1 in Fig. 5d) or TRAIL treatment (25, 50 or 100 ng ml-1 in Fig. 3e and 100 ng ml-1 in Fig. 5e). To examine the effects of Ku70 or antisense Ku70 (AS-Ku70), 106 cells were cotransfected with 0.5 g of pEGFP (GFP expression plasmid) to mark the transfected cells. One day after treatment, cells were stained with Hoechst dye and apoptotic nuclei were counted in GFP-expressing cells under a fluorescence microscope (three hundred cells were counted for each experiment), as previously reported21. Each point in figures showing percentages of apoptosis represents the mean s.e.m of three experiments. Caspase activities of cells were measured by detecting the cleavage of the fluorogenic caspase substrate DEVD-amino-4-trifluoromethylcoumarin (DEVD-afc), as previously described20. Anti-apoptotic activities of Ku70 and its mutants in Fig. 1b–f, Fig. 2a, c and Fig. 5a–e were examined as follows. Full-length and mutant Ku70 were expressed in the pCMV2B vector. Cells (106) were transfected by pCMV2B-plasmid. Unless indicated in the figure, 2g plasmid for HEK293T cells and 1g plasmid for HeLa and DU145 cells were used. One day after transfection, cells were treated with apoptotic stimuli. In the case of Bax overexpression, cells were transfected with plasmids expressing Bax and Ku70 (full length and mutants) and apoptosis was detected one day after transfection. The XIAP-expressing plasmid (pcDNA3-Myc-XIAP) was used as a control for apoptosis inhibitor in Fig. 1 (0.5, 1.0 or 2.0 g in Fig. 1b and 2g in Fig. 1d for 106 HEK293T cells) and Fig. 5a (1 g for 106 DU145 cells). Plasmids that express Bcl-2 (pcDNA3-Bcl2) and Bcl-xL (pcDNA3-Bcl-xL) were also used (1g for 106 DU145 cells) as a control in Fig. 5a. To decrease Ku70 levels in HEK293T and HeLa cells, cells (106) were transfected with 2g of pcDNA3(-)-antisense Ku70. One day after transfection, cells were treated with apoptotic stimuli, including Bax transfection. The vector plasmid of pcDNA3(-) was used as a negative control. Ku70 levels in antisense Ku70-treated cells and MEFs were confirmed by western blotting using 20 mg of protein cell lysates.

Cytochrome c detection
One day after pcDNA3-Bax transfection, STS treatment or UVC irradiation, cells were resuspended in 200l of homogenization buffer (250mM sucrose, 20mM Hepes at pH7.5, 10mM potassium chloride, 1.5mM magnesium chloride, 1mM EDTA and 0.1mM phenylmethylsulphonyl fluoride) and separation of the cytosol and heavy membrane fraction (containing mitochondria and ER) was performed as previously reported42, 43. A cytosolic fraction (20 1 protein) and 1 1 membrane fraction (out of 50 1 total) were analysed by western blotting with an anti-cytochrome c antibody (dilution 1:1000; BD-Pharmingen).

Immunoprecipitation
Co-immunoprecipitation of Ku70 and Bax: Transfected or untransfected HEK293T cells or DU145 cells (10-cm dish) were lysed in 200 l of Chaps buffer (150mM sodium chloride, 10mM Hepes at pH7.4 and 1.0% Chaps), detergent-free hypotonic buffer (hypotonic (5mM sodium chloride) PBS at pH7.4) or NP40 buffer (142.5mM potassium chloride, 5mM magnesium chloride, 1mM EGTA, 10mM Hepes at pH7.4 and 0.2% NP40) containing protease inhibitors (1:100 dilution of Protease Inhibitors Cocktail (Sigma, St Louis, MO), according to the previously reported method22. For the experiments in detergent-free conditions, the cytosol fraction was used and sodium chloride was added to prepare the isotonic condition before immunoprecipitation as previously reported22. Immunoprecipitation was performed as follows, in accordance with previous methods22, 38. After preclearing of 200l of the sample with 20l Protein A–Sepharose (CL-4B, 17-0780-01; Amersham Biosciences, Piscataway, NJ) at 4°C for 1h, immunoprecipitations were performed by incubating 200 l of lysates with 2 g of anti-Bax polyclonal antibody (Cat. #13666E; BD-Pharmingen) or 2 g of anti-Ku70 monoclonal antibody (Cat. #69141A; BD-Pharmingen) at 4°C for 2h. In some cases, 2 g of anti-Bax monoclonal antibody (clone 6A7, BD-Pharmingen) was used to detect the active form of Bax. Immunocomplex in 200 l lysates was precipitated with 20 l of Protein A–Sepharose. After extensive washing in the buffer, beads were boiled in 40 l Laemmli buffer and 20 1 of the eluted proteins were analysed by western blotting. Normal rabbit serum (NRS) and mouse IgG were used as negative controls. Western blot analysis of pre-immunoprecipitation (20 g protein, Input) and immunoprecipitated samples (IP) were performed with an anti-Ku70 monoclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen) or an anti-Bax polyclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen).

Co-immunoprecipitation of GFP–Bax and Ku70: co-immunoprecipitation of GFP–Bax and Ku70. HEK293T cells (10-cm dish) were transfected with 10 g pEGFP, pEGFP–Bax, pEGFP–Bax-N, pEGFP–Bax-2, pEGFP–Bax-9 or pEGFP–BaxP13A in the presence of 50 M z-VAD-fmk. One day after transfection, cells were lysed in 200 l of Chaps buffer and co-immunoprecipitation experiments of GFP–Bax and endogenous Ku70 were performed. An anti-GFP polyclonal antibody (Cat. #R970-01, Invitrogen; 2 g for 200 l sample) was used for immunoprecipitation (12% SDS–PAGE) and an anti-Ku70 monoclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen) for the detection of Ku70 (10% SDS–PAGE). Bax-N (deletion of amino acids 1–53), Bax-2 (deletion of amino acids 33–71), Bax-9 (deletion of amino acids 170–192) and BaxP13A were prepared using second-step PCR-based mutagenesis methods, as previously reported27.

Co-immunoprecipitation of Flag–Ku70 and endogenous Bax: HEK293T cells (10-cm dish) were transfected with 10g pCMV-2B-control vector (Flag-tagged firefly luciferase), pCMV-2B-Ku70wt (Flag–Ku70wt), pCMV-2B-Ku701–535 (Flag–Ku701–535), pCMV-2B-Ku70496–609(Flag–Ku70496–609) or pCMV-2B-Ku70536–609 (Flag–Ku70536–609 Co-immunoprecipitation was performed in Chaps buffer with an anti-Flag monoclonal antibody (Cat. #200472, Stratagene; 2 g for 200 l sample) and western blotting of Bax (15% SDS–PAGE) was performed with an anti-human Bax polyclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen).

Autoradiography of immunoprecipitated samples
HEK293T cells labelled with 35S-methionine and 35S-cysteine for 24h were lysed in 200 l of Chaps buffer containing protease inhibitors. Immunoprecipitation was performed as follows, in accordance with the previous methods22. After preclearing of 200 l of the sample with 20 l of Protein A–Sepharose at 4°C for 1h, immunoprecipitations were performed by incubating 200 l of lysates with 20 l of Protein A–Sepharose pre-absorbed with 2 g of anti-Bax polyclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen) or 2 g of anti-Ku70 polyclonal antibody (Cat. #sc-9033; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA) at 4°C for 2h. After extensive washing in buffer, beads were boiled in 40 1 of Laemmli buffer and 20 1 of the eluted proteins were resolved by SDS–PAGE. NRS was used as a negative control. After SDS–PAGE, the gel was fixed, washed, dried and exposed at -20°C for the periods indicated in figure legends.

Subcellular fractionation
Approximately 107 cells were homogenized with 200 l of ice-cold homogenization buffer (250 mM sucrose, 20mM Hepes at pH7.5, 10 mM potassium chloride, 1.5 mM magnesium chloride, 1mM EDTA and 0.1mM PMSF). Subcellular fractionation was performed as previously reported35. Each fraction was examined for appropriate marker proteins (cytosolic fraction, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) with anti-human LDH antibody (Cat. #L7016; Sigma) or glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PDH) with anti-G6PDH antibody (Cat. #A9521; Sigma); nuclear fraction; proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) with anti-PCNA antibody (Cat. #NA03; Oncogene, Boston, MA); mitochondria-containing heavy membrane fraction; F1-ATPase -subunit (F1) with anti-F1 subunit antibody (Cat. #A-11144; Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR). For total cell lysates, samples were prepared with ice-cold lysis buffer (50mM sodium chloride, 25mM Hepes at pH7.4, 1mM EDTA, 1mM EGTA, 1mM PMSF, 10 g l-1 E64 and 1% Triton X-100). In some experiments, samples containing 20 g of protein from total cell lysates or cytosolic fraction were analysed by western blotting with anti-Ku70 and -Bax antibodies. The pellets of the heavy membrane and nuclear fractions were dissolved in 100 l of SDS–PAGE sample buffer and the same proportion of the volume equal to that of the cytosol samples out of its total volume was used for western blotting. For the experiments examining the effects of Ku70 overexpression on STS- (HeLa cells) or UVC- (HEK293T cells) induced Bax movement, cells (10-cm dish) were transfected with 10 g pCMV-Ku70 or pCMV-vector. One day after transfection, cells were treated with 200nM STS or underwent UVC irradiation (200 J m-2). One day after STS treatment, subcellular fractionation was performed. For the experiments examining the effects of Ku70 expression on Bax-induced cell death, HEK293T cells (10-cm dish) were transfected with 5 g of pcDNA3-Bax and 10 g of pCMV-Ku70 or pCMV vector. Subcellular fractionation was performed one day after transfection. For the experiments examining the effects of antisense Ku70 on the localization of Bax, HEK293T cells (10-cm diameter dish) were transfected with 10 g of pcDNA3(-)-antisense Ku70 or pcDNA3(-) vector. One day after transfection, subcellular fractionation was performed. For the experiments examining the effects of z-VAD-fmk on the disappearance of Ku70 from the cytosol during apoptosis (Fig. 6c), caspase inhibition by z-VAD-fmk was confirmed by its suppression of apoptosis and PARP cleavage (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S4f). The percentages of apoptotic cells were 3 1% in control, 49 4% in STS-treated cells, and 5 3% in STS- and z-VAD-fmk-treated cells.

Confocal microscopy
HEK293T cells (106 cells) were transfected with 1.0 g pCMV-2B (Control and UV+z-VAD) or pCMV-2B-Ku70 (UV+Ku70). One day after transfection (except for the control group), cells were exposed to UVC irradiation in the absence (UV + Ku70) or presence of 50M z-VAD-fmk (UV+z-VAD). After 12h of UVC irradiation (200 J m-2), cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS for 10min and washed twice with PBS. Cells were permeabilized with 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS for 5min and then incubated in 0.05% Tween-20 in PBS for 5min at room temperature. Fixed cells were pre-incubated for 30min in PBS containing 5% BSA at 37°C before immunostaining. Cells were double stained with anti-Bax-monoclonal antibody (1:50; BD-Pharmingen) and anti-Ku70-polyclonal rabbit antibody (1:50; Santa Cruz) before detection of FITC-labelled anti-rabbit IgG (1:100; Jackson ImmunoResearch, West Grove, PA) and Texas-Red labelled anti-mouse IgG (1:100; Jackson ImmunoResearch). Microscopic analysis was performed on a BioRad MRC-600 confocal microscope.
