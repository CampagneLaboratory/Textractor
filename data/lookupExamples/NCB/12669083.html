Yeasts make their mark

Fred Chang1 & Matthias Peter2

1 Department of Microbiology, Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons, New York NY, 10032, USA

2 Swiss Federal Institute of Technology, Zurich, Institute of Biochemistry, ETH Hoenggerberg HPM G 6.2, 8093 Zurich, Switzerland

Correspondence should be addressed to Fred Chang fc99@columbia.edu
Budding and fission yeast serve as genetic model organisms for the study of the molecular mechanisms of cell polarity in single cells. Similar to other polarized eukaryotic cells, yeast cells have polarity programmes that regulate where they grow and divide. Here, we describe recent advances in defining the proteins that establish cell polarity and the numerous molecular interactions that may link these factors to the actin cytoskeleton. As many of these components are identified, a comprehensive understanding of complex pathways is beginning to emerge.
Generation of cellular polarity is critically important for the function of practically every cell type and underlies processes as diverse as cell division, differentiation, cell migration, cell–cell signalling and fertilization. Understanding how cellular components can generate asymmetry and architecture at a global cellular level therefore represents one of the key steps towards understanding the molecular bases of cellular assembly.

The budding yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae and the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe are used as model systems to define the molecular mechanisms responsible for cell polarity. Although both are simple, highly polarized unicellular cells, inherent differences between the two yeasts makes it valuable to compare the processes between the two organisms. Here, we emphasize the emerging conceptual mechanisms underlying the establishment of cell polarity. For a detailed comparison of the components involved, we refer the reader to recently published reviews1, 2, 3, 4, 5.

Budding yeast cells exhibit asymmetric growth, as growth is largely restricted to the daughter bud. In fission yeast, although there is no bud, cell growth is restricted to the tips of the rod-shaped cells. In both yeasts, the actin and microtubule cytoskeletons are polarized towards the cell growth site. It is thought that the actin cables emanating from the growth site act as one-way 'highways' along which secretory vesicles deliver the material necessary for cellular growth. In addition, the plasma membrane of the growth zones is in a separate membrane domain from the rest of the cell. The polarization of cytoskeletal structures, membranes and organelles is critically important for the growth and life cycle of these cells.

In both types of yeast, cell polarity is tightly controlled during the cell cycle6. In budding yeast, a site for bud formation is established during G1 phase in the round, unbudded cell at a specified site on the cortex. Patches of polarity proteins and rings of septins and actin delineate this site, and the bud begins to grow at the bud tip in a highly polarized fashion (termed apical growth). In G2 phase, the pattern of growth is redistributed so that the daughter cells grow evenly in all directions (isotropic growth). Then, during mitosis, the growth machinery is redistributed again, this time to the bud neck, to prepare for the growth of the septum and cell division. In fission yeast, the new daughter cells formed after cell division grow by apical extension from the 'old' end of the cell (that is, the end that existed before division). Then during G2 phase, in a transition dubbed 'new-end take-off' (NETO), cells initiate growth at the new end of the cell (the previous cell division site)7. Thus, these processes are under tight temporal and spatial control.

The establishment of cell polarity is conceptualized as a hierarchy of four key steps8, 9. First, a site on the cell surface is determined. For this, the cell may use a number of intrinsic spatial cues (cues present in the cell itself) or may use extrinsic spatial cues (cues present from extracellular environment, such as another cell). This site is then marked by the deposition of a 'landmark protein'. Third, cell polarity is established by a set of small GTPase proteins that are activated specifically in the vicinity of the landmark. Last, actin and other polarized components of the cell are organized by these GTPases, leading to polarized cell growth. Here we describe our current understanding of the molecular basis for each of these steps.

Cortical landmarks
In unicellular yeast cells (at least during vegetative growth), the site for polarization is determined by intrinsic signals10. Any source of asymmetry may potentially be used as a reference point in the cell. Examples include organelles, such as the nucleus, the mitotic spindle, gradients of intracellular factors, the cytoskeleton or stable sites on the cell surface that are marked by specific proteins. In the absence of asymmetry, stochastic mechanisms may also generate asymmetry (see the commentary by Rong Li on page 267 of this issue). Both budding and fission yeast use the site of cell division or cell growth from previous cell cycles as spatial cues to establish new cell polarity sites (Fig. 1). In addition, fission yeast also seem to use the distribution of interphase microtubule ends as an additional cue. In general, this marking process is not essential for polarized growth, but controls the proper placement of the site.

Budding yeast have two distinct programmes for specifying the bud site, which are regulated by the mating type (Fig. 1a, b). Haploid cells ( cells) exhibit an 'axial' pattern in which, after cell division, both the mother and the daughter form buds adjacent to the cell division plane (Fig. 1a). Studies of mutants that fail to achieve this axial budding pattern have defined a number of potential landmark proteins — Bud3p, Bud4p and the integrin-like membrane protein Axl2p–Bud10p11. The localization of these proteins to the bud neck is dependent on septins — filament-forming GTPase proteins that assemble into a discrete ring structure. This septin ring marks the future bud site during G1 phase and remains at the bud neck throughout the cell cycle12. The landmark proteins only localize with the septin ring at the bud neck at the end of the cell cycle, and may direct assembly of the new bud at a point adjacent to the septin ring. Little is known about how a specific point on the old ring is chosen for the new ring, as linear patterns of bud scars suggest these proteins must somehow register spatial information from previous generations. Another interesting question is how the uniform dimensions of the new bud neck ring (approximately 0.5 m in diameter) are achieved. Septins can self assemble in vitro into rings of filaments approximately 0.5 m in diameter13, suggesting that the physical properties of septin filaments themselves may contribute to determining the spatial dimensions of the ring.

In contrast to haploid cells, diploid budding yeast cells exhibit a bipolar budding pattern dependent on persistent spatial markers at two sites in the cell — the previous cell division site (proximal) and an opposite site (distal), a cell polarization site in the previous cell cycle (Fig. 1b). Bud8p and Bud9p are related plasma membrane glycoproteins that mark these distal and proximal poles respectively14, 15, 16. In support of this, bud8 mutants only bud from the proximal pole, whereas bud9 mutants only bud from the distal poles. The cell-cycle-regulated expression of Bud8p and Bud9p contributes to their distinct localization — expression of Bud8p from the BUD9-promoter results in it localizing predominantly to the sites typical for Bud9p, and vice versa17. Expression of Bud8p from the BUD9-promoter also allows Bud8p to compensate for loss of Bud9p function, suggesting that the timing of gene expression is a primary determinant of both localization and function. This cell-cycle effect may reflect general changes in patterns of secretion during the previous cell cycle from the cell tip to the bud neck, suggesting secretion is important in establishing these cortical cues. Whereas Bud8p and Bud9p are important in establishing patterns in the newborn daughter cell, another membrane protein (Rax2p) is required for the maintenance of the bipolar budding pattern after the first cell cycle. Rax2p is a stable protein that persists at the proximal and distal bud sites through many cell generations, and may thus provide a molecular 'memory' of all the bud sites throughout the life of the cell18. A recent systematic genome-wide screen for diploid cells with an altered budding pattern identified 11 functional classes of mutants19, including genes involved in actin cytoskeletal organization, cell polarity, vesicular transport, cell wall synthesis, protein modification, transcription and translation. It is clear that multiple processes are required to establish and maintain a stable axis of cell polarity. Whether these affect Bud8p, Bud9p or Rax2p, or define additional pathways remains to be determined.

Fission yeast cells also initiate growth at the site of previous cell growth and the site of cell division. But in contrast to budding yeast, which polarize only at one point, fission yeast can polarize at two sites at once. Initially, after cell division, new daughter cells grow in a monopolar manner at the sites of previous cell growth (old ends), and then at NETO they initiate growth at the site of previous cell division (the 'new end') to grow in a bipolar manner7. Numerous mutants that disrupt this growth pattern in different ways have been identified. One class of mutants form 'T-shaped' or bent cells, where the new cell polarity site forms on the side of the cell instead of at the ends20, 21. These mutants also have defects in initiating growth at certain cell tips and so grow as monopolar cells. Many of these mutants are defective in the organization of interphase microtubules4. Therefore, in contrast to budding yeast, where cell polarity is largely independent of microtubules, the importance of the microtubule cytoskeleton for cell polarity in fission yeast has led to much research into the characterization and function of interphase microtubules.

The interphase microtubules that regulate cell polarity are organized in bundles extending the length of the fission yeast cell. These bundles contain microtubules bound in an anti-parallel configuration, in which the microtubule plus ends face both cell tips whereas the microtubule minus ends are located near the nucleus22, 23, 24. The plus ends of the microtubule are constantly growing, touching the cell tip and then shrinking back. Microtubule catastrophe — the rapid shrinkage of microtubules — is regulated so that the microtubule shrinks only after it hits the cell tip. The microtubules continue to grow after hitting the sides of cells, orientating the microtubule bundles along the long axis of the cell and the microtubule plus ends towards the cell tips. Thus, both cell tips are continuously probed by the dynamic microtubules.

Tea1p, a kelch repeat protein, may be a landmark protein directly localized by microtubule plus ends. tea1 mutants resemble tubulin mutants — they grow as monopolar and bent cells and frequently form T-shaped cells under certain conditions (such as recovery from starvation)25. However, tea1 mutants only have very mild defects in microtubule organization and dynamics, suggesting that it may function primarily in regulation of cell polarity, rather than microtubule regulation25, 26. The Tea1p gene product is located in dots at the growing plus ends of microtubules. When the microtubule end reaches the cell tip, Tea1p dots seem to be released from the microtubule, dock onto the cortex, and accumulate in clusters at both cell tips26. Tea2p (a Kip2p-like kinesin) and Tip1p (a CLIP-170 homologue) are required to move or maintain Tea1p at the plus ends of microtubules24, 27. In tea2 or tip1 mutants, Tea1p is no longer concentrated at the microtubule plus end or at cell tips, but is in motile dots along microtubules. These cells have aberrant cell polarity similar to that of tea1 mutants. The deposition of Tea1p depends on microtubule catastrophe, which is somehow restricted to the cell tip24 (F.C., unpublished results). To understand how Tea1p is localized to the cell tips, we now need to understand the spatial regulation of microtubule catastrophe.

Analysis of tea1 and other cell polarity mutants revealed alternative cell polarity programmes reminiscent of bud site selection patterns in budding yeast. For example, in a tea1 mutant, one of the daughter cells only grows from the previous cell end, whereas the other cell grows only from the previous cell division site, in effect, like the 'bipolar' budding pattern28. In pom1 or tea3 mutants, the two daughter cells often only grow at the previous cell division site, recreating the 'axial' pattern29. Such patterns suggest there is complex regulation to specify when and where the previous cell growth or division sites are used. As in S. cerevisiae, there must also be cortical cues that mark and remember the sites of previous cell division and growth in S. pombe. Microtubules cannot be the sole spatial cue, as cells lacking Tea1p or even microtubules usually still polarize at the proper locations30, 31. The possible importance of Bud8/9-like, Bud10-like and Rax2p-like proteins, as well as the septins, in providing historical landmarks at sites of previous cell growth and division in S. pombe needs to be explored.

GTPases downstream of the cortical landmarks
Once a site is marked at the cell surface, the next step is establishing cell polarity at that site through a cascade of small GTPases (Ras-like factors and Cdc42p). In general, these proteins are essential for cell polarity — their mutants do not polarize and grow isotropically into spheres. Here we describe findings that reveal direct molecular links between the landmark proteins and the localized activation of these GTPases.

In S. cerevisiae, the Rsr1p–Bud1p GTPase (here called Bud1p) functions downstream of the landmarks (Fig. 2). Bud1p is a small GTPase of the Ras family regulated by its guanine nucleotide-exchance factor (GEF) Bud5p and the GTPase-activating protein (GAP) Bud2p32. These proteins are required for the correct spatial regulation of budding, but not for polarized growth, as mutants exhibit randomized budding patterns. The cytoplasmic tail of the axial landmark protein Axl2p–Bud10p interacts with Bud5p33, providing a possible mechanism for the selective activation of Bud1p at the site of the landmark.

Figure 2. Polarity pathways in S. cerevisiae during budding and mating.

Schematic representation of the molecular pathways leading to a polarized organization of the cytoskeleton in response to internal cues during budding and external signals during mating. Common to both pathways is the asymmetric activation of Cdc42p at the site determined by the landmarks. In turn, activated Cdc42p triggers the polarized assembly of the actin cytoskeleton by binding to various effectors. The mediators Bud1p and Far1p are thought to recruit and activate the GEF Cdc24p downstream of the specific landmark proteins. Far1p interacts directly with activated G at activated receptors, whereas Bud1p is regulated at landmarks by the GEF Bud5p and the GAP Bud2p. The mechanisms required to localize the landmark proteins Bud10p, Bud8p and Bud9p are not fully understood, but in the case of Bud9p and Bud10p require an intact septin structure. Genetic analysis has identified a number of novel proteins, which alter the budding pattern of diploid cells, and may thus be involved in localizing these landmark proteins.

Activated Bud1p regulates the Cdc42p GTPase through another GEF Cdc24p, which in turn switches Cdc42p into its active GTP-bound state1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Bud1p–GTP directly interacts with Cdc24p34 and functions to recruit and activate Cdc24p GEF activity35 (Y. S., M.-P. G. and M. P., unpublished results). In this way, the local activation of Bud1p directly results in the local activation of Cdc42p at the landmark site.

In addition to Bud1p, other proteins generate activating and inhibitory signals for Cdc42p. Bem1p is another adaptor protein required to maintain activated Cdc24p at sites of polarized growth36. Interestingly, the recruitment of Bem1p to the site of polarization requires Cdc42p–GTP, providing a positive feedback loop to stabilize active Cdc24p at these sites37. The establishment of a stable polarization axis may also require inhibitory signals. In contrast to fission yeast, polarization towards multiple sites in budding yeast is rare, suggesting that mechanisms must exist to prevent the establishment of additional growth sites once an axis of polarization has been chosen. Interestingly, a mutant allele of Cdc42p that is slow to hydrolyse GTP results in formation of multiple buds38. Although the molecular nature of such inhibitory signals is not known, further investigation into the importance of GAPs for Cdc42p (for example, Rga1p, Rga2p, Bem2p and Bem3p) may be warranted.

In S. pombe, Ras and Cdc42 GTPases are also required for cell polarity, but less is known about their regulation. In contrast to the Bud1p pathway, the related Ras1p pathway in fission yeast is absolutely required for polarized growth, as ras1 mutants form small, spherical cells39. Much like in budding yeast, Ras1p may regulate polarized cell growth by activating Cdc42p through the GEF Scd1p (a Cdc24p homologue). However, additional functions of the Ras1p pathway in processes such as mating and mitotic spindle function, as well as the presence of multiple GEFs, have so far complicated the analyses of these GTPase modules in cell polarity40, 41, 42. As a result, the direct links from landmark proteins to Cdc42p are not yet well defined, although a synthetic-lethal relationship between the landmark protein tea1 and ras1 does hint at such an interaction40.

Extrinsic signals can override intrinsic ones
During mating, yeast cells use pheromones as extrinsic signals to direct the axis of polarity. Both budding and fission yeast cells sense a gradient of pheromones emitted from other yeast cells of the opposite mating type and grow towards the pheromone source43, 44. During this process, the intrinsic cues directing cell polarity must be overridden or turned off, and the basic cell polarity machinery becomes usurped by pheromone receptors.

In budding yeast, the pathway linking the activated pheromone receptors to the polarity machinery is relatively well understood (Fig. 2), although we still do not know how the cell transforms a shallow pheromone gradient (estimated at 1% concentration difference across the cell diameter) into a robust axis of polarization. Binding of pheromones to their receptors triggers the dissociation of the coupled heterotrimeric G-protein into G–GTP and G. A crucial G effector with respect to cell polarity is the adaptor protein Far1p that binds to both G and Cdc24p45, 46. Far1p may recruit Cdc24p to the site of receptor activation, ensuring activation of Cdc42p at the site of greatest pheromone receptor activation. Far1p seems to constitute a switch that regulates whether the polarity machinery becomes localized towards the intrinsic or extrinsic polarity cues47. In haploid G1 cells, Cdc24p is bound to Far1p in the nucleus. In the absence of mating pheromone, activation of the cell cycle kinase Cdc28p–Clnp triggers degradation of Far1p, allowing Cdc24p to interact with activated Bud1p at the cell cortex. In the presence of mating pheromone, Far1p is not degraded and the signalling pathway promotes nuclear export of the Far1p–Cdc24p complex48, 49. As Far1p and Bud1p bind to overlapping domains of Cdc24p (Y. S. and M. P., unpublished results), Cdc24p tethered to Far1p is preferentially recruited to the site of pheromone receptor activation marked by G.

In fission yeast, down-regulation of the Tea1p pathway allows cells to follow spatial cues from activated mating factor receptors50. During mating, fission yeast cells must be able to bend towards a mating partner. This suggests that Tea1p function, which is required to keep cells growing straight, is inhibited in the presence of mating pheromone. In pheromone-treated cells, Tea1p appears to be displaced from microtubules and the localization of Tea1p, Tea2p and Tip1p are all greatly reduced at the growing, bending end of the cell. Tea1p may be directly affected by the pheromone signalling pathway, as it is differentially de-phosphorylated in pheromone-treated cells. Consistent with these effects, tea1 mutants mate perhaps even better than wild-type cells. However, components of the alternative polarity programme mediating polarity effects downstream of pheromone-activated receptors, such as a Far1-like protein, have not yet been identified.

Polarizing the cytoskeleton and forming membrane compartments
Once activated at the proper location, Cdc42p functions to regulate many aspects of polarized cell growth — it organizes the actin and microtubule cytoskeletons and the septin ring and directs membrane traffic and the formation of membrane compartments. Consistent with these multiple functions, a large number of Cdc42p effectors regulating one or more aspects of Cdc42p function have now been identified51, 52. Extensive webs of protein–protein and genetic interactions between polarity factors suggest that these proteins probably function in large protein complexes2, 3, 53.

The mechanisms of how Cdc42p triggers local changes in actin organization are beginning to be revealed. The assembly of both actin patches and actin cables are localized at the sites of polarized growth5, 54, 55, 56 and Cdc42p may activate the key actin nucleators for each — the Arp2/3 complex and the formins, respectively. In the case of actin patches, Cdc42p somehow activates the Arp2/3 activators WASp, Vrp1p and myosin type I57, 58. To trigger actin cable formation, Cdc42p may bind directly to and activate the formin proteins (Bni1p and Bnrp1 in budding yeast59, For3p in fission yeast60, 61). Formins, which are regulated by Cdc42p and other Rho GTPases, are required for assembly of actin cables and cytokinetic actin rings, and overexpression of Bni1p fragments can result in overly abundant actin cables60, 62, 63, 64. Recent studies show that the highly conserved FH2 domain of Bni1p nucleates actin filaments in vitro65, 66. In contrast to the Arp2/3 complex (which forms a branched network of actin filaments) the formins may ride on the fast-growing barbed end of actin filaments and form linear actin filaments that may be more suitable for actin cable assembly. As formins associate with many other proteins, including profilin and Bud6p, they may be a key component of a multi-protein machine that spools out actin cables from the cell surface.

In S. pombe, recent work demonstrates that Tea1p and microtubules may control the spatial distribution of actin cables by regulating the formin For3p and the formin-binding protein Bud6p (F. C. and B. F., unpublished results)28. Tea1p, For3p and Bud6p associate together in a large protein complex, dubbed the 'polarisome'. Data so far support a model in which Tea1p recruits Bud6p, which in turn interacts with For3p, thereby localizing actin cable assembly to the cell tip (Fig. 3). The possible role of GTPase signalling modules in this pathway is still not clear, although For3p has been demonstrated to bind to Cdc42p and Rho3p61. Other putative downstream effectors of Tea1p in cell polarity regulation are the protein kinase Pom1p and a Tea1p-like protein Tea3p26, 29, 67. So, microtubules may contribute to cell polarity by helping to localize cell polarity machinery to the proper locations in the cell.

A critically important process for polarized cell growth is localized protein secretion2, 68. The secretory network may directly contribute to cell growth by providing new membrane and delivering components needed for cell wall growth and remodelling. During polarization, secretory vesicles are targeted to sites of polarized cell growth by moving along actin cables in a myosin V-dependent manner69. The geometric arrangement of formins and actin filaments in the cable ensure the proper orientation of actin cables, so that vesicles move towards the site where activated Cdc42p and formins reside. Cdc42p may also regulate vesicle docking and fusion at the plasma membrane70, 71. For example, the Cdc42p effectors Msb3p and Msb4p52 function as GAPs for the Rab-type GTPase Sec4p72, which regulates polarized transport of secretory vesicles to sites of polarized growth.

Recent evidence also suggests a role of lipids and lipid rafts in the control of cell polarization73. Sterol-rich membrane domains, indicative of lipid raft sites, have been found at sites of polarized growth in both budding and fission yeast. In budding yeast, disturbances in ergosterol biosynthesis disrupt shmoo polarization74 (M.P., unpublished results). In fission yeast, sterol-rich membrane domains also appear at the septation site, and disruption of these domains causes cytokinesis defects75. Membrane domains are altered in polarity mutants in S. pombe, such as tea1 and for3 (formin) and in some cytokinesis mutants (F. C. and T. T., unpublished results). Interestingly, in budding yeast, several proteins involved in the production of specific phospholipids are preferentially localized at the mother cell cortex, and may thus establish distinct membrane domains76. As septins provide a diffusion barrier to prevent exchange between mother and daughter cells 77, 78, they may contribute to the stabilization of such membrane compartments. The lipid composition of the plasma membrane may be responsible for setting up specialized zones in the plasma membrane that facilitate the clustering of certain membrane proteins, the activation of actin polymerization pathways and the selective targeting of vesicle trafficking. As lipid rafts may also help to localize small GTPases and actin structures, it is not clear if lipid rafts are 'upstream' or 'downstream' of proteins, such as Cdc42p and actin, or should be considered as part of a positive feedback loop.

Cell division site positioning and the nucleus
Cytokinesis shares many similar processes with polarized cell growth, including assembly of actin structures, directed secretion and membrane insertion and assembly of cell wall material. During cytokinesis in budding and fission yeast, cell growth at the tip is turned off, and the polarity machinery is directed towards the cell division site. The spatial determinants of the cytokinesis site in budding yeast are the same as for bud formation, as budding yeast divide at the bud neck. However, S. pombe cells confront a more complex task of defining a site for cell division at the middle of the cell. In fission yeast, the position of the contractile ring correlates with the position of the predivisional nucleus in a number of circumstances, suggesting that the position of the nucleus may dictate the division plane79. The nucleus is positioned in the middle of the cell by the interphase microtubule bundles that attach and push on the nuclear envelope23. Mid1p is a candidate 'landmark' protein that may link the nucleus with contractile ring assembly. Mid1p is required for the proper positioning of the contractile ring, as mid1 mutants divide at random sites in the cell80, 81. In addition, Mid1p appears to shuttle between the nucleus and to a broad band on the cell surface overlying the nucleus that anticipates the future site of ring assembly82, 83. Finally, Mid1p may function in the proper recruitment of other ring proteins, such as myosin, actin and the formin Cdc12p for construction of the contractile ring84.

New challenges for yeast polarity
Studies of S. cerevisiae and S. pombe invite valuable comparisons for mechanisms involved in cell polarity. Work in S. cerevisiae has so far emphasized the role of cortical cues, whereas studies in S. pombe focused on the contributions of the microtubule cytoskeleton. However, despite some differences in the biology of the two systems, it is likely that most of the underlying mechanisms are conserved not only between these distantly related yeast cells, but also in most, if not all, eukaryotes. In particular, the concept that cortical landmarks break cellular symmetry and direct future growth by locally activating Rho–GTPases is widely used in many organisms. As outlined in this Review, much progress has been made in elucidating these polarity pathways in molecular terms — genetic studies in yeast have identified components of the cortical landmarks and the links between these landmark proteins, the Rho-type GTPase Cdc42p, and the effectors that organize the different cellular processes.

One emerging theme in the analysis of cell polarity is the importance of positive amplification loops85. For example, cortical cues may provide only a weak initial signal, which is sufficient to break the symmetry of Cdc42p and the downstream cytokeletal components. A polarized cytoskeleton may then be required to amplify this signal and establish a stable axis of polarization. One mode of amplification could be that the polarized cytoskeleton, vesicle trafficking and membrane domains help to localize polarity factors themselves86. As in mammalian systems, the importance of vesicle trafficking along cytoskeletal networks, as well as the establishment of distinct membrane domains is becoming increasingly apparent in yeast, and understanding their importance for the establishment and/or maintenance of cell polarity emerges as an important area for future research. Clearly, in most experimental assays it is difficult to distinguish between proteins functioning as bona-fide polarity cues, and proteins involved in feedback loops that amplify such signals. Moreover, the dynamic assembly and disassembly of these complexes appears to be important to modulate and adapt the polarity response, and we are far from understanding the dynamic nature of these interactions.

Recent advancements in proteomics promise to identify the nearly 'complete set' of proteins involved in cell polarity53. Proteomic projects are revealing extensive webs of protein–protein interactions among proteins involved in polarity establishment, providing tantalizing physical links between proteins involved in trafficking, polarity establishment and cytoskeletal dynamics. The daunting but exciting challenge in the near future is to make sense of all these protein webs, to define hierarchies, the key interactions and feedback loops, and finally relate this information back to the biology.
