Viral spread through protoplasmic kiss

Michael Dustin

Michael Dustin is at the Skirball Institute of Biomolecular Medicine, New York University School of Medicine, 540 First Avenue, NY 10021, USA.
dustin@saturn.med.nyu.edu

Cajal described the neural synapse as a "protoplasmic kiss", and this metaphor applies equally well to the immunological synapse. Viruses are masters at co-opting the machinery of the immune response and, remarkably, the retrovirus HTLV-1 (human T lymphotrophic virus 1) seems to use an immunological-synapse-like structure to conceal viral spread.
The HTLV-1 retrovirus infects millions worldwide and is strongly linked to the development of cancer and inflammatory disease. A major mystery regarding the natural history of HTLV-1 infection has been how it moves from cell to cell. Very few viral particles are generated and those that are are inefficient at infecting T cells. Infection requires the transfer of intact blood cells between individuals and there is no evidence of transmission through cell-free blood products, as occurs with HIV. A study published last month in Science provides support for a striking new hypothesis: namely, that HTLV-1 subverts a process normally used for immune cell communication — the formation of an immunological synapse — to efficiently transfer viral genomes directly from cell to cell1.

The immunological synapse is a specialized cell–cell junction that provides a stable platform for a number of immune cell functions, including signalling for cytokine production, directed secretion of cytokines and cytotoxic agents, and antigen receptor downregulation. It is structurally defined as a ring of adhesion molecule interactions in the plane of the cell–cell interface surrounding a central cluster of antigen receptors2. The central cluster is a site of regulation for signalling and the focused secretion of cytotoxic agents and cytokines. The T cell has been shown to extract membrane vesicles from target cells3, an interesting process in the context of viral particle transfer. The adhesion ring may serve to form a sealed synaptic cleft in which secreted molecules are concentrated and prevented from interacting with bystander cells. This protection probably goes both ways, and it stands to reason that this space is also protected from antibodies, complement and other anti-viral proteins. Therefore, the immunological synapse may also function as an ideal environment for the protected transfer of unstable viral particles. The study of Igakura et al. describes the specific induction of cytoskeletal and viral coat protein polarization within infected cells interacting with non-infected cells1. This polarization is accompanied by formation of an adhesion ring almost identical to that formed in an antigen-dependent immunological synapse. Infected T cells acquired the ability to form these virological synapses with many different cell types. These findings raise many questions about how HTLV-1 organizes such structures.

There are two different proposed mechanisms for immunological synapse formation. One is based on cytoskeletal transport of components to their final locations at the interface2. The other takes into account the physics of membrane interfaces and the forces that can be generated by thermal motions to move molecules in the plane of the interface, resulting in large-scale self assembly of the antigen receptor cluster and adhesion ring4. It is probable that both types of process operate in an interdependent manner. An important principle in both models is topological segregation with larger adhesion molecules and smaller antigen receptors requiring lateral segregation to interact at different inter-membrane distances. It has been demonstrated that this mode of segregation takes place even when cytoskeletal interactions are excluded5. Setting up the adhesion ring and the central T-cell receptor (TCR) cluster is not an instantaneous process, but requires several minutes after initial contact. Initially, the pattern is inverted so that the antigen receptors are engaged on the outside and the adhesion molecules are on the inside; this pattern rapidly inverts to form the mature immunological synapse, which can be stable for hours6. Another interesting feature of the immunological synapse detected in recent electron micrographs is regions of plasma membrane fusion, suggesting that cytoplasmic bridges are present between cytotoxic T cells and target cells7. On the basis of this understanding of synapse formation in T cells, future questions for the virological synapse become clear.

One major question is how the viral genome is actually transferred from the virion-producing cell to its partner. On the basis of cytoplasmic bridges discovered by Griffith's and colleagues, the mechanism of viral passage could be through direct intercellular transport of an uncoated viral nucleoprotein particle, perhaps using cytoplasmic filament systems7. Alternatively, a coated particle may bud into the synaptic cleft where it could be efficiently endocytosed by the recipient cell. Either way, the process will be largely insulated from extracellular attack by the host immune system. In the former case, the viral envelope protein would be used only for cell–cell interaction and possibly for enhancing the formation of cytoplasmic bridges. In the latter case, the Env protein could participate in its conventional way, by inducing binding and fusion of the membrane-enveloped virus in the protected environment of the virological synapse. An intriguing possibility is that the Env protein could participate in synapse formation and then allow itself to be extracted from the T cell by the recipient-cell Env receptor along with the attached genome, a reverse of the normal direction for membrane transfer in the immunological synapse.

It is not clear whether the assembly of the virological synapse by HTLV-1 is mediated by mis-regulation of host proteins or by the viral envelope protein. Again, insights into possible mechanisms can be gained from what we know about immunological synapse formation. Studies with supported planar bilayers have demonstrated that two receptor–ligand systems are sufficient to form an immunological synapse6. The adhesion ring is formed by the interaction of integrins such as LFA-1 (lymphocyte-function-associated antigen 1) with ligands such as ICAM-1 (intercellular adhesion molecule 1). The other essential component is an antigen receptor–ligand system: for example, the interactions of the TCR with an agonist MHC–peptide complex. The topology of these interactions seems to be very important for immunological synapse formation, with the LFA-1–ICAM-1 interaction spanning 40 nm and the TCR–MHC interaction spanning 15 nm. The antigen receptors, which can be the TCR, an Fc receptor or an immunoglobulin, share the property that they signal by association with non-receptor tyrosine kinases of the Src, Syk and Itk families2. There are several possibilities for how viral infection could co-opt the machinery of immunological synapse formation in the absence of any real antigen receptor ligand. The simplest possibility is that the Env protein itself may interact with components of the TCR signalling machinery, after engagement of its unknown cellular receptor to both produce the physical interactions needed for synapse assembly and the signals needed to induce cytoskeletal polarization. The structure of the HTLV-1 Env protein has been determined8, but its ligand is not known. If it has evolved to act as a 'stand in' for an antigen receptor, it might be expected to interact with its cellular receptor to form a complex that spans a distance of 15 nm to assume the same position in the immunological synapse as the TCR–MHC interaction. This is consistent with the small size of the viral envelope protein core (7 nm). The Env protein might also link to signal transduction machinery that could accomplish activation of 'inside out' signalling to activate LFA-1 and to set up conditions for synapse assembly and cytoskeletal polarization. This polarization involves actin polymerization and movement of the microtubule organizing centre to the centre of the interface, a process that may involve dynein-like motors linked to the boundary between the adhesion ring and the central cluster9. This idea is not unprecedented: there are cellular proteins that do not look like antigen receptors themselves, but which can activate T cells through the antigen receptor complex when engaged. For example, crosslinking of CD2, an adhesion molecule important for T-cell function, activates T cells through the TCR signalling machinery10. There are many other alternatives for how the virus could induce synapse formation, including the induction of cellular machinery that produces immunological synapse patterns. Interestingly, the ligands used on the target cell do not actually need to do anything to promote immunological synapse formation. B cells treated with the actin cytoskeleton inhibitor cytochalasin-D and passive-supported bilayer systems both allow immunological synapse formation by T cells, as long as ICAM-1 and an agonist MHC–peptide complex are available6, 11. Alternatively, the target cell may modulate immunological synapse formation in a cytoskeleton-dependent manner, as recently demonstrated for dendritic cells12. It may also be possible that the virus has evolved a way to configure an immunological synapse using only adhesion molecules and cytoskeletal modifications induced by gene products in the cytoplasm. This would explain the wide range of cells with which the infected cells can synapse and could be tested by determining whether infected cells can polarize towards supported planar bilayers containing only ICAM-1.

Although the generation of an antigen-independent immunological synapse is a novel mechanism for viral transmission, other viruses may use different but related strategies to avoid the hostile extracellular environment of the host. Vaccinia virus uses actin rockets, and the filipodia that occur when these rockets encounter the plasma membrane behave like 'harpoons' to penetrate and enter neighbouring cells without generating an extracellular viral particle13. It has also been demonstrated that HIV particles are endocytosed by dendritic cells using a cell-surface C-type lectin (DC-SIGN)14. Paradoxically, the uptake by dendritic cells enhances the infectivity of viral particles for cocultured T cells15. This suggests that dendritic-to-T cell transfer of the viral particle (without true infection of the dendritic cell) through an antigen-independent immunological synapse may be critical for efficient HIV infection of T cells. The new array of imaging tools to probe cytoskeletal polarity and single-particle tracking to follow viral particles will hopefully shed more light on virological synapses.
