PKC is a permissive link in integrin-dependent IFN- signalling that facilitates JAK phosphorylation of STAT1

Johanna Ivaska1, Lisardo Bosca2 & Peter J. Parker1

1 Protein Phosphorylation Laboratory, Cancer Research UK London Research Institute, Lincoln's Inn Fields Laboratories, 44 Lincoln's Inn Fields, London WC2A 3PX, UK

2 Instituto de Bioquímica CSIC-UCM, Facultad de Farmacia, 28040 Madrid, Spain

Correspondence should be addressed to Peter J. Parker peter.parker@cancer.org.uk
The critical dependence of receptor-triggered signals on integrin-mediated cell–substrate interactions represents a fundamental biological paradigm in health and disease. However, the molecular connections of these permissive inputs, which operate through integrin–matrix interactions, has remained largely obscure. Here we show that the serine-threonine kinase protein kinase C (PKC) functions as a signal integrator between cytokine and integrin signalling pathways. Integrins are shown to control PKC phosphorylation acutely by determining complex formation with protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) and the upstream kinase PDK1 (phosphoinositide-dependent kinase 1). The PP2A-induced loss of PKC function results in attenuated interferon (INF-)-induced phosphorylation of STAT1 (signal transducer and activator of transcription 1) downstream of Janus kinase 1/2 (JAK1/2). PKC function and the IFN- response can be recovered by inhibition of PP2A if PDK1 is associated with PKC in this complex. More directly, a PP2A-resistant mutant of PKC is sufficient for restoration of the IFN- response in suspension culture. Thus, PKC functions as a central point of integration through which integrin engagement exerts a permissive input on IFN- signalling.

The integrins are a large family of heterodimeric transmembrane receptors that mediate cell adhesion to matrix molecules1 and transmit signals regulating cell responsiveness to cytokines and growth factors2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7. The protein kinase C (PKC) family of effector-dependent protein kinases, including the novel forms PKC and PKC, is involved in the control of many cellular processes and cell adhesion is central to the regulation of phosphorylation and the catalytic output of PKC and PKC8, 9, 10.

In adherent HEK293 cells, IFN- treatment induces a characteristic time-dependent phosphorylation of STAT1 on Tyr 701 (Fig. 1a, b). However, detachment of cells from matrix or plating of cells in fibrillar collagen substantially attenuates this response. Immunofluorescence microscopic staining of STAT1 and STAT1 phosphorylated on Tyr 701 in mouse embryo fibroblasts (MEFs; clone PKC-RE; see below) shows clearly that the IFN--induced phosphorylation and nuclear accumulation of STAT1 occurred in attached, but not in detached, cells (Fig. 1c). Analysis by subcellular fractionation verified these observations, showing that IFN--induced activation and nuclear translocation of STAT1 occurred only during stimulation of adherent cells (Fig. 1d).

PKC is important for cytokine- and growth-factor-receptor-mediated signalling. This has been demonstrated by the observations that loss of PKC results in a severely attenuated response to treatment with lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and IFN- in macrophages from PKC-/- animals and impaired platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) receptor signalling in CHO cells where PKC expression has been blocked with antisense oligonucleotides11, 12. We investigated whether PKC is required for IFN--induced activation of STAT by examining STAT1 phosphorylation in mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs) lacking PKC (PKC-KO) or re-expressing PKC (PKC-RE). A requirement for PKC was evident from the attenuated response to IFN- treatment in PKC-KO cells, compared with PKC-RE cells (Fig. 2a, b). Furthermore, a reduced response was also observed in PKC-RE cells treated with the PKC inhibitor BIM1 (Fig. 2c) or in HEK293 cells co-expressing a kinase-inactive mutant of PKC and green fluorescent protein (GFP)–STAT1 (Fig. 2d). This suggests that PKC facilitates IFN--induced signalling through its kinase activity, rather than functioning as an adaptor. IFN- also induced rapid tyrosine phosphorylation of both JAK1 and JAK2 independently of PKC (Fig. 2e, f), suggesting that PKC regulates the IFN- signalling pathway downstream of JAKs. Furthermore, IFN- induced a similar level of JAK1 and JAK2 autophosphorylation activity, irrespective of PKC expression (Fig. 2g; JAK1 immunoprecipitation from PKC-KO cells, 735.5 30.4 arbitrary units of incorporated phosphate relative to protein mean value range; JAK1 immunoprecipitation from PKC-RE cells, 790.5 79.1; JAK2 immunoprecipitation from PKC-KO cells, 317.5 14.4; JAK2 immunoprecipitation from PKC-RE cells, 295 26.7; n = 4).

The altered response to IFN- as a function of integrin engagement implies that certain integrins control the activity of the IFN- signalling pathway upstream of STAT1. The requirement for PKC in this response downstream of JAKs provides a candidate for integrating this control. To assess this possiblity, the phosphorylation state of PKC was determined in cells cultured under different cell matrix conditions. There are three priming phosphorylation sites (Thr 566, Thr 710 and Ser 729) in PKC8. Detachment of cells resulted in an acute reduction in PKC phosphorylation at Thr 566 and Thr 710, whereas adhesion to fibrillar collagen resulted in dephosphorylation at all the three sites (Fig. 3a, b). Cells detached by EDTA treatment also elicited the same pattern of PKC dephosphorylation (Fig. 3c). In parallel with the dephosphorylation observed, these cells displayed reduced kinase activity (Fig. 3d; note that in detached cells, the determined activity is overestimated by the presence of co-immunoprecipitated PDK1; see below). Re-adherence of cells on fibronectin or type-IV collagen resulted in full phosphorylation, suggesting a general 1-integrin-mediated response, rather than specific input from a given – heterodimer. In marked contrast, the inactivation of PKC as a response to alterations in cell–matrix interactions was specific for this PKC isoform, as the three equivalent priming sites (Thr 505, Ser 643 and Ser 662) in another novel PKC (PKC) were not altered by the same acute changes in matrix conditions (Fig. 3e, f). To determine the mechanisms controlling this site-specific integrin-dependent input to PKC, the role of protein phosphatases was investigated. Cell adhesion to fibrillar collagen induced a twofold activation of PP2A (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1a)4. Cell detachment stimulated serine-threonine phosphatase activity approximately ninefold (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1a). Although fibrillar collagen primarily stimulates PP2A activity4, detachment also induces both PP1 and PP2A activities (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1b). However, in vitro phosphatase activity towards all three phosphorylation sites of PKC was greater in cell extracts from fibrillar collagen cultures than in those originating from detached cells (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1c).

Increased phosphatase activity in cells detached or grown in fibrillar collagen can explain the reduced phosphorylation of PKC, but does not provide an explanation for site-specific phosphorylation differences under these two conditions. Therefore, we investigated whether the Ser 729 phosphorylation site could be selectively protected by PDK1 in detached cells, as PDK1 can bind directly to this phosphorylated hydrophobic motif13, 14, 15. Gel filtration experiments performed on chemically cross-linked lysates from differentially treated cells indicated that after detachment, endogenous PKC was partially shifted to higher-molecular-weight fractions (Fig. 4a). Concomitantly, PDK1 was also partially shifted into these fractions. Fractionation of fibrillar collagen cultures, however, did not identify a shift in PDK1 migration (data not shown and see below).

Previous studies have shown that PP2A can form complexes with other PKC isoforms16, 17. Interestingly, the gel filtration studies demonstrated that levels of the phosphatase catalytic subunit (PP2Ac) were also increased in the high-molecular-weight PDK1/PKC-containing fractions from detached cells (Fig. 4a). Co-immunoprecipitation studies from HEK293 cells transfected with haemagglutinin (HA)-tagged PP2Ac and Myc-tagged PKC confirmed the existence of the PDK1–PKC–PP2A complex (Fig. 4b). PP2Ac was detected in PKC immunoprecipitates from both detached and fibrillar collagen cultures, but not from attached cells (Fig. 4b). Unlike PP2Ac, PDK1 co-immunoprecipitates with PKC only in detached cells, and not in fibrillar collagen cultures. Consistent with this selective association, immunoprecipitation of HA–PP2Ac recovered both PDK1 and PKC from detached cell extracts (indicative of a heterotrimeric complex), but only PKC from fibrillar collagen extracts (Fig. 4b). Collectively, these observations indicate that in detached cells, PDK1 is associated with PKC and PP2Ac. In fibrillar collagen, however, PP2Ac, but not PDK1, is associated with PKC.

To establish if PDK1 is binding to, and thus protecting, phosphorylation of the PKC Ser 729 site, wild-type PKC and the phosphorylation-site alanine mutant PKCS729A were cotransfected with PDK1 into PKC-/- MEF cells. Immunoprecipitation of PDK1 from attached or detached MEFs demonstrated that wild-type PKC selectively associated with PDK1 in detached cells only (Fig. 4c). In contrast, PKCS729A was not recovered in PDK1 immunoprecipitates from detached cells, despite a higher level of expression (Fig. 4c). Interestingly, the assembly of the PKC–PDK1 complex induced after cell detachment is inhibited by inclusion of the phosphatidylinositol-3-OH kinase (PI(3)K) inhibitor, LY294002, suggesting a role for PI(3)K in complex recruitment (Fig. 4c). These observations demonstrate that in detached cells, PDK1 is associated with PKC through the Ser 729-P hydrophobic motif.

After phosphatase inhibitor treatment of detached cells, PKC became rephosphorylated on its PDK1 site, Thr 566 (Fig. 4d), suggesting that in the detachment-induced PKC complex, associated PP2Ac regulates PKC function. This rephosphorylation requires associated PDK1, as no calyculin-induced phosphorylation at Thr 566 was detected for PKCS729A in detached cultures or in cells cultured on fibrillar collagen (Fig. 4d). This implies that the presence of PP2Ac in the complex with PKC is sufficient to suppress accumulation of Thr 566 phosphorylation catalysed by the associated PDK1 and that its inhibition is sufficient to reverse this effect.

On the basis of these studies, we would predict that calyculin treatment should be sufficient to rescue the IFN- response in detached cells, but not in cells grown on fibrillar collagen. Indeed, we found that calyculin treatment of detached HEK293 cultures substantially increased STAT1 Tyr 701 phosphorylation in response to IFN- (2.6 0.46-fold; n = 4), but had no significant effect on the response of cells cultured in fibrillar collagen (1.2 0.04-fold; n = 4; Fig. 5a, b). The requirement for active PKC in IFN- induced activation of STAT1 was demonstrated directly using a PP2A-resistant mutant of PKC (PKCT566E), whose kinase activity is similar to that of the wild-type kinase immunopurified from attached cells (data not shown). PKC-KO cells were cotransfected with GFP–STAT1 and either wild-type PKC or PKCT566E. Nuclear accumulation of GFP–STAT1 after IFN- stimulation was observed not only in attached cells, but also in detached cells expressing the PKCT556E mutant (Fig. 5c). Furthermore, in HEK293 cells, co-expression of GFP–STAT1 with PKCT556E, but not wild-type PKC, substantially rescued IFN--induced STAT1 phosphorylation in detached cells (Fig. 5d). Thus, PKC functions as a point of signal integration, facilitating the integrin-permissive input to IFN- signalling.

Full Figure and legend (92K)
The critical dependence of receptor-triggered signals on cell–substrate interactions has been long established; however the molecular basis of such permissive inputs has remained largely obscure. Here, we demonstrate that integrin engagement determines the phosphorylation status and activity of PKC through differential complex formation, correlating with the capacity of IFN- to transmit signals through the transcription factor STAT1. The reduced IFN- response in PKC-knockout MEFs indicates that the presence and/or activity of PKC is required. The integrin-dependent changes in PKC phosphorylation are reflected in changes in activity and we demonstrate that PKC kinase activity is indeed essential for the altered IFN- response. The influence of PKC on IFN- signals occurs downstream of the receptor, as there is no change in IFN- signalling to JAK1 or JAK2. Thus, the role of PKC is to relay integrin control and facilitate the coupling of JAK1/2 to its substrate STAT1.

The detachment/fibrillar collagen-dependent formation a PP2A complex with PKC determines the dephosphorylation of PKC. Under detached conditions, this is limited by the association of PDK1 at the carboxy-terminal PKC hydrophobic site, which appears to be sufficient to protect this site from dephosphorylation in the acute phase. This difference in acute detachment versus fibrillar collagen response is important in setting a cellular threshold for PKC re-activation and the recovery of IFN- signalling. The presence or absence of PDK1 not only parallels the state of Ser 729 phosphorylation, but also the efficiency with which PKC can become re-phosphorylated. Rapid recovery of PKC activity as a result of Thr 566 phosphorylation can be achieved after phosphatase inhibition if PDK1 is retained in the PKC complex. However, when all three sites are dephosphorylated (that is, in fibrillar collagen), the rescue of PKC activity is attenuated, resulting in a sustained impairment of INF-induced STAT1 phosphorylation.

To date, several protein kinases have been shown to associate with PP2A4, 16, 17, 18, 19. However it is significant that complex formation between PKC and PP2A is not constitutive, but seems to be delicately regulated by changes in integrin engagement. This provides a mechanism whereby PKC functions as a dynamic control point to co-ordinate cell function in response to environmental changes. In summary, PKC is established as a point of convergence for the pathways operating downstream of the IFN- receptor and integrins.

Methods
Collagen gels
Collagen gels were prepared from bovine dermal collagen. Eight volumes of Cellon were mixed with one volume of 10-fold-concentrated DMEM and one volume of Hepes–sodium hydroxide buffer (0.2 M at pH 7.5) and kept on ice. Cells were trypsinized, resuspended in 1/10 gel volume of DMEM, mixed into neutralized Cellon solution and transferred into cell culture plates. The collagen was allowed to polymerize for 1 h at 37 °C, one gel volume of DMEM was then added and the incubation was continued for another hour. After the 2-h incubation, IFN- (10U ml-1) or calyculin A (10 nM) was added to the DMEM for the indicated times. The release of cells from collagen gels was achieved by brief treatment with 0.5 mg ml-1 collagenase (type II; Sigma, St Louis, MO) in PBS (pH 7.4) at 37 °C before centrifugation (2,000g, 4 min) to isolate the released cells.

Immunoblot analysis
Cells harvested by collagenase were lysed in Laemmli sample buffer. Alternatively, cells on tissue culture plates were washed once with PBS and scraped directly into Laemmli sample buffer. The samples were sonicated, fractionated by SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) and transferred to nitrocellulose membranes.

Western blotting was performed with commercial antibodies raised against STAT1, STAT1-Tyr 701-P (New England Biolabs, Beverly, MA), PKC (Santa Cruz Biottechnology, Santa Cruz, CA), PKC (BDBiosciences, Lexington, KY). The anti-PP2Ac17, -Myc (9E10; Cancer Research, London, UK), -EE (Cancer Research UK) and -HA (12CA5; Cancer Research UK) mouse monoclonal antibodies were used at a dilution of 1:1,000. PDK1 sheep antisera (a gift from D. Alessi, University of Dundee) was used at 1:4,000. The generation of the phosphorylation-state-specific PKC/ antibodies has been described previously9. Western blot analyses with these antibodies (1:2500) were performed in the presence of the cognate dephospho-peptide at 1 g ml-1. Specific binding of antibodies was detected with peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibodies and visualized by enhanced chemiluminescence detection (Amersham, Piscataway, NJ).

Cell culture, transfections and immunoprecipitation
Cell culture and transfection procedures for HEK293 cells are described previously20. Generation of PKC-null and PKC-reconstituted MEFs has been described previously21. These cells were transiently transfected on 10-cm dishes using the Fugene 6 (Roche Diagnostics, Indianapolis, IN) reagent as described previously21. The expression constructs used were PDK-EE (a gift from D. Alessi), Myc-PKC22, HA-PP2Ac (a gift from D. Brautigan, University of Virginia) and GFP-STAT1 (ref. 23). The construction of the PKC expression vector has been described previously21. The S729A and the T556E mutants of PKC were generated using PCR-based site-directed mutagenesis. For all experiments, cells were incubated for 36 h post-transfection and treated as indicated for each experiment. The cells were lysed in cell lysis buffer (1% NP-40, 10% glycerol, 137 mM sodium chloride, 20 mM Tris-HCl at pH 7.4, 20 mM sodium fluoride, 1 mM phenyl methylsulphonyl fluoride (PMSF) and 2 g ml-1 each of leupeptin, antipain and pepstatin) and equal amounts of protein from each sample were precleared with protein G–Sepharose for 30 min at 4 °C. After centrifugation, antibodies were incubated with protein G–Sepharose and immunoprecipitation was performed at 4 °C for 2 h. The immunoprecipitates were washed three times with cell lysis buffer. Protein kinase assays of immunoprecipitated PKC were performed as described previously20. The immunoprecipitation and phospho-tyrosine analysis of JAK1 and JAK2 was performed as described previously24. Protein kinase assays for the autophosphorylation of immunoprecipitated JAK1 and JAK2 were performed as described previously25.

Confocal microscopy
For analysis of attached cells, PKC-expressing MEFs were plated overnight on acid-washed glass coverslips in DMEM containing 10% foetal calf serum (FCS). The cells were then treated for 20 min with 10U ml-1 IFN- or left untreated, washed in PBS and fixed with 2% paraformaldehyde for 10 min. For analysis of detached cells, MEFs were trypsinized, washed with medium containing 0.2% (w/v) soybean trypsin inhibitor (Sigma), resuspended in serum-free DMEM, treated for 20 min with 10U ml-1 IFN- or left untreated, washed in PBS and fixed with 2% paraformaldehyde for 10 min. Cells were recovered by cytospin onto microscope slides (100g for 10 min). For antibody staining, cells were permeabilized in PBS/0.2% Triton X-100 and washed with PBS/1% (w/v) BSA for blocking. Cells were stained with antibodies to STAT1, STAT1-Tyr 701-P (New England Biolabs) overnight at 4 °C or with an anti-PKC antibody for 1 h at room temperature. Cells were then washed three times with PBS before detection with Cy3- or fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-conjugated anti-rabbit or anti-mouse antibodies (Jackson Immunoresearch Laboratories, West Grove, PA). After washing in PBS and water, cells were mounted in Mowiol (100 mM Tris-HCl at pH 8.5, 10% (w/v) Mowiol (Calbiochem, San Diego, CA) and 25% (v/v) glycerol containing antifade (2.5% (w/v) 1,4-diazadicyclo-2.2.2-octane (Sigma)). Slides were examined using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Axioplan 2 with LSM 510; Carl Zeiss Inc., Jena, Germany) equipped with 63/1.4 Plan-Apochromat oil immersion objectives. Each image represents a single z-section of approximately 1.0 m.

Cell fractionations
Detached cells were harvested before or after IFN- stimulation (10U ml-1, 20 min) by centrifugation and lysed in cytosolic extraction buffer (10 mM potassium chloride, 20 mM Hepes, 0.2% NP-40, 1mM EDTA, 10% glycerol, 0.1 mM vanadate, 0.1 mM PMSF, 0.1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) and Complete protease inhibitors (Boehringer Mannheim, Germany) at pH 7.6) for 20 min on ice. Attached cells were harvested directly into extraction buffer from the plates. The extracts were spun at 3000g at 4 °C for 3 min and the supernatants were used as cytosolic extracts for immunoblotting. Pellets were washed twice in cytosolic extraction buffer before resuspension in nuclear extraction buffer (420 mM potassium chloride, 20 mM Hepes, 0.1 mM vanadate, 20% glycerol, 1 mM EDTA, 0.1 mM PMSF, 1 mM DTT and complete protease inhibitors at pH 7.6) and left on ice for 20 min. The extracts were centrifuges at 15,000g for 20 min at 4 °C and the supernatants were used as nuclear extracts.

Gel filtration and cross-linking
The cross-linking reagent DTSSP (3,3'-dithiobissulphosuccinimidylpropionate) was obtained from Pierce. HEK293 cells (15-cm dish) were treated as indicated (lysed on the plate for attached cells, detached with trypsin for detached cells, or cultured in collagen for 2 h for fibrillar collagen) and homogenized on ice (two 15-s sonication bursts) in buffer A (25 mM Hepes at pH 7.6, 150 mM sodium chloride, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, 1 mM PMSF, 10 g ml-1 leupeptin, 20 g ml-1 soybean trypsin inhibitor, 2 g ml-1 pepstatin and 2 mM benzamidine) containing 5 mM DTSSP for 15 min on ice. Excess reactive cross-linker was quenched by the addition of 0.1 M Tris-HCl at pH 7.5 before 0.5% CHAPS was added to the whole cell lysate in buffer A. Insoluble material was removed by centrifugation (12,000g for 10 min at 4 °C) and fractionated by FPLC on a preparative Superdex-200 gel filtration (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). For each run, 50 fractions (each 50 l) were collected. The fractions were pooled as indicated and analysed by immunoblotting.
