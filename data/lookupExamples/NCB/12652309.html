Cytoprotective membrane-permeable peptides designed from the Bax-binding domain of Ku70

Motoshi Sawada, Paulette Hayes & Shigemi Matsuyama

Blood Research Institute, The Blood Center of South Eastern Wisconsin and Department of Biochemistry, Medical College of Wisconsin 8727 Watertown Plank Rd, Milwaukee WI 53226

Correspondence should be addressed to Shigemi Matsuyama smatsuyama@bcsew.edu
Bax is a pro-apoptotic member of Bcl-2 family proteins and is central to mitochondria-dependent apoptosis1, 2, 3. Bax resides in the cytosol as a quiescent protein and translocates into mitochondria after apoptotic stimuli4. Ku70 is a 70K subunit of the Ku complex, which has an important role in DNA double-strand break (DSB) repair in the nucleus5. In another article in this issue, we reported that Ku70 interacts with pro-apoptotic protein Bax in the cytosol and prevents its mitochondrial translocation6, suggesting that Ku70 suppresses Bax-mediated apoptosis. Here, we describe the development of a new membrane-permeable peptide, Bax-inhibiting peptide (BIP) that inhibits Bax-mediated apoptosis, on the basis of the previous finding that showed an interaction between Ku70 and Bax. BIP is comprised of five amino acids designed from the Bax-binding domain of Ku70, and suppresses the mitochondrial translocation of Bax. BIP inhibited Bax-mediated apoptosis induced by staurosporine, UVC irradiation and anti-cancer drugs in several types of cells. BIP may provide valuable information in the development of therapeutics that control apoptosis-related diseases.

We reported that the carboxy-terminal 74 amino acids of Ku70 are sufficient to bind Bax and to inhibit Bax-mediated apoptosis in several cell types6. Recent structural analysis of Ku70 revealed that the 74-amino-acid C-terminal portion is comprised of three -helices and four flexible loop domains5, 7 (Fig. 1a). Further analysis of a series of deletion mutants of Ku70 revealed that the C-terminal 32 amino acids are sufficient to inhibit Bax-induced apoptosis in HEK293T cells (Fig. 1a–c). A Flag-tagged Ku70 mutant expressing amino acids 578–609 of Ku70 (Ku70578–609) bound to endogenous Bax, whereas a Ku70 mutant in which these amino acids have been deleted (Ku701–577) did not (Fig. 1b). Furthermore, Ku70578–609, but not Ku701–577, suppressed Bax-induced apoptosis in HEK293T cells (Fig. 1c). These results suggest that the Bax-inhibiting domain is located between amino acids 578–609 of Ku70.

Previous reports suggest that there are two -helices in these 32 amino acids5, 7 (Fig. 1a). Synthetic peptides corresponding to these two -helices were made and their ability to suppress Bax-induced apoptosis was examined (Fig. 1d). As these peptides are not membrane-permeable, fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-labelled peptides were delivered into cells by a liposome-mediated approach. The presence of the peptides in cells was confirmed by examining FITC fluorescence (data not shown). The peptide corresponding to amino acids 578–587, but not 596–600, inhibited Bax-induced apoptosis, suggesting that the Bax-inhibiting domain is located in the second -helix from the C terminus of Ku70 (Fig. 1d).

Further analysis of the peptides designed from the Ku70 Bax-inhibiting domain revealed that six amino acids (VPMLKE; V6 peptide) in the Bax-inhibiting domain (amino acids 578–587) of Ku70 is sufficient to suppress Bax-mediated apoptosis (through overexpression of Bax, staurosporine (STS) treatment or UVC-induced apoptosis; data not shown). However, the V6 peptide is not membrane-permeable and liposome-mediated delivery of the peptide is required to suppress Bax-mediated apoptosis. Interestingly, the deletion of one amino acid from either the N or C terminus of VPMLKE made these peptides membrane-permeable and did not abrogate their Bax-inhibiting activity (Fig. 1e, f). In fact, the five-amino-acid peptides VPMLK (V5) and PMLKE (P5) were equally effective in suppressing apoptosis induced by overexpression of Bax (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1, for FACS analysis). As V5 and P5 are membrane-permeable, addition of these peptides into the culture medium is sufficient to block Bax-mediated apoptosis. However, further deletion of one amino acid resulted in the abrogation of Bax-inhibiting activity (Fig. 1f). Both PMLK (P4) and MLKE (M4) could not suppress Bax-mediated apoptosis (data not shown). In addition, when the Val and Leu residues in the V5 peptide (VPMLK) were changed to Ile, another non-polar amino acid, the peptide (IPMIK) lost its ability to suppress apoptosis, although it retained membrane permeability (Fig. 1e, f). The mutant peptide IPMIK was used as a negative control (NC) peptide in subsequent experiments.

The BIP peptides had anti-apoptotic activities at 50–200 M (Fig. 1e), and therefore function as effectively as the caspase inhibitor z-VAD-fmk. Pretreatment of HeLa cells with BIP peptides for 1 h was sufficient to provide protection from STS- and UVC-induced apoptosis (Fig. 2a, b). It should also be noted that BIPs were both stable and functional for at least three days in the culture medium (Fig. 2a, b). BIP did not enhance z-VAD-fmk-mediated suppression of apoptosis induced by UVC irradiation (Fig. 2c) or STS treatment (data not shown). As Bax functions upstream of caspase activation, BIP may not be able to show the additive effects to the caspase inhibitor. BIPs also suppressed apoptosis induced by the anti-cancer drugs etoposide, cisplatin and doxorubicin in several types of cancer cell lines, including glioma cells (U87-MG; Fig. 2d–f), breast cancer cells (MCF-7) and prostate cancer cells (LNCaP; see Supplementary Information, Fig. S2a–f). To confirm that BIP specifically suppresses Bax-mediated apoptosis, the effects of BIPs in Bax-deficient cells (DU145) were examined. BIPs did not suppress STS- or UVC-induced apoptosis in DU145 cells, whereas BIP showed anti-apoptotic activity in these cells when non-toxic levels of Bax expression were restored by plasmid transfection (Fig. 2g, h and see Supplementary Information, Figs S2g, h and S3a, b). This suggests that BIP specifically inhibits the Bax-mediated signal in apoptosis. These results are consistent with the observations that Ku70 does not confer cytoprotection against STS treatment or UVC irradiation in Bax-deficient cells, and that Ku70 does not block Bak-induced apoptosis6. Furthermore, BIPs did not suppress Fas- and TRAIL-induced apoptosis (data not shown). Fas and TRAIL can trigger the mitochondria-independent cell death pathway8, 9; therefore, BIP may not be able to suppress apoptosis induced by these factors. These results suggest that BIP only suppresses Bax-mediated apoptosis. However, BIP suppressed STS- and UVC-induced apoptosis in Ku70-deficient mouse embryonic fibroblasts (MEFs), suggesting that BIP does not require endogenous Ku70 to suppress Bax-mediated apoptosis (Fig. 2i, j)

BIP (V5) suppressed the mitochondrial translocation of Bax stimulated by STS treatment and UVC irradiation (Fig. 3a) as efficiently as Ku70 (ref. 6). As we reported, apoptotic stimuli decrease Ku70 levels in the cytosol6. BIP did not inhibit the disappearance of Ku70 from the cytosol (Fig. 3b), suggesting that the anti-apoptotic activity of BIP is not caused by inhibiting the degradation of endogenous Ku70. The release of cytochrome c from mitochondria and caspase activation triggered by apoptotic stimuli were also significantly suppressed by BIP (Fig. 3c, d), further supporting the hypothesis that BIP protects cells by inhibiting the Bax-mediated mitochondria-dependent apoptosis pathway. The interaction of BIP and Bax was confirmed by experiments using biotin-labelled BIP (biotin–VPMLK) and streptavidin beads. Biotin–VPMLK precipitated Bax from cell lysates when streptavidin beads were added to the samples (Fig. 3e). In contrast, Bax was not precipitated when a negative control peptide (biotin–IPMIK) was used, explaining why IPMIK (NC peptide in the figures) did not suppress Bax-mediated cell death. Furthermore, Bak was not precipitated by biotin–VPMLK (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S3c). Scatchard analysis with FITC–BIP (VPMLK) and endogenous Bax in Ku70-deficeint MEF cell lysates suggested that the BIP–Bax interaction has a Kd of 1.3 M (Fig. 3f). Cells treated with FITC–BIP (VPMLK) are shown (Fig. 4a, b). FITC–BIP was localized mostly in the cytosol in HeLa cells (Fig. 4a), but was distributed in both the cytosol and the nucleus in Bax-deficient DU145 cells (Fig. 4b). Expression of Bax in DU145 cells increased the cytosolic levels of FITC–BIP (Fig. 4c–f), suggesting that BIP binds to cytosolic Bax. Colocalization of Bax and BIP was also observed in HeLa cells (Fig. 4g–i). These results further support the hypothesis that BIP inhibits Bax-mediated apoptosis by interacting with Bax in the cytosol. It has been suggested that the conformational change of Bax (exposure of the N terminus) occurs before its mitochondrial translocation10 and that Ku70 inhibits this event6. The monoclonal antibody clone 6A7 is known to detect exposure of the Bax N terminus11, 12. BIP reduced the amount of Bax protein recognized by the 6A7 antibody in cells treated with apoptotic stimuli (Fig. 4j), suggesting that similarly to Ku70, BIP inhibits the conformational change of Bax.

BIP (VPMLK (V5) and PMLKE (P5)) inhibited the interaction (co-immunoprecipitation) of endogenous Bax and Ku70 in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 5a, b). However, BIP did not interfere with the Ku70–Ku80 interaction or with Bax–Bcl-2 heterodimerization (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S3d, e). These results suggest that BIP specifically interacts with Bax through the Ku70-binding domain in Bax and that BIP competes with Ku70 to bind to Bax. Interestingly, addition of PMLK (P4) to cell lysates inhibited the interaction of Bax and Ku70 (Fig. 5c), although PMLK (P4) could not suppress apoptosis in cell culture. In contrast, MLKE (M4) did not interfere with the Bax–Ku70 interaction (Fig. 5d). These results suggest that four amino acids of PMLK may be sufficient to bind to Bax, at least in vitro. We have shown that VPMLK and PMLKE can suppress Bax-induced apoptosis (Fig. 1). Although further analysis is required, the reason why PMLK did not suppress apoptosis in culture may be because it is rapidly degraded or modified in living cells. It is also possible that addition of Val (N terminus) or Glu (C terminus) to PMLK may improve the membrane permeability or the cytosolic retention of the peptide. Future biochemical studies should answer this question.

In summary, we have developed membrane-permeable peptides (BIPs) from the Bax-binding domain of Ku70 that inhibit Bax-mediated apoptosis. We also found that BIP inhibits exposure of the Bax N terminus induced by apoptotic stimuli. However, the mechanism of Bax activation remains unclear. It may be possible to use BIP as a tool to elucidate the mechanism of the Bax conformational change. At present, it is unclear whether BIP can bind Bax directly. It remains possible that other factors are involved in this interaction. Future biochemical study to analyse complex formation of purified inactive Bax proteins and peptides may answer these questions. Bax-mediated cell death is known to be involved in several types of degenerative diseases and in the loss of viability of cultured cells13, 14, 15. The membrane-permeable Bax-inhibiting peptide developed in this study may provide the valuable information required for the development of new therapeutics to control apoptosis-related diseases.

Methods
Cell culture and detection of apoptosis
HEK293T and HeLa cells were cultured in MEM supplemented with 10% foetal bovine serum (FBS). MEFs and DU145 cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS. Transfections were performed with SuperFect (Qiagen, Valencia, CA) in accordance with the manufacturer's instructions. Apoptosis was induced by Bax overexpression (1 g of pcDNA3-human Bax for 106 HEK293T cells), STS treatment (200 nM for HeLa cells) or UVC irradiation (600 J m-2 for HeLa cells and 1,200 J m-2 for DU145 cells). To examine anti-apoptotic activity of Flag–Ku70 (wild type and mutant), 1 g of Bax plasmid and 2 g of Flag–Ku70 (wild type and mutant) plasmid were cotransfected into 106 HEK293T cells. One day after transfection, apoptosis was analysed. Apoptosis in transfected cells was analysed as follows: a plasmid encoding green fluorescent protein (GFP; 0.5 g for 106 cells) was transfected into all the groups to mark transfected cells. After treatment, cells were stained with Hoechst dye and cells with apoptotic nuclei were counted in GFP-expressing cells under a fluorescence microscope, as previously described14. Each point in the figures showing percentages of apoptosis represents the mean s.e.m. of three experiments. Caspase activities of cells were measured by detecting cleavage of the fluorogenic caspase substrate, DEVD-amino-4-trifluoromethylcoumarin (DEVD-afc), as previously described11.

Plasmids
The plasmid pcDNA3-Bax (human) has been described6. The plasmid vectors pCMV-2B and pEGFP were purchased from Stratagene (Cedar Creek, TX) and Clontech (Palo Alto, CA), respectively, and human full-length Ku70 and the deletion mutants of Ku70 were subcloned into the BamHI and XhoI sites of pCMV-2B vector. The deletion mutants of Bax were subcloned into EcoRI and SalI sites of pEGFP plasmid. The full-length Ku70 cDNA was prepared by RT-PCR using HeLa cell cDNA6. The mutant constructs of Ku70 described were prepared by the second step PCR mutagenesis method6.

Delivery of peptide into cells
For the membrane-permeable peptide, the stock solution was prepared in PBS and the peptides were added directly into medium at 37 °C for 1 h (or for the indicated periods (Fig. 2a, b)) before apoptosis-inducing treatment. Non-membrane-permeable peptide was delivered into cells using the BioPorter reagent (Gene Therapy Systems, San Diego, CA) according to the manufacturer's instructions. BioPorter-based peptide delivery was performed 4 h before apoptosis-inducing treatment.

Cytochrome c detection
One day after transfection, STS treatment or UVC irradiation, cells were resuspended in 200 l of homogenization buffer (250 mM sucrose, 20 mM Hepes at pH 7.5, 10 mM potassium chloride, 1.5 mM magnesium chloride, 1 mM EDTA and 0.1 mM phenylmethylsulphonyl fluoride) and separation of the cytosol and heavy membrane fraction (containing mitochondria and ER) was performed as previously reported6. A cytosolic fraction (20 g protein) and 1 l of membrane fraction (out of 50 l total) were analysed by western blotting with an anti-cytochrome c antibody (dilution 1:1000; BD-Pharmingen (San Diego, CA).

Immunoprecipitation
Endogenous protein: HEK293T (107 cells) were lysed in 200 l of Chaps buffer (150 mM NaCl, 10 mM Hepes at pH 7.4 and 1.0% Chaps) containing protease inhibitors (100 times dilution of Protease Inhibitors Cocktail; Sigma, St Louis, MO) according to the previously reported method11, 12, 16. After preclearing of 200 l of the sample with 20 l of Protein A–Sepharose (CL-4B, 17-0780-01; Amersham, Piscataway, NJ) at 4 °C for 1 h, immunoprecipitations were performed by incubating 200 l of lysates with 2 g of anti-Bax polyclonal (Cat. #13666E, BD-Pharmingen) or anti-Ku70 monoclonal antibody (Cat. #69141A, BD-Pharmingen) at 4 °C for 2 h. In some cases, 2 g of anti-Bax monoclonal antibody (clone 6A7, BD-Pharmingen) was used to detect the active form of Bax. Immunocomplex in 200 l lysates was precipitated by 20 l of Protein G–Sepharose. After extensive washing in buffer, beads were boiled in 40 l of Laemmli buffer and 20 l of the eluted proteins were resolved by SDS–PAGE.

Co-precipitation of biotin-labelled peptides and endogenous Bax with Streptavidin beads: HEK293T cells (107 cells) lysed in 200 l Chaps-based buffer were incubated with biotin-labelled NC peptide or VPMLK (V5) peptide for 1 h. The concentrations of NC and V5 peptide labelled with biotin are described in the figure legends. Coprecipitation was performed with 20 l of Streptavidin beads (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech). Flag-tagged-Ku70 and endogenous Bax: HEK293T cells (107 cells) were transfected with 10 g of pCMV-2B-control vector (Flag-tagged firefly luciferase), pCMV-2B-Ku70wt (Flag–Ku70wt), pCMV-2B-Ku701–577 (Flag–Ku701–577) or pCMV-2B-Ku70578–609(Flag–Ku70578–609) in the presence of 50 M z-VAD-fmk. Co-immunoprecipitation was performed with anti-Flag monoclonal antibody (Cat. #200472, Stratagene; 2 g for 200 l sample) and western blot analysis of Bax (15% SDS–PAGE) was performed with anti-human Bax polyclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen).

Subcellular fractionation
One day after treatment, cells (107 cells) were homogenized with 200 l of ice-cold homogenization buffer (250 mM sucrose, 20 mM Hepes at pH 7.5, 10 mM potassium chloride, 1.5 mM magnesium chloride, 1 mM EDTA and 0.1 mM phenylmethylsulphonyl fluoride). Subcellular fractionation was performed as reported6, together with the confirmation of each fraction with appropriate marker proteins (cytosolic fraction, Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) with anti-human LDH antibody (Cat. #L7016, Sigma); nucleus fraction, Proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) with anti-PCNA antibody (Cat. #NA03, Oncogene); mitochondria containing heavy membrane fraction, F1-ATPase -subunit (F1) with anti-F1 subunit antibody (Cat. #A-11144, Molecular Probes). For total cell lysates, samples were prepared with ice-cold lysis buffer (containing 50 mM sodium chloride, 25 mM Hepes at pH 7.4, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, 1 mM PMSF, 10 g l-1 E-64 and 1% Triton X-100). In some experiments, samples containing 20 g of protein from total cell lysates or cytosolic fraction were subjected to western blot analysis of Bax or Ku70. The pellets of the fractions of heavy membrane and nucleus were dissolved in 50 l of Laemmli buffer and the same proportion of the volume equal to that of the cytosol samples out of its total volume was used for western blot analysis.

Fluorescence microscopy
HeLa cells and DU145 cells (106 cells; untransfected or transfected with pcDNA3-Bax) were incubated with 200 M FITC-labelled VPMLK (V5) peptide for 1 h before microscopic analysis. For immunostaining, cells incubated with FITC–V5 peptide for 1 h were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS. Cells were permeablized with 0.5% Triton X-100 and 0.05% Tween-20 in PBS. Fixed cells were incubated in PBS containing 5% BSA at 37 °C and stained with anti-Bax monoclonal antibody (BD-Pharmingen; dilution 1:50) followed by detection of Texas-Red-labelled anti-mouse IgG (Jackson ImmunoResearch, dilution 1:100).

Scatchard analysis of FITC–V5–Bax binding
Ku70-deficient MEFs (107 cells) were lysed in 200 l of detergent-free hypotonic buffer (hypotonic (5 mM sodium chloride) PBS at pH 7.4) containing protease inhibitors (100 times dilution of Protease Inhibitors Cocktail; Sigma) according to the previously reported method11, 12. For Scatchard analysis, the cytosol fraction was used and sodium chloride was added to prepare the isotonic condition before immunoprecipitation, as previously reported11, 12. After incubating the sample with various concentrations of FITC-labelled VPMLK (V5) peptide at 37 °C for 1 h, immunoprecipitations were performed under detergent-free conditions at 37 °C by incubating 200 l of lysates with 2 g of anti-Bax polyclonal antibody (Cat. #13686E, BD-Pharmingen) for 2 h. Immunocomplex in 200 l lysates was precipitated by 20 l of Protein-A–Sepharose. FITC fluorescence of pre-immunoprecipitation (B + F) and the supernatant after immunoprecipitation (F) were measured by fluorescence microplate reader (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA), and the values of B+F and B were calculated. Kd was calculated by Scatchard plot. For control experiment, immunodepletion of Bax was performed before incubation of FITC–V5 and cell lysates. Cell lysates (200 l) were mixed with 4 g of anti-Bax antibody and incubated at 37 °C for 2 h for immunodepletion of Bax. No significant binding of FITC–V5 to cellular components was detected in Bax-immunodepleted samples.
