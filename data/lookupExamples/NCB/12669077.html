A retention factor keeps death at bay

Steven F. Nothwehr1 & Jean-Claude Martinou2

1 Steven F. Nothwehr is in the Division of Biological Sciences, University of Missouri, Columbia, 65211 MO, USA.

2 Jean-Claude Martinou is in the Department of Cell Biology, University of Geneva,Quai Ernest-Ansermet 30, CH1211 - Geneva 4, Geneva, Switzerland.

Correspondence should be addressed to Jean-Claude Martinou jean-claude.martinou@cellbio.unige.ch
Apoptosis is a carefully orchestrated series of events that allows cells to be eliminated without damaging their neighbours. Regulation is essential, as too much apoptosis can contribute to neurodegenerative disorders, whereas too little can result in cancer. Two reports in this issue now demonstrate that Ku70, a protein previously shown to function in DNA repair, has an important role in regulating apoptotic events.
In recent years, it has become clear that many apoptotic responses converge on the mitochondrion, resulting in the release of multiple death-promoting factors (cytochrome c, SMAC/Diablo, apoptosis-inducing factor, endonuclease G) into the cytosol1. Once released, these factors activate caspases, a family of death-inducing proteases that cleave a wide array of cellular targets, ultimately resulting in the demise of the cell. Release of these mitochondrial proteins occurs through permeablization of the outer mitochondrial membrane and is controlled by members of the Bcl-2 family, which are either pro-apoptotic (for example, Bax, Bak and Bid) or anti-apoptotic (for example, Bcl-2 and Bcl-xL)2. In healthy cells, Bax is predominantly localized to the cytosol in an inactive form. After exposure to apoptotic stimulation, however, Bax undergoes a variety of conformational changes3 and translocates to the outer mitochondrial membrane, where it oligomerizes. This results in permeablization of the outer membrane, possibly through formation of lipid pores4, 5. In contrast, Bcl-xL prevents activation of Bax on the mitochondrial outer membrane and preserves membrane integrity.

Clearly, an important point of regulation in this process is the activation of Bax and its translocation to the mitochondrial outer membrane. Proteins that contain a single BH3 domain, such as the carboxy-terminal portion of Bid (tBid), bind directly to Bax and are essential for its activation6, 7. In liver cells, Bax is activated by tBid, which is produced by caspase 8-mediated cleavage in response to a death signal from tumour necrosis factor (TNF)/Fas receptors8. tBid induces a conformational change in an amino-terminal domain of Bax that allows insertion of Bax into the outer membrane through its C-terminal hydrophobic domain and oligomerization9. Until now, little was known of factors (other than Bcl-2 anti-apoptotic members) that directly inhibit Bax in healthy cells. On page 320 of this issue Sadawa et al.10 demonstrate that the Ku70 protein, a molecule previously shown to function in DNA repair, has an important role in inhibiting Bax translocation to the mitochondria. In addition, on page 352 they describe the discovery of a membrane-permeable Ku70-derived peptide that can block certain types of apoptosis11.

Using a yeast-based functional screen, Sawada et al.10 identify Ku70 as an inhibitor of Bax function. Overexpression of Ku70 in mammalian cell lines efficiently blocked apoptosis induced by a number of stimuli that are known to work through Bax; it did not, however, abrogate apoptotic responses that are not mediated by mitochondria. In contrast, depletion of Ku70 rendered cells more sensitive to apoptotic stimuli known to work through Bax. The specificity of Ku70 for Bax is further emphasized by the finding that Ku70 did not block Bak-mediated cell death in Bax-knockout cells. So how does Ku70 inhibit Bax function? A previously identified protein, Bax inhibitor-1, inhibited Bax-mediated apoptosis, but did not associate with Bax itself and probably functions at a downstream step12. In contrast, binding of Ku70 to Bax is dependent on the N-terminal 53 amino acids of Bax. Overexpression of Ku70 efficiently prevented translocation of Bax from the cytosol to mitochondria under conditions of apoptotic stress. Remarkably, a large proportion of Bax was found in complex with Ku70 in healthy cells. One possibility is that Ku70 may prevent the conformational change in the N terminus of Bax, which is known to be necessary for translocation and activation; consistently, binding of an anti-Bax monoclonal was decreased after overexpression of Ku70. In addition, previous work has identified a cytosolic retention signal in the N terminus of Bax13, 14 and therefore it is possible that Ku70 recognizes this signal. Alternatively, perhaps no conformational change occurs at the N terminus, but the N-terminal epitope is instead masked by Ku70. Activation would then involve dissociation of Ku70 and subsequent binding by BH3-only proteins and possibly also by other Bax activators (Fig. 1a). However, more work will be needed to determine the precise site on Bax that Ku70 binds to. So far, the association of Ku70 with Bax has only been analysed in tissue culture cell lines. Activation of Bax seems to be a multistep process3 and Bax may already be partially activated in cell lines, compared with intact tissues. Thus, it will be important to investigate the Bax–Ku70 interaction in tissues.

Figure 1. Activation of Bax in response to an apoptotic signal.

a, Activation of Bax during apoptosis requires the release of Ku70 through a mechanism that remains to be determined. As a result, the N terminus of Bax is exposed, allowing the protein to form a large, transient activation complex ('Baxosome') that includes Bax itself, BH3-only proteins, membrane lipids (cardiolipin (CL); brown) and possibly other unidentified proteins. Bax then undergoes conformational changes that allow it to be inserted into the outer mitochondrial membrane (light blue) and to oligomerize, resulting in the release of mitochondrial apoptotic factors into the cytosol. b, Dual activity of Ku70. After DNA damage, levels of Ku70 are upregulated. Ku70 inhibits the apoptosis programme by interfering directly with Bax activation. Ku70 is also involved in DNA repair, where it functions as the DNA-binding component (in conjunction with Ku80) of a DNA-dependent protein kinase.

During the initiation of an apoptotic response, an inhibitor of Bax would need to be downregulated in some way. Interestingly, the levels of immunoreactive Ku70 are reduced in cells undergoing apoptosis10, 15. The issue of whether caspase-mediated cleavage is responsible for the drop in Ku70 immunoreactivity during apoptosis remains controversial. In principle, Ku70 could be post-translationally modified in response to a death signal in such a way as to reduce binding by anti-Ku70 antibodies. Although more work will be needed to understand the mechanism involved, these results point to a novel strategy for initiating the activation of Bax.

It should be noted that in addition to Ku70, other factors help to regulate the translocation of Bax from the cytosol to mitochondria and insertion of its C-terminal hydrophobic domain into the membrane. For example, in Ku70-knockout cells, Bax did not exhibit translocation from the cytosol to mitochondria in the absence of apoptotic stresses. Thus, there could be other additional retention factors that prevent relocation of Bax under conditions in which Bax should be kept inactive. Moreover, it is unlikely that the conformational changes required for Bax activation are a passive mechanism resulting from the release of such retention factors. Instead, an active process involving BH3-only proteins, unidentified mitochondrial membrane-associated proteins16 and possibly lipids such as cardiolipin17 may be necessary to change the conformation of Bax and allow its oligomerization (Fig. 1a). Thus, new factors that regulate Bax activity await discovery.

In addition to this new apoptotic role in the cytoplasm, it has been known for some time that Ku70 has a very different function in the nucleus — DNA repair. A dimer containing Ku70 and another polypeptide, Ku80, mediates the non-homologous repair of double-stranded DNA breaks18. Interestingly, a small C-terminal domain of Ku70 is necessary and sufficient for association/inhibition of Bax, whereas the domains necessary for dimerization with Ku80 map to other regions of the molecule. Although it has been known for some time that overexpression of Ku70 protects against apoptotic stimuli that induce double-stranded DNA breaks, it was less clear why Ku70 would protect against apoptotic stimuli that do not. The data from Sadawa et al.10 help to link these observations by showing that the cytoprotective activity of Ku70 works, at least in part, through inhibition of Bax.

A cell that has undergone DNA damage needs to decide whether to repair the damage or to initiate apoptosis. Ionizing irradiation causes double-stranded breaks in DNA and activates p53 (ref. 19), which in turn upregulates a number of molecules required for the damage response. One such molecule is Bax, which can then propel the cell into apoptosis. Interestingly, Ku70 is also upregulated by ionizing irradiation20 and given its dual nature, would be well suited to participate in the repair/apoptosis decision. Increasing Ku70 levels would enhance the ability of the cell to repair the DNA damage and simultaneously would reduce the tendency to initiate Bax-mediated apoptosis (Fig. 1b).

In a related study from the same group11, the domain of Ku70 that binds to Bax has been defined in great detail, allowing the design of a five-amino-acid peptide that inhibits Bax function. This peptide is membrane permeable and very stable in culture media. Inhibition of Bax by the peptide seems to occur by the same mechanism as full-length Ku70, as the peptide competes with Ku70 for binding of Bax. Furthermore, cytoprotection by the peptide exhibits the same characteristics and specificity as Ku70. Hopefully, three-dimensional modelling of the peptide–Bax interaction should facilitate design of new drugs that inhibit Bax. Such therapeutics could be useful for inhibiting apoptosis in acute pathologies and degenerative diseases where it has run rampant.
