Human CENP-I specifies localization of CENP-F, MAD1 and MAD2 to kinetochores and is essential for mitosis

Song-Tao Liu1, James C. Hittle1, Sandra A. Jablonski1, Michael S. Campbell1, Kinya Yoda2 & Tim J. Yen1

1 Fox Chase Cancer Center, 7701 Burholme Avenue, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania 19111, USA

2 Bioscience Center, Nagoya University, Chikusa-ku, Nagoya 464-8601, Japan

Correspondence should be addressed to Tim J. Yen tj_yen@fccc.edu
The kinetochore, a macromolecular complex located at the centromere of chromosomes, provides essential functions for accurate chromosome segregation1, 2. Kinetochores contain checkpoint proteins that monitor attachments between the kinetochore and microtubules to ensure that cells do not exit mitosis in the presence of unaligned chromosomes3, 4. Here we report that human CENP-I, a constitutive protein of the kinetochore that shares limited similarity with Mis6 of Schizosaccharomyces pombe, is required for the localization of CENP-F and the checkpoint proteins MAD1 and MAD2 to kinetochores. Depletion of CENP-I from kinetochores causes the cell cycle to delay in G2. Although monopolar chromosomes in CENP-I-depleted cells fail to establish bipolar connections, the cells are unable to arrest in mitosis. These cells are transiently delayed in mitosis in a MAD2-dependent manner, even though their kinetochores are depleted of MAD2. The delay is extended considerably when the number of unattached kinetochores is increased. This suggests that no single unattached kinetochore in CENP-I-depleted cells can arrest mitosis. The collective output from many unattached kinetochores is required to reach a threshold signal of 'wait for anaphase' to sustain a prolonged mitotic arrest.

Human CENP-I (previously named LRPR1; ref. 5) is a protein of 756 amino acids that shows 72% and 56% identity to its respective rat and chicken6 homologues and limited similarity to Mis6 of S. pombe7 and Ctf3 of Saccharomyces cerevisiae8. Immunofluorescence staining with antibodies generated against human CENP-I showed that it was associated with the kinetochores of all interphase and mitotic cells (Supplementary Information Fig. 1a). We confirmed the immunofluorescence data by showing that a fusion protein of green fluorescent protein (GFP) and CENP-I also localized to kinetochores in transfected cells (Supplementary Information Fig. 1b). We used light microscopy to compare the localization of CENP-I relative to that of the inner and outer kinetochore proteins, CENP-A9, 10 and CENP-F11, respectively. CENP-I overlapped considerably with CENP-A, but it overlapped only partially with and was internal to CENP-F (Supplementary Information Fig. 1c). Thus, CENP-I is likely to be concentrated at or near the inner kinetochore plate.

We examined the fates of HeLa cells whose kinetochores were depleted of CENP-I by antibody injection (Supplementary Information Fig. 2) and identified three defects. Cells synchronized at the G1/S boundary were injected with antibodies against CENP-I and examined at various times after release from this block. At each time point, the number of cells in G2, in mitosis and those that had exited mitosis were compared (Fig. 1).

The first defect was that cell-cycle progression was significantly delayed in cells injected with antibodies against CENP-I, as compared with uninjected cells or cells injected with non-immune immunoglobulin- (IgG). Most control HeLa cells had already completed mitosis at 12 h after release from the G1/S boundary because the mitotic index at this time was only 6.0 4.8% (Fig. 1a). Injection of non-immune IgG did not adversely affect mitotic progression because the mitotic index at the same time point was 10.7 5.0%. By contrast, cells injected with antibodies against CENP-I had a mitotic index of 60.5 11.4% at this time point. Detailed analysis showed that the accumulation of mitotic cells resulted from both delayed entry into and exit from mitosis. We estimated that CENP-I-depleted cells show a delay of roughly 3 h in G2 progression and a second delay of 3–4 h in mitosis before exiting this stage (Fig. 1b).

The second defect in CENP-I-depleted cells was a high incidence of mitotic cells containing monopolar chromosomes. Roughly 20% of the cells appeared to be in prometaphase with all chromosomes unaligned (Fig. 2a, row 1). In the remaining 80% of the cells, however, most chromosomes appeared aligned at the metaphase plate but there were always a few chromosomes positioned close to one of the spindle poles or between a pole and the spindle equator (Fig. 2a, rows 2 and 3). This result obtained by antibody injection was confirmed by RNA interference (RNAi) experiments in which cells were transfected with a short interfering RNA (siRNA) targeted to CENP-I. In mock transfected cells, 7.4% (n = 216) of mitotic cells with identifiable metaphase plates contained several unaligned chromosomes, as compared with 72.8% (n = 190) of cells that lacked detectable CENP-I.

Closer inspection of the unaligned chromosomes showed that sister kinetochores faced opposite poles. In some cells, we could clearly see that the chromosome was mono-orientated because only one of the sister kinetochore pair was attached to the pole (Fig. 2b). Despite the similarity in phenotype with cells that lack the kinetochore motor CENP-E12, 13, kinetochores depleted of CENP-I retained CENP-E (Fig. 2a, third row). In addition, kinetochores depleted of CENP-I also retained CENP-A (Fig. 2a and Supplementary Information Fig. 2), as has been reported for chicken CENP-I6.

The third defect exhibited by CENP-I-depleted cells was a defective mitotic checkpoint. Analysis at 16 h after release from the G1/S boundary showed that cells with misaligned chromosomes did not arrest in mitosis but had divided to produce cells with micronuclei and abnormally shaped nuclei (Fig. 2a, row 4; compare the nuclei of the top uninjected and the bottom antibody-injected cell). Cells showing similar phenotype were also observed when RNAi was used to block the expression of CENP-I (data not shown).

We investigated the mechanism responsible for the mitotic checkpoint defect caused by depleting CENP-I from kinetochores. Cells injected with antibodies against CENP-I were fixed and stained with antibodies specific for various kinetochore proteins (Fig. 3a). Depletion of CENP-I from kinetochores did not affect localization of the checkpoint proteins hBUB1, hBUBR1, hZW10 or hROD. In addition, these checkpoint proteins seemed to respond normally to kinetochore–microtubule interactions because their quantities at unaligned kinetochores were noticeably higher than their quantities at kinetochores near the centre of the cell that appeared to have established bipolar attachments (Fig. 3a). However, kinetochores depleted of CENP-I by antibody injection lacked CENP-F and the checkpoint protein MAD2 (Fig. 3a). The depletion of CENP-F and MAD2 was not the result of kinetochore–microtubule attachments because these proteins were clearly absent even at unattached kinetochores. Instead, both CENP-F and MAD2 accumulated throughout the cytoplasm of the CENP-I-depleted cells.

We verified these results by analysing kinetochores in which CENP-I had been depleted by siRNA (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Information Fig. 3). Consistent with the antibody injection data, localization of MAD2 and CENP-F to kinetochores was sensitive to the loss of CENP-I. In addition, we found that localization of MAD1 to the kinetochore was dependent on CENP-I. The disappearance of CENP-F, MAD1 and MAD2 was not caused by degradation because protein quantities were comparable in lysates from control cells and those from cells treated with CENP-I siRNA (Supplementary Information Fig. 4). As shown in antibody injection experiments, the localization of hBUB1, hBUBR1, hROD, hZW10, CENP-E, CENP-A to kinetochores was unaffected in cells depleted of CENP-I by siRNA (Supplementary Information Fig. 3 and data not shown). CENP-I therefore specifies a discrete branch of the kinetochore assembly pathway.

Although CENP-I-depleted cells could delay mitotic progression, as exemplified by the large increase in its mitotic index, these cells delayed mitosis only transiently (3–4 h) before exiting in the presence of unaligned chromosomes. This transient mitotic delay was unexpected because the unattached kinetochores of these cells lacked detectable MAD2. Our observation would seem to be inconsistent with studies that have shown that inhibition of MAD2 accelerates cells with unaligned chromosomes out of mitosis3, 14. This discrepancy could be resolved if MAD2 can function at two steps along the checkpoint pathway: that is, MAD2 can act at unattached kinetochores to generate the 'wait for anaphase' signal, and a separate MAD2 population can act downstream of the kinetochores by directly inhibiting the anaphase-promoting complex3, 15, 16. Although depletion of CENP-I selectively blocked MAD2 function at kinetochores, it might not directly interfere with downstream functions of MAD2 that could be responsible for the transient delay seen in these cells.

We therefore tested whether MAD2 is still required for CENP-I-depleted cells to delay mitotic progression despite its absence at kinetochores. We first injected antibodies against CENP-I into cells to induce a transient delay in mitosis. As shown above, MAD2 was not detectable at the kinetochores of these cells. Cells delayed in mitosis were then re-injected with non-immune IgG or antibodies against MAD2 and examined 1 h and 2 h later. After 1 h, only 1.2% (1 of 81) of the cells injected with non-immune IgG had exited mitosis. By contrast, 67.3% (33/49) of the cells injected with antibodies against MAD2 had exited mitosis. After 2 h, 84% (47 of 56) of the cells injected with antibodies against MAD2 had exited mitosis, as compared with 1.8% (1 of 56) of cells injected with non-immune IgG. These results clearly show that the mitotic delay induced by the loss of CENP-I is dependent on MAD2, even though MAD2 is not detectable at kinetochores.

The transient mitotic delay seen in CENP-I-depleted cells suggests that the checkpoint is functional for a short period of time. The inability to maintain a prolonged arrest may be due to the fact that the few unattached kinetochores in the CENP-I-depleted cells cannot generate sufficient amounts of wait for anaphase signal to arrest cells in mitosis (>14 h for HeLa cells). We therefore examined whether CENP-I-depleted cells would arrest in mitosis if the number of unattached kinetochores were increased. Cells transfected with CENP-I siRNA were exposed to the microtubule inhibitor nocodazole to disrupt the spindle and thereby to increase the number of unattached kinetochores. The mitotic index continued to climb in nocodazole-treated cells over a period of 8 h, indicating that nocodazole can arrest cells depleted of CENP-I in mitosis for as long as it can arrest normal cells.

Analysis of MAD2 in nocodazole-treated cells showed that its quantity at kinetochores depleted of CENP-I was much less than that at normal kinetochores (Fig. 4a). Quantitative analysis (Fig. 4b) showed that the average integrated fluorescence intensity of MAD2 staining at kinetochores lacking CENP-I was more than 15 times lower than in control cells (23.3 22.8, n = 22, versus 364.8 136.2, n = 20). The wide variance in MAD2 signal intensity was seen in both control and CENP-I-depleted cells, and this phenomenon has been documented in PtK cells that have been treated with taxol or nocodazole17, 18. The reasons for this variability in MAD2 are not understood but may be related to its rapid rate of turnover at kinetochores19.

Cells lacking CENP-I functions show a checkpoint defect that differs from that in cells lacking bona fide checkpoint proteins. CENP-I-depleted cells transiently delay in mitosis but eventually exit in the presence of unaligned chromosomes. By contrast, disruption of the checkpoint proteins BUB1, BUBR1 and MAD2 causes cells to accelerate out of mitosis before chromosomes have time to align properly14, 18, 20, 21. The mitotic delay in CENP-I-depleted cells provides time for bipolar attached chromosomes to align so that when cells exit mitosis they segregate properly, whereas the monopolar chromosomes undergo non-disjunction. This explains why CENP-I-depleted cells were rarely found with chromatin bridges or lagging chromosomes between the dividing cells — a feature that is commonly seen when checkpoint-defective cells are accelerated out of mitosis. In this regard, the phenotype of CENP-I-depleted cells is similar to those described for mis6 mutants in S. pombe7.

The ability of cells to delay transiently in mitosis despite the mislocalization of MAD1 and MAD2 suggests that other proteins at the kinetochore must generate the wait for anaphase signal. But the amount of signal generated from these unattached kinetochores is insufficient to maintain a prolonged mitotic arrest. This situation differs from kinetochores depleted of MAD2 after taxol treatment, because taxol arrests cells in mitosis for a prolonged period18. Thus, CENP-I may be crucial for proper functions of checkpoint proteins such as BUB1, BUBR1, ZW10 and ROD. In its absence, no single unattached kinetochore can generate sufficient amount of wait for anaphase signal to sustain a prolonged mitotic arrest; however, the combined signal generated from many unattached kinetochores can be enough to arrest cells in mitosis.

Our results differ from those reported for chicken CENP-I, for which conditional knockout DT40 cells were found to show severe defects in chromosome alignment and spindle formation6. In addition, CENP-I-depleted DT40 cells have been reported to arrest in mitosis as opposed to showing the defective checkpoint seen in HeLa cells. These differences might be due to the fact that the chicken cells were examined at least four generations after inducible CENP-I expression was turned off. By that time the mutant phenotypes could be the culmination of other defects that occurred early on. Indeed, in examples where the chicken cells were examined early, such as 24 h after turning off CENP-I expression, mitotic cells with a few monopolar chromosomes6 were observed. In addition, at these early time points anaphase cells with lagging chromosomes indicative of a checkpoint defect could be seen. When these observations are taken into account, they would resolve the apparent discrepancy in how chicken and human cells respond to the loss of CENP-I.

Methods
DNA constructs and transfection
We amplified full-length human LRPR1 cDNA using the Marathon-ready HeLa cell cDNA library (BD-Clontech, Palo Alto, CA) with primers designed on the basis of a sequence deposited in GenBank (accession number X97249). The 5' and 3' primers were GACAGATCTATGTCACCTCAAAAGA-GAGTTAAGAAC and GACGTCGACTTAA-TATTGATTGTTGCAGTTTATGCCC, respectively. The underlined restriction sites were inserted at the 5' ends of each primer to facilitate cloning of the polymerase chain reaction (PCR) product into the Gateway pENTR3C vector (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). We determined the DNA sequence of the PCR product with an automated sequencer (Fox Chase Cancer Center DNA Sequencing Facility). This sequence differed from the originally used GenBank sequence (accession number X97249) at several sites but was consistent with numerous expressed sequence tag clones and another sequence in GenBank (accession number XM_010071). The LRPR1 cDNA in pENTR3C was transferred into a custom-made pDEST:gfp vector in a recombination reaction in accordance with the manufacturer's instructions (Invitrogen). We transfected DNA into HeLa cells using Fugene 6 (Roche, Indianapolis, IN).

RNAi
RNAi in mammalian cells was based on the original description of siRNA22, except that we used 3.5-cm dishes for transfection. CENP-I siRNA, 5'-AAGCAACTCGAAGAACATCTC-3' (top strand), which was located 108 nucleotides downstream from the initiation codon, was synthesized commercially (Dharmacon, Lafayette, CO). We transfected annealed double-stranded RNA into HeLa cells using Transit-TKO (Mirus, Madison, WI) at a final concentration of 100 nM. Cells were split 1:4 24 h after transfection and were allowed to grow at least 3 more days before significant reduction in CENP-I staining was detected. By assaying for the loss of CENP-I staining, we estimated that our transfection efficiency was between 50 and 90%.

Antibodies
The DNA fragment encoding the amino-terminal 249 amino acids of CENP-I was cloned into pENTR3C and then recombined into the glutathione S-transferase (GST) destination vector pDEST15 (Invitrogen). We expressed the GST fusion protein in Escherichia coli BL21(DE3) Codon Plus RIL (Invitrogen) after induction with 1 mM isopropylgalactose (IPTG) at 37 °C for 3 h. The insoluble fusion protein was recovered from SDS–PAGE gels, minced and used to immunize rats or rabbits. The antiserum was affinity-purified using an Affigel-10 column crosslinked with (His)6-tagged CENP-I1–249. We concentrated the affinity-purified antibodies for use in microinjections to 3.4 mg ml-1 in calcium- and magnesium-free PBS and stored them at -80 °C.

We generated a mouse polyclonal antibody specific for human MAD1 against amino acids 331–718 of the human protein23. We used mouse monoclonal antibodies against -tubulin (Sigma, St Louis,MO), CENP-A24, hBUB1 and hBUBR1 (generated by the Hybridoma Facility at the Fox Chase Cancer Center). Rabbit polyclonal antibodies against CENP-F25, hROD26, hZW10 (ref. 26), CENP-E12, MAD2 (a gift from E. D. Salmon, Univ. of North Carolina) have all been described. Human anti-centromere autoimmune (ACA) serum was a gift from B. R. Brinkley (Baylor College of Medicine).

Cell culture, synchronization and microinjection
HeLa cells were cultured in DMEM medium supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum in a humidified 37 °C incubator with 5% CO2. Cells were synchronized using a double thymidine block. Cells were treated with 2 mM thymidine for 15 h, washed and released into fresh medium for 9 h, and then treated with thymidine for another 15 h to obtain cells uniformly blocked at the G1/S boundary.

We injected 2–4 mg ml-1 CENP-I antibodies into the nuclei of HeLa cells with an Eppendorf semi-automated microinjector (Model 5242; Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) attached to a Nikon Eclipse TE300 inverted microscope. The cells were plated on No. 1.5 coverslips (22 22 mm) and the injection area was scribed with a diamond pen. Between 250 and 300 cells were injected before release from the double thymidine block. In one experiment, we injected antibodies against CENP-I with dextran-conjugated Texas Red at the G1/S boundary (before release from thymidine block) and injected antibodies against MAD2 with dextran-conjugated Oregon Green at 12 h after release. These cells were examined live for exit from mitosis.

Immunodetection
For immunofluorescence staining, cells grown on the coverslips were fixed in 3.5% paraformaldehyde freshly prepared in PBS for 7 min, extracted in KB (10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and 0.1% bovine serum albumin) plus 0.2% Triton X-100 for 5 min, and washed in KB for at least 5 min. Primary antibodies diluted in KB (1 g ml-1 final concentration) were applied to the coverslips and allowed to incubate for 30 min before being washed in KB for 5 min. Secondary antibodies against mouse, rabbit, rat and human conjugated to Cy5, Texas Red or AlexaFluor 488 were usually diluted 1:1,000. We stained DNA with 50 ng ml-1 4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) for 5 min.

Images were viewed with a 60 Plan Apo objective and were captured using a charge-coupled device camera (Photometrics, Tucson, AZ) mounted on a Nikon Eclipse TE300 inverted microscope equipped with epifluorescence optics. Some images were taken on a Nikon Microphot-SA using a 100 NeoFluor objective. Optical sections along the z axis were acquired using a Bio-point motor (Ludl, NY). We captured and processed images with ImagePro Plus software (Media Cybernetics, MD) together with AdobePhotoshop. Quantification of kinetochore fluorescence intensity was done as described17, except that we used 7 7 and 9 9 square pixels corresponding to areas of 0.94 0.94 m and 1.2 1.2 m, respectively.
