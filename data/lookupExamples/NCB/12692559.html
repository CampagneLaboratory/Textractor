Degradation of DIAP1 by the N-end rule pathway is essential for regulating apoptosis

Mark Ditzel1, 3, Rebecca Wilson1, 3, Tencho Tenev1, Anna Zachariou1, Angela Paul2, Emma Deas1 & Pascal Meier1

1 The Breakthrough Toby Robins Breast Cancer Research Centre, Institute of Cancer Research, Mary-Jean Mitchell Green Building, Chester Beatty Laboratories, Fulham Road, London SW3 6JB, UK

2 Cell and Molecular Biology, Institute of Cancer Research, Chester Beatty Laboratories, Fulham Road, London SW3 6JB, UK

3 These authors contributed equally to this work

Correspondence should be addressed to Pascal Meier pmeier@icr.ac.uk
Some members of the inhibitor of apoptosis (IAP) protein family block apoptosis by binding to and neutralizing active caspases. We recently demonstrated that a physical association between IAP and caspases alone is insufficient to regulate caspases in vivo and that an additional level of control is provided by IAP-mediated ubiquitination of both itself and the associated caspases1. Here we show that Drosophila IAP 1 (DIAP1) is degraded by the 'N-end rule' pathway and that this process is indispensable for regulating apoptosis. Caspase-mediated cleavage of DIAP1 at position 20 converts the more stable pro-N-degron of DIAP1 into the highly unstable, Asn-bearing, DIAP1 N-degron of the N-end rule degradation pathway. Thus, DIAP1 represents the first known metazoan substrate of the N-end rule pathway that is targeted for degradation through its amino-terminal Asn residue. We demonstrate that the N-end rule pathway is required for regulation of apoptosis induced by Reaper and Hid expression in the Drosophila melanogaster eye. Our data suggest that DIAP1 instability, mediated through caspase activity and subsequent exposure of the N-end rule pathway, is essential for suppression of apoptosis. We suggest that DIAP1 safeguards cell viability through the coordinated mutual destruction of itself and associated active caspases.
In this study, we identify a new mechanism through which DIAP1 protein levels are regulated and elucidate its role in apoptosis. It has been shown that loss of DIAP1 function by mutation or deletion instigates spontaneous and unrestrained caspase-mediated cell death2, 3, 4, 5. Because DIAP1 is essential for cell viability, the regulation of its protein level is key to cell survival. DIAP1 protein has an in vivo half-life of approximately 30 min1, 6, allowing efficient depletion through double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) interference (RNAi) strategies1, 7. Unexpectedly, however, we found that depletion of DIAP1 levels by RNAi requires active caspases. Although diap1 dsRNA treatment reduced DIAP1 protein levels, the presence of caspase inhibitors blocked this reduction (Fig. 1a). DIAP1 protein levels were unaffected and remained high in cells treated with z-VAD-FMK, a cell permeable pan-caspase inhibitor. Similarly, expression of the baculovirus, pan-caspase inhibitor p35 blocked diap1 RNAi-mediated depletion of DIAP1. In control RNAi experiments, caspase inhibitors did not affect the efficiency of RNAi in general (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1). Thus, RNAi-induced depletion of DIAP1 seems to be caspase-dependent.

Consistently, we identified a caspase consensus cleavage site (DQVD) at position 20 in the N-terminal portion of the DIAP1 protein sequence (Fig. 1b). In agreement with a model in which DIAP1 is N-terminally processed, two distinct endogenous DIAP1 proteins with different mobilities were detected: full-length DIAP1 and a slightly smaller, faster-migrating form that reflects a smaller DIAP1 protein (Fig. 1c). This smaller form was markedly stabilized by treatment with MG132, a cell-permeable proteasome inhibitor (Fig. 1c, top, compare lanes 3 and 4). Interestingly, MG132 treatment preferentially stabilized the smaller form of DIAP1, whereas full-length DIAP1 remained largely unaffected (Fig. 1c, top, compare lanes 2 and 4). In cells treated with z-VAD-FMK or expressing p35, only full-length DIAP1 was evident. In contrast, induction of apoptosis through expression of Reaper (Rpr) or N-Dronc, exposure to cycloheximide or ultraviolet (UV) radiation promoted the appearance of the smaller DIAP1 form (Fig. 1c). Under the same apoptotic conditions, z-VAD-FMK treatment restored the appearance of full-length DIAP1. Immunoblot analysis using an anti-DIAP1 antibody raised against the first 20 amino acids of DIAP1 selectively detected only full-length DIAP1 (Fig. 1c, middle), suggesting that the smaller form of DIAP1 lacked this N terminus. Thus, DIAP1 seems to be processed, generating a slightly smaller, truncated form of DIAP1 through the action of a z-VAD-FMK- and p35-sensitive caspase.

To determine the cleavage site of DIAP1, we expressed and purified full-length DIAP1 protein from Drosophila S2 cells using the tandem affinity purification tag (TAP) system8. Protein sequencing identified the N-terminal sequence (21)NNTNA (data not shown), which corresponds to DIAP1 lacking the N-terminal 20 amino acids. Thus, proteolytic cleavage at the caspase consensus cleavage site DQVD(20) generates Asn-DIAP1(21–438) (Fig. 1b). In agreement with N-terminal cleavage of DIAP1, we found that ectopic expression of the truncation mutant Asn-DIAP1(21–438), which lacks the N-terminal 20 amino acids, comigrated with the smaller form of DIAP1 (Fig. 1d, compare lanes 1 and 3). Asn-DIAP1(21–438) was expressed using the ubiquitin (Ub) fusion technique9, in which a Ub reporter is fused to the test protein. Ub-specific proteases (UBPs) cotranslationally cleave this fusion protein at the carboxyl terminus of the Ub moiety10, 11, yielding the test protein Asn-DIAP1(21–438). However, the caspase cleavage site mutant DIAP1DQVA, in which the aspartate residue at the P1 position of the caspase cleavage site was changed to alanine (DQVD to DQVA) comigrated with full-length DIAP1. DIAP1DQVA was completely resistant to proteolytic cleavage during Rpr- or diap1 RNAi-mediated apoptosis (Fig. 1e, f). Under the same conditions, wild-type DIAP1 was readily cleaved by caspases, resulting in the generation of DIAP1(21–438). Taken together, our results indicate that active caspases cleave DIAP1 at position 20 of its N terminus.

Expression of the pan-caspase inhibitor p35 completely blocked N-terminal cleavage of DIAP1, indicating that DIAP1 is processed by a p35-sensitive caspase (Fig. 1c and data not shown). DIAP1 binds to and inhibits the Drosophila caspases Dronc12, 13, 14, Dcp-1 (ref. 15) and drICE16. As Dronc is resistant to p35-mediated inhibition13, 14, it is unlikely that Dronc mediates cleavage of DIAP1 directly. Therefore, we examined whether Dcp-1 and drICE cleaved DIAP1. To this end, V5/His-tagged Dcp-1 and drICE were expressed in 293 cells and their active forms were affinity purified from apoptotic cells. DIAP1 cleavage was assayed in vitro using purified DIAP1 protein and recombinant human PARP protein, a bona fide caspase substrate, was used as a control. We found that both DIAP1 and PARP are processed by Dcp-1 and drICE (Fig. 2a). In contrast, pre-incubation of active Dcp-1 and drICE with z-VAD-FMK completely abolished cleavage of DIAP1. Thus, the effector caspases Dcp-1 and drICE can cleave DIAP1 in vitro. Next, we used RNAi to examine whether Dcp-1 and drICE cleave DIAP1 in vivo. In the absence of dsRNA treatment, endogenous DIAP1 was preferentially detected in its smaller form (Fig. 2b, lanes 1, 3 and 5). However, treatment with drice and dcp-1 dsRNA significantly suppressed N-terminal cleavage and restored the appearance of DIAP1(1–438) (Fig. 2b; compare lane 1 with lane 2, and lane 3 with lane 4). RNAi-mediated silencing of drICE and Dcp-1 did not completely abrogate DIAP1 cleavage, presumably because dsRNA treatment was not fully effective in inhibiting drICE and Dcp-1 expression. Recent data indicate that even in the absence of an apoptotic signal, a constitutive positive input from the Drosophila Apaf-1 orthologue Dark provides a 'basal', low level of 'leaky' caspase activity2. To test whether Dark and Dronc promote 'basal' levels of caspase activity that result in N-terminal DIAP1 cleavage, we used RNAi to silence Dark and Dronc (Fig. 2c). In cells treated with dark or dronc dsRNA, N-terminal cleavage of DIAP1 was significantly suppressed when compared with untreated controls (Fig. 2c; compare lane 1 with lane 2, and lane 3 with lane 4), indicating that Dark and Dronc indeed contribute to basal levels of caspase activation.

Site-specific cleavage of DIAP1 by caspases generates a protein with an N-terminal Asn (Asn-DIAP1(21–438)), a destabilizing residue in the N-end rule pathway17, 18. In this pathway, the in vivo half-life of a protein is determined by its N-degron, consisting of its exposed N-terminal residue and internal Lys residues that function as the site of Ub attachment. In the mammalian N-end rule pathway, Asn is a tertiary destabilizing residue that is converted by N-terminal amidohydrolase (Nt-amidase, NTAN1) into the secondary destabilizing residue Asp17, 18. Consequently, Asp is conjugated to the primary destabilizing residue, Arg, a step that is catalysed by Arg-RNA protein transferase (Ate1). Such Arg-conjugated proteins are then recognized and ubiquitinated by the N-end rule specific UBR1 E3 Ub protein ligase. To examine whether endogenous DIAP1(21–438) is degraded by the N-end rule pathway, we used RNAi to silence Drosophila Ate1 expression and inhibit N-end-rule-mediated degradation. Loss of Ate1 through treatment with ate1 dsRNA markedly enhanced the levels of DIAP1(21–438) protein (Fig. 3a). Importantly, N-terminally truncated, but not full-length, DIAP1 strongly accumulated in cells treated with ate1 dsRNA, implying that N-terminal cleavage of DIAP1 is a determinant of DIAP1 protein stability. Consistent with the observation that DIAP1(21–438) is degraded by the N-end rule pathway, steady-state levels of Asn-DIAP1(21–438) were barely detectable when overexpressed in tissue culture cells using the Ub fusion technique (Fig. 3b). In parallel experiments, DIAP1(21–438) mutants where Asn was replaced with Gly (Gly-DIAP1(21–438); Fig. 3b lanes 1, 2) or Met (data not shown) — two stabilizing residues in the N-end rule17, 18 — were abundantly expressed and readily detectable. Treatment with lactacystin, an irreversible proteasome inhibitor19, markedly enhanced the levels of Asn-DIAP1(21–438), but had little effect on Gly-DIAP1(21–438) protein levels. This suggests that Asn-DIAP1(21–438) is degraded by the 26S proteasome through the N-end rule pathway. Experiments to establish the protein half-life of Asn-DIAP1(21–438) through pulse–chase analysis are ongoing. However, the extremely rapid degradation of N-end rule substrates may hamper this, as the majority (>90%) of labelled N-end rule substrate may be destroyed during the pulse20. As degradation of DIAP1 is dependent on its RING finger1, 6, 21, we examined the contribution of the N-end rule pathway to degradation of DIAP1. Significantly, we found that Asn-DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm RING finger mutants were barely detectable in the absence of lactacystin, but rapidly accumulated after proteasome inhibition (Fig. 3c; compare lane 1 with lane 2). In contrast, a DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm mutant carrying the stabilizing residue Met at the first position (Met-DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm) was abundantly expressed and readily detectable (Fig. 3c; compare lane 1 with lane 3). Lactacystin treatment failed to increase the expression level of Met-DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm further (Fig. 3c; compare lane 3 with lane 4). Furthermore, comparison of DQVD- (cleavable) and DQVA- (non-cleavable) DIAP-1(1–438)/RINGm RING finger mutant proteins demonstrated that lactacystin stabilized only the cleaved DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm, but not full-length DIAP-1(1–438)/RINGm (Fig. 3d). Therefore, we conclude that Asn-DIAP1(21–438) is targeted for degradation through its Asn-bearing N-degron, rather than through its C-terminal RING finger.

To assess the role of N-terminal DIAP1 cleavage in apoptosis, we tested various DIAP1 mutants for their ability to suppress Rpr-mediated cell death. Non-cleavable DIAP1DQVA mutants failed to rescue Rpr-induced cell death (Fig. 4a). Similarly, DIAP1N21G, a DIAP1 mutant that is cleaved (data not shown) and hence produces a stable Gly-bearing-N-degron, failed to suppress Rpr-induced apoptosis (58% of wild-type levels; data not shown). Remarkably, in parallel experiments Asn-DIAP1(21–438) but not Met-DIAP1(21–438) efficiently alleviated Rpr-mediated killing (Fig. 4a), with Met-DIAP1(21–438) exhibiting only 62% of wild-type degron levels (data not shown). Thus, DIAP1DQVA, DIAP1N21G and Met-DIAP1(21–438), all of which fail to produce an unstable Asn-N-degron, also fail to suppress Rpr-mediated killing. As Rpr induces cell death by binding to DIAP1 (ref. 5), we examined DIAP1 mutant proteins for their ability to bind to Rpr and found that N-terminal cleavage is not required for binding of Rpr (data not shown). Consequently, unlike class I DIAP1 mutations, which abrogate binding of Rpr and fail to suppress Rpr-mediated cell death3, 5, the failure of DIAP1DQVA to regulate apoptosis is not caused by the disruption of DIAP1 binding to Rpr. Surprisingly, DIAP1 instability mediated by the N-end rule pathway is, in fact, essential for the ability of DIAP1 to suppress Rpr-mediated killing.

Proteins with N-terminal Asn-N-degrons require NTAN1 and Ate1 to be degraded17, 18. To examine the role of the N-end rule pathway in regulating apoptosis in vivo, we searched the predicted Drosophila protein database for a homologue of murine NTAN1 (ref. 22). A standard protein–protein BLAST search using the protein sequence of mouse NTAN1 identified SP2637, the predicted Drosophila NTAN1 homologue (dm-ntan1). dm-ntan1 encodes a 310-amino-acid protein that is 37% identical and 51% similar in amino-acid sequence to mouse NTAN1. Next, using transgenic flies that ectopically express Rpr or Head involution defective (Hid) in the developing eye, we tested whether N-end-rule-mediated degradation is important for regulating apoptosis in vivo. Expression of Rpr and Hid in the developing retina induces excessive cell death, resulting in a rough and reduced eye phenotype that is highly sensitive to DIAP1 protein levels3, 5, 23, 24, 25. If disruption of the N-end rule pathway impairs degradation of DIAP1, we would expect the Rpr and Hid eye phenotypes to be modified. To determine the modifier phenotype of losing a single allele of dm-ntan1 or ate1 on the Rpr and Hid eye phenotypes, heterozygous dm-ntan1 or ate1 flies carrying a deletion in the dm-ntan1 or ate1 locus were crossed to GMR-rpr and GMR-hid flies (Fig. 4b). Intriguingly, mutations affecting the N-end rule pathway were enhancers of rpr-induced cell death in the eye, but suppressors of hid-induced eye ablation. Loss of a single allele of ate1 augments the effect of Rpr so severely that these flies fail to eclose and die trapped in their pupae cases with severely deformed eyes. This enhancement of the effects of Rpr and suppression of the Hid eye phenotype is highly reminiscent of the Rpr- and Hid-modifier phenotype of DIAP1 RING finger mutant flies1. Similarly, mutation of the DIAP1 RING finger enhanced Rpr-induced cell death and readily alleviated Hid-mediated killing. Thus, perturbing DIAP1 degradation, either by RING finger mutation or impairment of the N-end rule pathway, results in a failure of DIAP1 to regulate apoptosis. Taken together, these findings suggest that dm-ntan1 and ate1 mutants fail to regulate apoptosis because DIAP1 degradation is perturbed. In conclusion, our data argue that degradation of DIAP1 mediated by the N-end rule pathway is essential for regulation of Rpr- and Hid-mediated cell death.

In Drosophila, DIAP1 directly inhibits caspases and thus represents the last line of defence against caspase-mediated damage. DIAP1-mediated inhibition of caspases is essential for cell survival, as loss of DIAP1 function instigates spontaneous and unrestrained caspase activation2, 3, 4, 5. Hence, regulation of DIAP1 protein levels is key to cell viability. Currently, little is known about the mechanism through which DIAP1 stability is regulated. We find that DIAP1 degradation requires active caspases. This is evident because the presence of caspase inhibitors blocks RNAi-mediated DIAP1 degradation and depletion. It is noteworthy that caspase inhibitors do not affect RNAi in general (see Supplementary Information, Fig. S1), hence caspase inhibitors blocked RNAi-mediated DIAP1 depletion because DIAP1 cleavage was inhibited. Our results indicate that caspase-mediated cleavage of DIAP1 converts the pro-N-degron of DIAP1 into the highly destabilizing, Asn-bearing N-degron, causing rapid degradation of Asn-DIAP1(21–438) through the N-end rule pathway. Several lines of evidence argue that DIAP1 is an N-end rule substrate. First, in live cells, Asn-DIAP1(21–438) is a highly unstable protein whose degradation is strongly inhibited by lactacystin, which specifically inhibits degradation of ubiquitinated proteins by the 26S proteasome. Importantly, the E3 Ub protein ligase activity of the DIAP1 RING finger is dispensable for Asn-DIAP1(21–438) degradation, as Asn-DIAP1(21–438)/RINGm lacking a functional RING finger is equally unstable. Such an observation is in agreement with the role of the N-end-rule-specific E3 Ub protein ligase UBR1 in recognizing and degrading N-end rule substrates26, 27 independent of the DIAP1 RING finger. Second, mutation of the DIAP1 N-degron through substitution of the destabilizing residue Asn to Met or Gly, two stabilizing residues in the N-end rule, results in enhanced stability of DIAP1 protein, which is refractory to the effects of lactacystin. Third, substrates of the N-end rule pathway are targeted for degradation in a multistep process catalysed by NTAN1, Ate1 and specific E3 enzymes. We find that disruption of the N-end rule pathway through ate1 dsRNA treatment induces marked and selective accumulation of Asn-DIAP1(21–438). In addition, expression levels of full-length DIAP1 do not accumulate in ate1 dsRNA-treated cells. Taken together, these data argue that caspase cleavage exposes an N-degron that targets DIAP1 for degradation by the N-end rule pathway. Surprisingly, DIAP1 apparently represents the first known N-degron-based physiological N-end rule metazoan substrate. Until now, only viral proteins and the yeast protein cohesin were known to be substrates of the N-end rule pathway18, 28, 29, 30.

It is currently thought that degradation of DIAP1 protein lowers the cellular apoptotic threshold. Remarkably, with respect to Rpr-killing, we find that mutations that inhibit N-end-rule-mediated DIAP1 degradation act as loss-of-function mutations that prevent DIAP1 from suppressing apoptosis. This failure, similarly to that of DIAP1 RING finger mutants, is most probably caused by the inability of DIAP1 to regulate caspases. Thus, cells with mutations that increase the stability of DIAP1 are 'sensitized' to undergo cell death through caspase deregulation. Furthermore, in the Drosophila eye, disruption of the N-end rule pathway strongly enhances Rpr-mediated cell death, but suppresses Hid-induced killing. Similarly, disruption of the DIAP1 RING finger, which has previously been shown to increase stability of the DIAP1 protein1, equally enhances Rpr and suppresses Hid-induced eye phenotypes.

In conclusion, our observations suggest that DIAP1 utilizes two independent degradative mechanisms, both of which employ 26S proteosomal degradation, to effect its role in apoptotic regulation. One mechanism relies on the DIAP1 RING finger for ubiquitination and degradation, whereas the other mechanism — the N-end rule — functions independently of this domain but requires active caspases. Overall, DIAP1 instability seems to be essential for DIAP1 function. Our genetic and molecular studies of Drosophila caspases and DIAP1 suggest that the destruction of DIAP1 may actually reflect part of its normal role as an anti-apoptotic molecule, possibly functioning as a 'sink' for unscheduled, basal levels of active caspases. Thus, we suggest that DIAP1 safeguards cell viability through the coordinated destruction of itself and associated caspases31, 32, 33 or IAP antagonists34, 35, 36.

Methods
Generation of expression constructs and antibodies
C-terminal Myc/His-tagged DIAP1DQVA and DIAP1N21G were generated using site-directed mutagenesis with Pfu Turbo polymerase (Stratagene, La Jolla, CA) and cloned into pAc5.1 (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) and pN-21 (ref. 37). Ub–DIAP(21–438) fusion constructs were generated by PCR using Expand High Fidelity (Roche, Basel, Switzerland) and cloned into pN-21 or pAc5.1. C-terminal Flag-tagged drICE, Dcp-1, Ate1 and C-terminal V5-tagged Dark CARD (amino acids 1–412), Dronc, drICE and Dcp-1 were amplified by PCR from a Drosophila oligo-dT-primed yeast two-hybrid library (a gift from S. J. Elledge) or total S2 cDNA, and cloned into pAc5.1 and pMT vectors (Invitrogen). All constructs were verified by DNA sequencing. Anti-DIAP1 raised against the first 20 amino acids is a peptide-derived antibody generated in guinea pigs. Anti-full-length DIAP1 and anti-RING DIAP1 have been described previously1, 38. Anti-V5 (Invitrogen), anti-Flag (M2)–HRP (Sigma, Poole, UK) and anti--tubulin (Sigma) were used according to the manufacturer's instructions.

Cell culture, transfections and apoptosis assay
S2, 293 and Rat1 cells were cultured as described previously13 and transfected with either calcium phosphate (Clontech, Palo Alto, CA) or Effectene (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Apoptosis assays were performed as previously described39. Briefly, 0.20 g of wild-type or mutant diap1 pAc5.1 and 0.30 g of rpr pAc5.1 constructs were cotransfected with 0.03 g pIE1-3--galactosidase (Novagen, Madison, WI) into each well of a 24-well plate of S2 cells using Cellfectin (Invitrogen). At 24 h post-transfection, the cells were fixed and stained for LacZ activity and the number of blue cells were counted as an indicator of cell survival. Twelve independent experiments were carried out and the mean and standard error were calculated. diap1 RNAi was performed as described previously1. The following regions (nucleotides) of the corresponding coding sequences were used to generate RNAi: Dcp-1 (152–937); drICE (84–763); dark (84–811); dronc (1–765); ate1 (10–799). For generation of S2 stable cell lines: pMT-Rpr, -N-Dronc and pAc5.1-p35 (a gift from P. Friesen) were cotransfected (10:1) with pMk33-Hygro (a gift from C. Thummel) and selected in hygromycin. Rpr and Dronc expression was induced with 0.7 mM copper sulphate for 3 h.

Protein extracts, in vitro caspase cleavage assay and N-terminal sequencing of DIAP1
Where necessary, transfected cells were induced for eight hours with copper sulphate, harvested 24 hours after transfection and lysed in Laemlli buffer or as described previously1. For the caspase cleavage assay, DIAP1–TAP was purified from S2 cells and the TAP tag was cleaved by TEV protease (Invitrogen) as described previously38. 293 cells were transfected with constructs encoding C-terminally V5/His-tagged Dcp-1 and drICE and affinity purified using nickel beads according to the manufacturer's instructions (Qiagen). After purification, Dcp-1 and drICE were assayed for their ability to cleave purified DIAP1 or PARP (R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN) in the presence or absence of z-VAD-FMK. Caspase activity of purified Dcp-1 and drICE was measured using DEVD-pNA. The absorbance of the supernatant was assayed at 405 nm. For determination of the actual caspase cleavage site in DIAP1, DIAP1–TAP was expressed in S2 cells and purified using the TAP system, as described previously38. The purified material was resolved by SDS–PAGE and visualized by colloidal Coomassie Blue staining. Protein sequence analysis was performed using a 477A Liquid Phase Sequencer (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA).

Treatments with proteasome inhibitors, cycloheximide and UV
Cells were incubated for up to 6 h in the presence or absence of lactacystin (8 M; Calbiochem, San Diego, CA) and MG132 (50 M; Sigma). For experiments in Rat1 cells, cells were transiently transfected with various diap1 expression constructs. After 24 h of transfection, cells were split into two dishes to avoid variations in transfection efficiency. After further incubation for 18 h, each dish was treated for 6 h with dimethyl sulphoxide (control) or lactacystin. S2 cells were treated with 50 g ml-1 cycloheximide for 3 h. Cells were irradiated with UV, as described previously38.

Fly strains
The following mutant and transgenic fly strains were used for the genetic analysis: GMR-rpr, GMR-hid, Df(2R)017/SM1, Df(2R)PC66/CyO. Df(2R)017/SM1 and Df(2R)PC66/CyO were obtained from the Bloomington Drosophila Stock Center. All genetic crosses were performed at 25 °C.
