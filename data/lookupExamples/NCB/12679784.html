Reconstitution of -secretase activity

Dieter Edbauer1, Edith Winkler1, Joerg T. Regula2, Brigitte Pesold1, 3, Harald Steiner1 & Christian Haass1

1 Adolf-Butenandt-Institute, Department of Biochemistry, Laboratory for Alzheimer's and Parkinson's Disease Research, Ludwig-Maximilians-University, 80336 Munich, Germany.

2 Adolf-Butenandt-Institute, Protein Analysis Unit, Ludwig-Maximilians-University, 80336 Munich, Germany.

3 Present address: Central Institute of Mental Health, Department of Molecular Biology, 68159 Mannheim, Germany.

Correspondence should be addressed to Harald Steiner hsteiner@pbm.med.uni-muenchen.de
-Secretase is a membrane protein complex with an unusual aspartyl protease activity that catalyses the regulated intramembranous cleavage of the -amyloid precursor protein (APP) to release the Alzheimer's disease (AD)-associated amyloid -peptide (A) and the APP intracellular domain (AICD)1. Here we show the reconstitution of -secretase activity in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae, which lacks endogenous -secretase activity. Reconstituted -secretase activity depends on the presence of four complex components including presenilin (PS)1, nicastrin (Nct)2, APH-1 (refs 3, 4, 5, 6) and PEN-2 (refs 4, 7), is associated with endoproteolysis of PS8, and produces A and AICD in vitro. Thus, the biological activity of -secretase is reconstituted by the co-expression of human PS, Nct, APH-1 and PEN-2 in yeast.
Biochemical and genetic studies have provided strong evidence that -secretase activity requires the formation of a complex between PS, which apparently constitutes the active site of -secretase1, and at least three other membrane proteins, Nct2, 9, 10, APH-1 (refs 3, 4, 5, 6) and PEN-2 (refs 4, 7). However, it is currently unclear if the four components are sufficient for -secretase activity. In cells derived from higher eukaryotes, which all contain -secretase activity, this is impossible to prove, as these cells may contain additional so-far unknown -secretase complex components. Therefore, co-expression of the four known -secretase complex components in mammalian cells will not reveal the minimal composition of the biologically active -secretase. To define the minimal set of components that are required for -secretase activity, we sought to co-express PS, Nct, APH-1 and PEN-2 in S. cerevisiae. No homologues of PS, Nct, APH-1 or PEN-2 have been identified in yeast, and indeed we do not detect any endogenous -secretase activity (see below). Although we cannot formally rule out the possibility that yeast may contain endogenous proteins required for this activity, we consider it improbable, given the complete absence of orthologous -secretase complex components from the yeast genome. Therefore, yeast may not contain other -secretase-binding proteins/cofactors of similar functional importance as PS, Nct, APH-1 and PEN-2.

Previously, we have shown that membrane-associated proteolysis can be efficiently monitored in yeast by using a highly sensitive reporter gene assay11. We have now adapted this assay to monitor reconstitution of -secretase and constructed an APP-based type I transmembrane reporter protein, C1–55–GAL4. Cleavage of C1–55–GAL4 after co-expression of functional -secretase is expected to liberate GAL4 from the cytosolic side of the membrane. GAL4 then translocates to the nucleus where it activates transcription of the Escherichia coli LacZ gene, which encodes -galactosidase (-gal). We co-expressed C1–55–GAL4 with PS1wt, Nct, APH-1aL5 tagged at the carboxyl terminus with the haemagglutinin (HA) epitope and PEN-2 tagged at the amino terminus with the Flag epitope. To control for specificity, we also co-expressed the functionally inactive PS1D385A mutant12 with the same set of proteins. -Gal assays identified robust activity after co-expression of PS1wt, Nct, APH-1aL and PEN-2 (Fig. 1a). In contrast, co-expression of the proteolytically inactive PS1D385A mutant with Nct, APH-1aL and PEN-2 did not result in significant -gal activity (Fig. 1a). Co-expression of C1–55–GAL4 with PS1 only or with triple combinations lacking one component also failed to produce significant -gal activity (Fig. 1a). These results demonstrate that reconstitution of -secretase activity is strictly dependent on the co-expression of functionally active PS with Nct, PEN-2 and APH-1. Immunoblotting of membrane fractions confirmed that all four -secretase complex proteins were robustly expressed (Fig. 1b). Previous studies have shown that endoproteolysis of PS is associated with the biological activity of the -secretase complex8, 9. Strikingly, a significant fraction of PS1 was endoproteolysed into its N- and C-terminal fragments (NTF, CTF) when Nct, APH-1 and PEN-2 were co-expressed, but not if one or all of the latter components was missing (Fig. 1b). Consistent with findings in mammalian cells, the non-functional PS1D385A mutant occurred as a holoprotein (Fig. 1b)12. Thus, endoproteolysis of PS is stimulated after co-expression of Nct, APH-1, PEN-2 and functionally active PS.

Having demonstrated the in vivo reconstitution of -secretase activity and its associated PS endoproteolysis, we investigated whether it also formed a -secretase complex. Membrane fractions from yeast cells expressing PS1 together with Nct, APH-1aL and PEN-2 were solubilized with the detergent CHAPSO and subjected to immunoprecipitation with antibodies to the N- or C-terminal fragments of PS1. Both antibodies co-immunoprecipitated Nct, APH-1aL–HA and Flag–PEN-2 (Fig. 2). PS1-NTF and -CTF were co-immunoprecipitated in cells expressing PS1, demonstrating PS heterodimer formation (Fig. 2a). Thus, PS, Nct, APH-1 and PEN-2 undergo complex formation similar to that detected in mammalian cells1. We next asked whether the immunoprecipitated -secretase complex has -secretase activity in vitro. The immunoprecipitated g-secretase complex generated A and AICD from an exogeneously added APP-based C-terminally hexahistidine-tagged -secretase substrate (C100–His6) in a similar manner to the -secretase complex isolated from untransfected HEK293 cells (Fig. 2b). In contrast, no -secretase activity was observed when control immunoprecipitates from untransformed yeast cells were analysed. Importantly, cleavage of C100–His6 was inhibited in the presence of L-685,458, a -secretase inhibitor that is known to interact directly with PS13 (Fig. 2b). Analysis of in-vitro-produced A by MALDI-TOF-MS (matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry) demonstrated that the reconstituted -secretase complex generated heterogeneous peptides with relative molecular masses corresponding to A1-38, A1-40 and A1-42, similar to -secretase isolated from HEK293 cells (Fig. 2c). Generation of these peptides was strongly inhibited in the presence of L-685,458 (data not shown) and none of these peptides were observed in in vitro assays utilizing vector-transformed yeast cells or yeast cells co-expressing the non-functional PS1D385A mutant together with Nct, APH-1aL and PEN-2 (data not shown). Moreover, MALDI-TOF-MS demonstrated that the reconstituted -secretase complex generated AICD species with relative molecular masses corresponding to AICD51 (starting with Leu49, A numbering) and AICD50 (starting with Val50, A numbering), again in a very similar manner to -secretase isolated from HEK293 cells (Fig. 2d)14, 15. These AICD species were not observed in the presence of L-685,458 (data not shown). Thus, the reconstituted -secretase generates heterogenous A and AICD by cleaving at the expected intramembraneous sites1.

Taken together, these results demonstrate that -secretase complex formation and activity is reconstituted in an organism lacking any endogenous -secretase activity/component. Thus, our work provides the first reconstitution of a proteolytically active, intramembrane-cleaving -secretase complex, which is pivotal in signal transduction and Alzheimer's disease.
