Polarized epithelial membrane traffic: conservation and plasticity

Keith Mostov, Tao Su & Martin ter Beest

Department of Anatomy, Genentech Hall, 600 16th Street, University of California, San Francisco, CA 94143-2140, USA

Correspondence should be addressed to Keith Mostov Mostov@itsa.ucsf.edu
Most cells are polarized and have distinct plasma membrane domains, which are the result of polarized trafficking of proteins and lipids. Great progress has been made in elucidating the highly conserved polarized targeting machinery. A pre-eminent challenge now is to understand the plasticity of polarized traffic, how it is altered by differentiation and dedifferentiation during development, as well as the adaptation of differentiated cells to meet changing physiological needs.
Polarity is a property of virtually all eukaryotic cells and as a subject area has moved to the forefront of cell biology. It is intertwined with all other aspects of cell biology, including differentiation, signalling, cytoskeletal organization, migration and division. A dramatic facet of this process is the polarization of the plasma membrane, whose accessibility makes it a window on the underlying polarity of the cell. Membrane and secretory proteins are delivered to specific regions of the plasma membrane, thereby defining and maintaining their unique identities and functions1, 2. This is the result of fundamental molecular mechanisms that are conserved from yeast to humans. Furthermore, these processes are deranged in many important human diseases3. During development, polarized traffic pathways are modified to accommodate the specific needs of individual cell types, as well as organization of cells into tissues and organs; now, the principles of these modifications are emerging.

In this review, we focus on polarized traffic in epithelial cells, the fundamental polarized cell type in metazoa; indeed, epithelial tissue organization is a defining feature of metazoa. Epithelial cells form sheets that line surfaces and often form the interface between the interior and exterior of the organism. The simplest metazoa — hydra, for example — consist essentially of two concentric epithelial cylinders. In more complex animals, most internal organ systems (digestive, respiratory, genito-urinary and vascular) are lined by a monolayer of epithelial cells4. The epithelial plasma membrane is divided into two surfaces, an apical surface facing the lumen and a basolateral surface contacting adjacent cells and the underlying connective tissue. As apical and basolateral surfaces have distinct functions, their protein and lipid compositions are quite different. Apical and basolateral surfaces are separated by cellular junctions, such as tight junctions in vertebrates, which prevent the mixing of proteins and outer-leaflet lipids between the two surfaces. Epithelial polarization can be modified to produce a wide variety of specialized cell types; for instance, photoreceptors have a massive expansion of the apical surface5.

Pathways of polarized traffic
The polarized distribution of apical and basolateral components is the result of three processes (Fig. 1)1, 2. First, newly synthesized proteins are inserted into the rough endoplasmic reticulum and transported through the Golgi to the trans-Golgi network (TGN), where they are sorted into carriers that deliver them to apical or basolateral surfaces6, 7. Some proteins are transported from the TGN to endosomes, and only then to the cell surface8. Second, some proteins are selectively retained at the cell surface, often through an interaction of their carboxyl termini with PDZ-domain-containing proteins; however, this is reviewed elsewhere and is not considered further here9. Third, components that are not retained at the surface are rapidly endocytosed and delivered to early endosomes, from where they can be recycled back to the cell surface, transferred to late endosomes or transported across the cell and delivered to the opposite surface, a process known as transcytosis1. All epithelial cells use biosynthetic sorting from the TGN and selective recycling/transcytosis to transport proteins to the correct surface, although the relative importance of these two pathways varies with cell type. For example, hepatocytes use transcytosis extensively, although some proteins are delivered directly from the TGN to the apical surface10, 11. In MDCK cells, 50% of the cell surface is endocytosed per hour, and a typical membrane protein has a half-life of approximately 20 h. Even in MDCK cells, which are heavily reliant on TGN sorting, flux through the endocytic pathway is approximately ten times greater than through the biosynthetic pathway, indicating that selective recycling/transcytosis is essential for steady-state polarity12.

Transcytosis has been most extensively studied through analysis of the polymeric immunoglobulin receptor (pIgR), which binds its ligand polymeric IgA (pIgA) at the basolateral surface and is then transcytosed to the apical surface13. One widely discussed model is presented in Fig. 1a–c and summarized below (also reviewed in ref. 13). In this model, pIgR and other receptors are endocytosed at the basolateral surface. Endocytosed molecules pass through a series of endosomal compartments: basolateral endosomes, common endosomes and probably apical recycling endosomes (although see ref. 14 for a somewhat different view). These compartments sort some molecules (for example, transferrin) back to the basolateral surface, whereas others (for example, pIgR and pIgA) are transcytosed to the apical surface. Hepatocytes have features of common endosomes (that is, a subapical compartment) and apical recycling endosomes15, 16. During transcytosis, viral-specific pIgA can neutralize viruses within the epithelial cell, apparently within the apical recycling endosome17. Proteins can also be transcytosed in the plane of the epithelial monolayer, thereby moving morphogens for planar polarity18.

Polarized sorting
Basolateral plasma membrane proteins are directed to the appropriate surface by short sequences in their cytoplasmic domains1. Deletions in the cytoplasmic domain result in mis-sorting of proteins to the apical surface, suggesting that apical targeting occurs by default or through recognition of a cryptic signal19. Crucially, basolateral sorting signals in the cytoplasmic domain can be transplanted to heterologous reporter molecules and redirect them to the basolateral surface20. Interestingly, the low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) contains two basolateral sorting signals; inactivation of one signal can cause mistargeting of LDLR and result in hypercholesterolemia21, 22. Mutation of the basolateral signal in the anion exchanger AE1 causes mis-targeting to apical membranes, resulting in distal renal tubular acidosis23. Basolateral signals are recognized by adaptor coat proteins. These include AP-1B, a protein that is related to the AP-1 clathrin adaptor protein, but which contains an alternative, epithelial-specific subunit24. Expression of AP-1B is sufficient to confer correct basolateral sorting in cells that otherwise lack AP-1B. It has been proposed that AP-1B functions either at the TGN or in endosomal sorting25, 26. Given that some basolateral sorting signals seem to work in both pathways, we suggest that AP-1B may work in both pathways27, 28. Another adaptor, AP-4, is also involved in basolateral sorting29.

In contrast to basolateral sorting signals, apical sorting signals and machinery are less well understood. Some apical targeting is mediated by lipid rafts30, 31. Some proteins enter rafts, either because their transmembrane segments partition into rafts or because the proteins are anchored to the external leaflet by glycosylphosphatidyl inositol lipid anchors, which associate with rafts. Lipid rafts originate in the Golgi and vesicles enriched in these components are then sent to the apical surface. Some traffic to the apical surface occurs independently of rafts, and conversely, not all raft-associated molecules are sent via an apical route6, 31, 32. Other signals for apical targeting include glycosylation, as well as signals in the cytoplasmic and luminal domains of the polypeptide2, 33. Apparently, apical traffic also involves two other proteins, Annexin 13b34 and MAL1/MAL2 (refs 35, 36).

Polarized vesicle targeting and fusion
After exit from the TGN, vesicles move in a process that is dependent on microtubule motors37, 38, 39. In polarized MDCK cells, basolaterally targeted vesicles are found in the apical two-thirds of the cytoplasm, whereas apically targeted vesicles are found in a narrow band just beneath the apical surface. These restricted distributions are dependent on microtubules7. In addition, the Rho family GTPases and actin are also involved in polarized trafficking, especially in the transcytotic pathway37.

In yeast, exocytic vesicles are targeted to their specific fusion site by the multi-subunit exocyst complex40, 41. Mammals also have an exocyst homologue (the Sec6/8 complex)42. Antibodies against the exocyst blocks delivery of proteins to basolateral, but not the apical, surfaces of MDCK cells43. As epithelial cells polarize, the exocyst is recruited from the cytoplasm to the lateral surface of the cells. It was also reported that the exocyst is localized just below the tight junction, suggesting that the exocyst directed delivery of basolateral vesicles to precisely this location, rather than to the entire basolateral surface43. A prominent achievement was the recent observation of traffic in live cells, showing that basolaterally targeted vesicles apparently fuse with the upper two-thirds of the lateral surface, not just the region immediately beneath the tight junction7. The exocyst is also involved in vesicular delivery in other polarized cell types, such as neurons44, 45.

Similarly to other intracellular fusion events, fusion at apical and basolateral surfaces involves SNAREs. In many epithelial cell types, the t-SNARE syntaxin 3 is localized exclusively to apical surfaces, whereas syntaxin 4 is only found at basolateral surfaces46, 47. Syntaxin 3 is required for transport between the TGN and the apical surface, whereas syntaxin 4 is involved in transport between the TGN and the basolateral surface48, 49. These data suggest that syntaxins 3 and 4 may be involved in specifying vesicle delivery to the correct surface. Consistently, when microtubules are depolymerized, syntaxin 3 becomes mislocalized to the basal surface and vesicles that would normally be delivered to the apical surface are misdirected to the basal surface in a syntaxin-3-dependent manner7. However, we must also be aware that the mechanism for specifying delivery of vesicles to the correct target has not been unequivocally demonstrated for any intracellular trafficking event.

The v-SNAREs for polarized traffic have not been completely defined; VAMP7/TI-VAMP is involved in syntaxin 3-mediated delivery to apical membranes50. Retinal pigment epithelial cells are unusual in that they lack syntaxin 3 altogether, although the apical and basolateral surfaces of these cells are partially inverted relative to conventional epithelial cells and thus they may not have a true apical surface51.

Plasticity of traffic: physiological regulation
As the apical surface is usually the interface between the organism and the outside world, its composition must be adjusted to meet changing physiological needs. Often this occurs by regulated endocytosis and recycling of transporters and other molecules at the apical surface. In a dramatic example of this phenomenon, the surface area of the urinary bladder can be increased several-fold by stretch-induced exocytosis of intracellular membrane stores52.

As mentioned above, the most extensively studied pathway for regulated apical delivery is transcytosis of pIgR (Fig. 2)1, 13. In the absence of pIgA, pIgR is largely recycled to the basolateral surface. However, binding of pIgA stimulates transcytosis approximately threefold53. Stimulation involves two pathways: first, binding of pIgA results in activation of the Src family non-receptor tyrosine kinase, p62Yes, which in turn causes elevation of intracellular free calcium and thereby stimulates transcytosis54 (reviewed in ref. 1); second, pIgR associates directly with the low-molecular-weight GTPase, rab3b55, which normally exists in an active GTP-bound state. This promotes recycling of the pIgR to the basolateral surface. Binding of pIgA stimulates GTP hydrolysis on rab3b and dissociation of rab3b from pIgR, resulting in transcytosis.

Figure 2. Regulation of pIgR transcytosis.

Binding of pIgA to the pIgR causes dimerization of the pIgR and activation of p62Yes, probably at the basolateral cell surface (blue) or in BEE. In turn, p62Yes activates phospholipase C (PLC-), which produces diacylglycerol (DAG) and inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (InsP3), although it is not clear where this occurs. Finally, protein kinase C (PKC) is activated and levels of free calcium increase, both of which promote delivery from the ARE to the apical surface. Rab3b-GTP binds to pIgR, but when pIgA binds, Rab3b-GTP is converted to Rab3-GDP. This induces dissociation from the pIgR and promotes transcytosis. After the pIgR reaches the apical surface, its extracellular ligand-binding domain is proteolytically cleaved and released with pIgA into secretions.

Plasticity of polarized traffic: development
Many developmental processes are inextricably linked to alterations in polarized membrane traffic56. Understanding these linkages is crucial to developmental biology and is a major challenge for the future. In Saccharomyces cerevisiae, the bud scar functions as a cue to initiate and orientate polarity. The bud scar recruits the exocyst complex, which in turn recruits vesicles and organizes the cytoskeleton. This model has been adapted to epithelial cells, where cell–cell interactions have been proposed to be the cue for polarization57. This interaction is mediated by the adhesion proteins E-cadherin and nectin58 and has been suggested to create a 'targeting patch' for vesicles, probably by recruiting the exocyst (Fig. 3).

We speculate that they recruit the exocyst (yellow) from the cytoplasm to the lateral membrane. d, The lateral surface then expands and tight junction components become concentrated at the apical-most portion of the lateral surface. The cell becomes fully polarized along an apicobasal axis (grey arrow). Polarization of epithelia depends on three protein complexes — The Par3–Par6–aPKC complex, the Crumbs (Crb)–Stardust (Sdt) complex and the Discs large (Dlg)–Scribble (Scrib) complex — which are conserved throughout metazoa and function in a genetic hierarchy59, 60. This burgeoning field is reviewed elsewhere61 and so we mention only a few aspects here. The Par3–Par6–aPKC complex initially functions to define the apical surface and polarization of the cell, and is dependent on cell–cell adhesion. Notably, Par3 binds to both the tight junction molecule JAM and to nectin62, 63. Downstream of this complex, the Crb–Sdt and Dlg–Scrib complexes (and an associated protein, Lethal giant larvae (Lgl)) compete to promote formation of apical and basolateral surfaces, respectively. Although these complexes have been defined through elegant genetic analyses, we lack a comprehensive molecular understanding of how they control polarization. However, we have a few hints that these proteins work in part through polarized membrane traffic. For example, the yeast homologue of Lgl interacts with SNAREs at the plasma membrane64, mammalian Lgl interacts with syntaxin 4 at the basolateral surface and apparently regulates basolateral exocytosis65, and loss of Crb activity causes accumulation of transport vesicles beneath the apical surface66. Crb interacts with H-spectrin and moesin, two components of the cortical cytoskeleton, at the apical surface and may control the cytoskeleton underneath the apical membrane67.

A basic principle of polarization is that the polarization of a cell and the orientation of that polarization are distinct68, 69. Although cell–cell adhesion and the three complexes mentioned above are required for polarization, they apparently do not specify the orientation of the apicobasal axis. Orientation of this axis requires an additional interaction, commonly with the extracellular matrix (ECM)68. The importance of apicobasal axis orientation has been largely overlooked because cells are usually cultured on artificial supports, for example, coverslips or filters. The support provides an overriding cue to orientate the cell with the basal surface facing the support and the apical surface facing the overlying medium. Similarly, most of the work in Drosophila melanogaster defining the roles of the three complexes in polarization has been performed on the epithelium covering the embryo. Here, the free surface facing the outside of the embryo and the adhesive surface facing the underlying organism may provide strong cues for orientation. In contrast, when epithelial cells are grown in an isotropic three-dimensional gel of ECM, they must break symmetry to orientate the apicobasal axis (Fig. 3)4. In this environment, cells proliferate and form a hollow sphere or cyst lined by a monolayer of cells, with their apical surfaces facing the lumen of the cyst. In this simple model of organogenesis, the lateral and basal surfaces are determined by interactions with other cells and the ECM, respectively. Interaction of cells with the ECM, especially laminin, has been shown to determine the orientation of apicobasal axis68. We speculate that laminin interacts with a receptor, probably an integrin, which sends a signal across the cell to create an apical surface and lumen at the side of the cell opposite from where the laminin is assembled. This signalling mechanism may involve Cdc42, which interacts with the Par3–Par6–aPKC complex61, 70.

Changes in membrane traffic during polarization and depolarization
During development, epithelial cells must often rearrange themselves into new multicellular structures. For instance, epithelial cells often form branching tubes, such as lung and mammary glands71, 72. Treatment of cysts cultured in ECM gels with growth factors, especially hepatocyte growth factor (HGF), causes them to elaborate branching tubules that resemble in vivo tubes73. During tubulogenesis, cells transiently lose epithelial polarization and partially take on the polarization of a migrating fibroblast, a process that we refer to as partial epithelial–mesenchymal transition (PEMT)74, 75. Much of the morphogenesis of epithelial organs involves sequential PEMT, followed by repolarization and re-establishment of the epithelial phenotype4.

Polarization and depolarization involves profound changes to almost all cellular processes, including membrane traffic. In fact, during PEMT, the apical surface disappears74. In polarized epithelial cells, the small GTPase ARF6 regulates clathrin-mediated endocytosis at the apical surface, which may function to regulate physiological changes in apical membrane composition, as described above76. A higher degree of ARF6 activation may result in endocytosis and disappearance of the apical surface, as well as some junctions, which is necessary for PEMT77. Interestingly, in non-polarized cells, ARF6 seems to have a different function, regulating a novel non-clathrin-mediated endocytosis and recycling pathway78. This is but one of several examples where components of the membrane traffic machinery have different functions in polarized epithelial cells versus non-polarized cells and emphasizes the importance of studying trafficking components in multiple cell types29, 79, 80.

During PEMT, cells develop intracellular compartments containing components that are found at the surface in polarized cells81, 82, 83, 84. Polarization often involves the rapid redeployment of membrane from intracellular reservoirs into new configurations56, 85. At least in the Fischer rat thyroid (FRT) cell line, polarized sorting occurs through the transcytotic pathway during early polarization, with sorting in the TGN becoming significant only later; this stresses the importance of understanding the pathway and regulation of transcytosis (Figs 1, 2)16, 86.

We are beginning to understand the molecular mechanisms by which membrane traffic is redirected during development. In budding yeast, vesicles are targeted to the tip of the early bud. When the bud is full-grown, exocytic vesicles are instead targeted to the bud mother junction to complete cytokinesis. Targeting to different sites is mediated by the exocyst40, 41 through interactions with the actin cytoskeleton. In yeast, these interactions are under the control of Sec4, a member of the rab family, as well as three Rho family members (Rho1, Rho3 and Cdc42), which are master regulators of the actin cytoskeleton and many other processes41, 87, 88. In metazoa, the exocyst does not seem to interact directly with Rac1, RhoA or Cdc42, although an indirect interaction89 or an interaction through one of the less well-characterized Rho family members may occur. Cdc42 does, however, regulate polarized budding of vesicles from the TGN and it will be interesting to determine if the exocyst is somehow involved90, 91. The mammalian exocyst does interact with the Ral GTPase, which is involved in epithelial membrane traffic, as well as the actin cytoskeleton and traffic in neurons41, 88, 92. In addition, Ral may be regulated by Cdc42. Interestingly, in mammalian neurons, the exocyst interacts with microtubules, in contrast to the actin cytoskeleton in yeast93.

The exocyst is important for coordinating traffic with the cytoskeleton and other cellular processes during epithelial polarization and depolarization, and is recruited to the upper two-thirds of the lateral surface during polarization. Conversely, the exocyst is localized to the cytoplasm in non-polarized cells grown on coverslips and during PEMT accompanying tubulogenesis (Fig. 4)7, 94. Accordingly, in non-polarized cells, basolaterally targeted vesicles fuse with the entire basal surface, presumably in an exocyst-independent manner. As the cells polarize, the fusion of basolaterally targeted vesicles at the basal surface ceases. Apparently, fusion of these vesicles becomes confined to the apical two-thirds of the lateral surface, although it is difficult to rigorously prove where this lateral fusion is actually occuring7.

a, In non-polarized cells grown on a solid support, the exocyst is localized to the cytoplasm and is possibly associated with vesicles. Syntaxins 3 and 4 are localized uniformly around the entire surface. Both apical- and basolateral-targeted vesicles fuse with the surface in contact with the support. b, In polarized cells, syntaxin 3 is confined to the apical surface, whereas syntaxin 4 is confined to the lateral surface. Apical vesicles fuse only with the apical surface, whereas basolateral vesicles fuse only with the lateral surface. The exocyst is recruited to the lateral surface. The exact location of the exocyst on this surface has been reported to be either confined to immediately below the tight junction43, 94 or along the upper one-to-two-thirds of the lateral surface7.

Unexpectedly, overexpression of the exocyst Sec10 subunit in MDCK cells has pleiotropic effects94. These cells are taller than control cells and when cultured in ECM gels, they form cysts more rapidly and efficiently. When these cysts are exposed to HGF, they form more tubules than control cysts. These results show that the exocyst controls polarized processes and are an example of the intimate connection between polarized membrane traffic and development. Overexpression of Sec10 also causes increased synthesis of basolateral membrane proteins, and indeed all secretory proteins, which probably underlies the morphological effects. All major secretory proteins undergo a comparable increase in synthesis, and as these proteins are under diverse types of transcriptional control, it is probable that the increase in synthesis is regulated by another mechanism. These proteins are all transported through the translocon, the channel that translocates proteins across the rough endoplasmic reticulum. Genetic interactions have been detected between a homologue of the Sec61 subunit of the translocon and the Sec15 subunit of exocyst, which in turn directly contacts Sec10 (ref. 95). These data suggest that there is a feedback mechanism connecting the final step of the secretory pathway (exocytosis of cargo) with the first committed step of the pathway (translocation across the rough endoplasmic reticulum). This is analogous to the regulation of metabolic pathways, where a product at the end of the pathway feeds back to regulate the first committed step. During development, the cell must change the composition and quantity of its basolateral plasma membrane. The unexpected exocyst–translocon interaction could provide a way to sense changing needs at the plasma membrane and relay this information back to the translocon to coordinate demand and synthesis. Furthermore, the localization and function of the exocyst may be deranged in diseases such as polycystic kidney disease.

Apart from the exocyst, there are a few other examples of potential mechanisms that coordinate polarization and membrane traffic. First, the mammalian homologue of Dlg (a subunit of one of the three polarization complexes discussed above) associates with the rough endoplasmic reticulum ribosome receptor, suggesting that similarly to the exocyst, Dlg may regulate the translocon96. Second, the plasma membrane SNARE syntaxin 2 was independently discovered as 'epimorphin', an epithelial morphogenetic signalling factor97. It is not clear how this protein can function both in intracellular membrane fusion and as an extracellular signalling factor. Syntaxin 2/epimorphin may reach its extracellular location via a non-classical secretory pathway, which is primarily apical in MDCK cells98.

Common principles are emerging through which polarized trafficking is integrated with the other processes of the cell. The exocyst–translocon system may be just one of many mechanisms responsible for this coordination. These mechanisms underlie the plasticity of membrane traffic and are thus key to understanding differentiation and dedifferentiation during development, as well as the adaptation of differentiated cells to changing physiological needs99.
