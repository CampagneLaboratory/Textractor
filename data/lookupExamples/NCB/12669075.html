Anti-silencing from the core: a histone H2A variant protects euchromatin

Marc Hild & Renato Paro

Marc Hild and Renato Paro are in Zentrum für Molekulare Biologie Heidelberg (ZMBH), University of Heidelberg, Im Neuenheimer Feld 282, 69120 Heidelberg, Germany.

Correspondence should be addressed to Renato Paro paro@sun0.urz.uni-heidelberg.de
To coordinate the coding complexity of the eukaryotic genome, mechanisms have evolved that protect active genes from silent chromatin structures such as heterochromatin. Although post-translational modifications of histone tails can regulate chromatin states, a new report now demonstrates that the composition of the nucleosome itself is also important for maintaining chromatin states.
Early cytological studies defined euchromatin and heterochromatin as two differentially stained portions of nuclear chromatin. Subsequent work has identified a multitude of additional structural and functional features that characterize these two forms. In general, heterochromatin is less active, whereas euchromatin represents the more active state. The fundamental packing unit of chromatin is the nucleosome, a histone octamer composed of two dimers (H2A–H2B) and a tetramer (H3–H4) around which genomic DNA is wound. Recent years have seen substantial progress in our understanding of the biochemical differences that result in changes in the higher-order organisation of the nucleosomes and form the basis of the two chromatin states. Euchromatin is rich in acetylated histone H3 and H4 and shows methylation of H3 at Lys 4. In contrast, heterochromatin is marked by hypo-acetylation of H3 and H4 and shows methylation of H3 at Lys 9. Recent evidence suggests that each of these epigenetic marks can influence the acquisition of the other two. Without protective mechanisms, heterochromatin can, for example, invade neighbouring active gene regions and silence transcription. Thus, an important question in the field is how the different chromatin states become limited to their domain of action along the chromosome and in the three-dimensional space of the nucleus.

A network of self-reinforcing loops and feedback mechanisms drive nucleation, spreading and maintenance of heterochromatin1. Assembly of heterochromatin at yeast telomeres exemplifies such a process. Nucleation is achieved by telomere-binding proteins, the yKu70–yKu80 heterodimer and Rap1, which recruit a complex of the Sir2 and Sir4 proteins to DNA. After deacetylation of histone tails by Sir2, Sir3 is recruited through interactions with Rap1, Sir4 and histone tails (Fig. 1). Given the mutual interactions of Sir2 and Sir4 with Sir3, and paralleled by the further deacetylation of histone tails, such a complex, once nucleated could spread along the chromatin fibre, thereby generating and maintaining a silenced state2.

Cells have developed antagonistic mechanisms to protect active regions of chromatin from being silenced. Studies of such counteracting sequence elements — called barriers or boundary elements — suggest that they can bind factors that attract chromatin-modifying activities and thereby create a localized region of open chromatin3. Despite the extensive progress that has recently been made in understanding how these elements might work, we are still far from a complete picture. The fact that in some cases, deletion of such elements results only in a limited spread of heterochromatin clearly argues for the existence of additional, so far undiscovered, mechanisms that oppose the spread of silencing structures.

Meneghini and colleagues now introduce a new combatant, H2A.Z (Htz1 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae), a variant of histone H2A, that funtions within the nucleosome itself in the battle between heterochromatin and euchromatin4. As Htz1 had previously been implicated in both silencing5 and activation6 of certain genes, Meneghini and colleagues embarked on an analysis of global changes in gene expression that occur in htz1 cells by using whole-genome yeast microarrays. This analysis yielded over 200 genes that are significantly activated by Htz1 and about 100 that are repressed. Strikingly, Htz1-activated genes tended to occur in small clusters that the authors termed HZADs (Htz1-activated domains) and were highly enriched within 30–40kb of the chromosome ends, whereas genes that were repressed by HTZ1 were randomly distributed. These findings raised the exciting possibility that Htz1 might protect genes from Sir-dependent telomeric silencing. To test this hypothesis, the authors examined global transcript levels in htz1 sir2 double-mutant cells and asked whether interfering with the machinery responsible for silencing at the telomere ends reverses the expression defect observed in htz1 cells. Indeed, for most of the Htz1-activated genes, they saw the predicted reversal. A careful analysis of their microarray results allowed the authors to exclude the trivial explanation that increased expression of silencing factors results in the htz1 defect, thus leaving open the possibility that Htz1 does indeed antagonize telomeric silencing.

The presence of a prominent HZAD flanking the mating-type cassette HMR in S. cerevisiae (another well-studied example of Sir-dependent silencing) allowed the authors to test whether Htz1-dependent gene activation is a general mechanism that is not limited to telomeric regions alone. The defect in expression of the HMR-proximal genes (HZAD2) in htz1 cells was suppressed by either deleting HMR, which removes known nucleation sites for silencing, or by deleting Sir2, showing that anti-silencing by Htz1 is indeed a general mechanism. But is this really caused by an unhindered spread of ectopic heterochromatin initiated at HMR into otherwise active chromatin? To resolve this question, the authors performed chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) analysis to monitor the characteristic changes in histone modifications that accompany heterochromatization. In both regions studied, they not only observed the expected decrease in the acetylation state of histone H4, but also a decrease in the methylation of histone H3 on Lys 4. Moreover, these changes in histone modification were also paralleled by spreading of the Sir complex, as demonstrated by increased binding of Sir2 and Sir3 to euchromatin. On the basis of this evidence, spreading of heterochromatin into regions that are normally protected against silencing by Htz1 seems the most likely explanation for the observed gene repression in htz1 cells.

So where and how does Htz1 perform its anti-silencing function? Htz1 could be located in silenced regions, where it may tether Sir proteins to heterochromatin. Alternatively, it could be a component of boundary elements and block the spread of heterochromatin into active regions. Htz1 could also be bound to the anti-silenced, that is, active genes. ChIP experiments found a relative enrichment of Htz1 at promoters and within the open reading frames of anti-silenced genes versus silencers and boundary elements. These findings argue that Htz1 functions locally because it is incorporated in the nucleosomes of anti-silenced genes. To rigorously exclude the possibility that Htz1 functions by binding to boundary elements, Meneghini and colleagues looked for synergistic interactions between htz1 and a deletion of the well-characterized boundary element (be) that flanks the HMR locus. They observed a significant increase in Sir3 spreading in the htz1 be double-mutant when compared with either single mutant, which is only consistent with Htz1 acting independently of the boundary element. Thus, incorporation of Htz1 in nucleosomes seems to be a second, novel mechanism to protect euchromatin from the inappropriate spreading of heterochromatin.

What are the structural features of H2A.Z that alter nucleosome functions to promote its anti-silencing activity? H2A.Z, an essential histone variant found in all higher eukaryotes, is highly conserved, with 90% sequence identity among different organisms. However, it shares only 60% sequence identity with major H2A, suggesting a unique function. Other histone variants have already been linked to specific roles. For example phosphorylation of H2AX is linked to DNA double-strand breaks, whereas macroH2A is enriched in the inactivated X chromosome7, 8. Similarly, variants of histone H3 seem to function in centromere function (Cid)9 and may also be involved in gene regulation (H3.3)10. Biochemical studies have shown that nucleosome arrays containing H2A.Z are more resistant to condensation, and thus the mere presence of H2A.Z-containing nucleosomes might interfere with condensation features required for heterochromatin. In addition, the interaction between the H3–H4 tetramer and the H2A.Z–H2B dimer is weakened by the presence of H2A.Z, thus lowering the energy of activation for polymerase migration through such chromatin structures. Recent studies have indicated that H2A.Z–H2B dimers are more readily released during transcription by Pol II, leaving behind a hexameric nucleosome structure on the DNA that seems to be positioned at the same location as the initial octamer11. Interestingly, this might also be a way to exchange H2A–H2B dimers with H2A.Z–H2B dimers and thereby allow the DNA to become locked into an active chromatin state.

This study introduces a novel mechanism to restrict silencing and simultaneously raises a host of new unresolved questions. The most pressing of these will be whether H2A.Z is in fact the signal that protects euchromatin, or whether H2A.Z deposition at anti-silenced regions is merely the consequence of an active chromatin state. Moreover, although boundary elements are inherent to the DNA and cannot change location, the deposition of H2A.Z must be controlled, so which factors decide where H2A.Z is localized and how is this mechanism regulated?
