A nucleolar disappearing act in somatic cloning

Tom Misteli

Tom Misteli is at the National Cancer Institute, National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD 20892, USA
mistelit@mail.nih.gov

One of the earliest structural changes in cloning by nuclear transfer is the disassembly of the nucleolus. The first insights into the molecular mechanisms of this event have now emerged and the results have tantalising implications for nucleolar architecture.
Eukaryotic cells are characterized by the presence of a multitude of morphologically and functionally distinct compartments. We understand little about how cytoplasmic organelles, such as the endoplasmic reticulum or the Golgi complex, are formed and how they are maintained. Even more puzzling is the issue of how distinct compartments within the cell nucleus, such as the nucleolus or splicing factor compartments, are spatially defined. In contrast to cytoplasmic compartments, nuclear compartments — which are stable and can even be biochemically isolated — are not surrounded by membranes. Other mechanisms must therefore exist to physically define these cellular domains. One of the major nuclear compartments, the nucleolus, has long been a major paradigm for the study of the intriguing biogenesis of nuclear compartments due to its distinct morphological features and its almost ubiquitous presence in most cell types and tissues. On page 205 of this issue, Gonda et al. now describe the first molecular activity involved in breaking down nucleolar architecture in Xenopus laevis1.

The nucleolus is an essential organelle because it is the site of synthesis of ribosomal RNAs (rRNA), which are indispensable for building ribosomes2, 3. The organelle forms around clusters of tandemly repeated rRNA genes and immature pre-rRNAs are then processed by a series of nuclease and base-modification events as they move vectorially away from their site of transcription (Fig. 1a)2, 3, 4. The morphological appearance of the nucleolus is heterogenous, with each region correlating with a distinct function. Whereas rRNA transcription occurs in the central fibrillar regions and dense fibrillar components, late processing of rRNAs occurs in the surrounding, morphologically distinct, granular components5. To address the question of what regulates this intricate architecture, Gonda et al. set out to identify factors that break it down. The motivation for their investigation was the fact that loss of the nucleolus is one of the hallmarks in cloning by nuclear transfer. When somatic cells of various species are placed into enucleated oocytes and undergo reprogramming, one of the first morphological changes in the transferred nucleus is the rapid loss of nucleolar integrity. This event can be faithfully reproduced in vitro by placing somatic Xenopus nuclei into frog egg extracts6. Using this system, Gonda et al. biochemically fractionated extracts to identify nucleolar disassembly activities. They found a single peak of activity containing two known RNA-binding proteins, FRGY2a and b. Consistent with the biochemical analysis, the authors found that recombinant FRGY2a/b is sufficient for nucleolar reorganization in vitro and overexpression of the protein resulted in nucleolar disassembly in vivo. Importantly, nucleolar disassembly by FRGY2a/b is reversible and occurs in the presence of ongoing transcription1.

Figure 1. Maintenance and disassembly of nucleolar architecture.

a, The interphase nucleolus forms around sites of rRNA transcription in fibrillar centres and dense fibrillar components. Immature rRNAs are processed in the surrounding granular compartment as they move vectorially away from their sites of transcription. The high local concentration of nucleolar processing factors may contribute to the structural coherence of the nucleolus by cross-linking rRNAs and proteins during processing. b, Nucleolar morphology is disrupted by inhibition of transcription, resulting in depletion of the rRNA pool. Alternatively, FRGY2a/b may interact non-specifically with rRNA and block RNA–protein interactions. In either case, the cross-links are disrupted, resulting in disassembly of the nucleolus.

This latter observation challenges the conventional view of nucleolar architecture. Two key findings have suggested that the raison d'être for the nucleolus is rRNA transcription itself7, 8, 9. After inhibition of rRNA transcription, nucleolar integrity is rapidly lost. Conversely, introduction of ribosomal genes on minichromosomes results in the formation of 'mini-nucleoli'. On the basis of this behaviour, it has implicitly been assumed that rRNA transcription is sufficient to maintain nucleolar structure7, 8, 9. This model has been supported by observations of reassembling nucleoli as they exit mitosis, where the nucleolar morphology is observed to re-establish in concert with a resumption of rRNA transcription10. However, recent evidence has hinted at additional levels of control in the maintenance of the nucleolus, possibly acting at the level of RNA processing3. In one study, early stages of nucleolar re-assembly of nucleoli have been reported to occur before resumption of rRNA transcription11. In addition, CDK2 inhibitors have been found to slow down RNA processing while having only a marginal effect, or no effect at all, on rRNA transcription3, 12. In these experiments, nucleolar morphology was grossly altered, although complete disassembly did not occur. The biochemical identification of FRGY2a/b as molecular factors that mediate nucleolar disassembly confirms these hints at the molecular level and it is the clearest evidence to date that nucleolar morphology can be uncoupled from transcription.

How, then, do FRGY2a/b disassemble nucleoli? They are highly homologous RNA-binding proteins that contain four stretches of basic/aromatic amino acids in their carboxyl terminus, the so-called 'BA' islands, which mediate RNA binding in a non-sequence-specific manner. The most likely scenario for their role in disassembly is one of competition, whereby FRGY2a/b binds ribosomal RNA non-selectively in the nucleolus and in this way prevents the interaction of rRNA with other RNAs or with nucleolar proteins (Fig. 1b). This idea is supported by the fact that the RNA-binding portion of FRGY2a/b can fully account for the disassembly effect and that sequential deletion of the BA islands from the protein results in increasingly weaker disassembly effects1. These observations make it probable that the intact morphological appearance of the organelle is dependent on intermolecular interactions involving RNA–RNA or RNA–protein interactions (Fig. 1a). This conclusion has the fascinating implication that rRNA functions as a structural element in the nucleolus. How exactly it does so, is unclear. rRNA molecules might directly interact with each other or, more likely, they might be cross-linked by nucleolar proteins. In either case, FRGY2a/b might interfere with these interactions and destabilize the nucleolar structure (Fig. 1b). One of the key questions from a conceptual point of view is whether the rRNA crosslinking is simply a 'by-product' of the complex rRNA processing events or whether it constitutes a separate, structural function of rRNAs. Furthermore, there is the possibility that FRGY2a/b does not exert its effect on rRNA at all, but on some other, as yet unidentified, structural RNA in the nucleolus. To address these important questions, it will now be crucial to identify protein and RNA interaction partners of FRGY2a/b and to determine what the function of these proteins is in the nucleolus.

Of course, the identification of a biochemical activity that results in transcription-independent nucleolar disassembly does not mean that transcription is not essential for nucleolar morphology, as suggested by the earlier transcriptional inhibition studies. The effect of FRGY2a/b is probably downstream of transcriptional inhibition (Fig. 1a). What the study by Gonda et al. does show is that nucleolar morphology can be lost by multiple, although probably related, ways. The nucleolus disassembles if the flow of transcripts is interfered with, either by blocking synthesis of the transcripts or by interference with rRNA interaction partners (Fig. 1b). The relative contributions of these two events to nucleolar morphology are unknown. It is also not clear at present whether FRGY2a/b is essential for disassembly or whether it simply facilitates the disassembly process. Conversely, it is clear that FRGY2a/b alone does not control nucleolar morphology, as it is not sufficient to maintain the nucleolus in the absence of transcription. Some of the answers to how FRGY2a/b disassembles the nucleolus might come from the study of what FRGY2a/b does in the interphase nucleus.

Apart from the obvious question of what the molecular basis for the effect of FRGY2a/b is, a fascinating issue will be to uncover what role, if any, FRGY2a/b has in nuclear reprogramming. As a somatic nucleus is introduced into the enucleated egg, rRNA transcription is repressed until the mid-blastula stage and one would therefore expect disassembly of the nucleolus to occur in any case. It is not obvious why a protein such as FRGY2a/b is needed at this point. It is possible that FRGY2a/b ensures rapid disassembly to prevent accumulation of partially processed rRNAs. An intriguing alternative possibility comes from the consideration that the nucleolus contains several regulators of cell proliferation13, 14. Facilitation of nucleolar disassembly by FRGY2a/b might purge the disassembling nucleolus in injected nuclei and re-establish the proliferative properties of embryonic stem cells. It will be important to determine whether FRGY2a/b is required for nuclear reprogramming, and if so, what its molecular targets and functions are.

Whatever the answers to these questions, the identification of a nucleolar disassembly activity is an important first step in the characterization of the molecular mechanisms underlying the biogenesis of nuclear structures, and it provides an interesting insight into the structural changes a nucleus undergoes during nuclear transfer and reprogramming. The fact that the evidence points strongly to a role for RNA interactions as a crucial building block in nucleolar architecture is intriguing and it would not be surprising if evidence were found for RNA as a crucial component for the structural integrity of other nuclear structures. How RNAs 'glue' the nucleolus, and possibly other nuclear compartments, together is a tantalising question and its answer is likely to tell us a great deal about the mysterious ways in which the cell maintains its nuclear compartments.
