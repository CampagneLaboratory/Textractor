A polarity complex of mPar-6 and atypical PKC binds, phosphorylates and regulates mammalian Lgl

Pamela J. Plant1, 3, James P. Fawcett1, 3, Dan C.C. Lin1, 2, Amy D. Holdorf1, Kathleen Binns1, 2, Sarang Kulkarni1 & Tony Pawson1, 2

1 Programme in Molecular Biology and Cancer, Samuel Lunenfeld Research Institute, Mount Sinai Hospital, 600 University Avenue, Toronto M5G 1X5 Canada

2 Department of Medical Genetics and Microbiology, University of Toronto, 1 Kings College Circle, Toronto M5S 1A8 Canada

3 These authors contributed equally to this work

Correspondence should be addressed to Tony Pawson pawson@mshri.on.ca
The evolutionarily conserved proteins Par-6, atypical protein kinase C (aPKC), Cdc42 and Par-3 associate to regulate cell polarity and asymmetric cell division, but the downstream targets of this complex are largely unknown. Here we identify direct physiological interactions between mammalian aPKC, murine Par-6C (mPar-6C) and Mlgl, the mammalian orthologue of the Drosophila melanogaster tumour suppressor Lethal (2) giant larvae. In cultured cell lines and in mouse brain, aPKC, mPar-6C and Mlgl form a multiprotein complex in which Mlgl is targeted for phosphorylation on conserved serine residues. These phosphorylation sites are important for embryonic fibroblasts to polarize correctly in response to wounding and may regulate the ability of Mlgl to direct protein trafficking. Our data provide a direct physical and regulatory link between proteins of distinct polarity complexes, identify Mlgl as a functional substrate for aPKC in cell polarization and indicate that aPKC is directed to cell polarity substrates through a network of protein–protein interactions.
Cellular polarity results from the differential localization of molecular components in the cell and is indispensable for the development and diversity of multicellular organisms. The molecular mechanisms underlying polarity have been explored in systems such as asymmetrically dividing cells and polarized epithelia. Such studies have identified distinct groups of polarity genes, whose members function coordinately to establish cellular asymmetry and encode multidomain proteins that interact with one another to form molecular complexes. In turn, these protein complexes mediate processes such as the assembly of cell junctions and cell adhesion, cytoskeletal reorganization, scaffolding of signalling complexes, regulation of protein trafficking and distribution of cell fate determinants1, 2, 3.

A central participant in cell polarity is a multiprotein complex containing the evolutionarily conserved Par-6, aPKC and Par-3 proteins. In Caenorhabditis elegans, these proteins localize in an interdependent fashion to the anterior pole of the single-cell embryo and, by controlling the orientation of the mitotic spindle and the distribution of regulatory proteins, determine asymmetric cell division4, 5, 6. In Drosophila, the orthologous proteins control epithelial polarity, as well as the fate of neuroblasts that delaminate from the epithelium7, 8. The vertebrate counterparts of Par-3, Par-6 and aPKC have been implicated in the organization of epithelial tight junctions9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, and mutations that inactivate the aPKC isoform in zebrafish lead to defects in epithelial polarity, mitotic spindle orientation and organogenesis15, 16.

The organization of the Par complex has been analysed primarily in mammalian cells. mPar-6 has a central scaffolding and regulatory function through its interactions with mPar-3, aPKC and the Cdc42 GTPase. The amino terminus of mPar-6 contains an OPC/PC motif within a PB1 domain that heterodimerizes with a complementary PB1 domain in the non-catalytic N-terminal region of aPKC17. The mPar-6 PB1 domain is followed by a CRIB motif, which engages the GTP-bound forms of the Cdc42 GTPase14, 18, 19; these findings are consistent with genetic data from C. elegans that show that Cdc42 functions together with Par-3, aPKC and Par-6 in asymmetric cell division20, 21. These observations implicate Par-6 and its associated proteins as potential Cdc42 targets involved in re-organization of the cytoskeleton22, 23, post-Golgi vesicle trafficking24, 25 and polarizing the microtubule network26. On the carboxy-terminal side of the CRIB motif, Par-6 has a PDZ domain that interacts with the N-terminal PDZ domain of mPar-3 (refs 14, 18).

A common theme in the regulation of cell polarity by the Par complex is the importance of the serine/threonine kinase activity of aPKC12, 13, 14, 18, 19, 27, 28. Although the aPKC isoforms / and are unaffected by Ca2+, diacylglycerol or phorbol esters, the activity of aPKC is potentially stimulated by its binding to Par-6, and the subsequent binding of Cdc42 to Par-6–aPKC11, 18, 19, 26. A crucial issue involves the substrates of aPKC that mediate its effects on cell polarity. In this context, additional complexes comprised of multidomain proteins have been implicated in related aspects of cell polarity. In Drosophila, Lethal (2) giant larvae (L(2)gl) and the PDZ domain proteins Discs large (Dlg) and Scribble (Scrib) act in concert as regulators of epithelial polarity and differentiation29, 30, 31, 32. L(2)gl contains five WD40 repeats, which are located N-terminal to a cluster of highly conserved serine residues, and was originally identified as a tumour suppressor in Drosophila imaginal discs33, 34.

L(2)gl may function in establishing polarity through a role in vesicle trafficking. The Saccharomyces cerevisiae homologue of L(2)gl, Sro77p, binds to Sec9p and functions in the docking and fusion of post-Golgi vesicles35. In Madin–Darby canine kidney (MDCK) cells, Mlgl localizes to the lateral membrane during the polarization and formation of cell–cell contacts36. Membrane-associated Mlgl co-precipitates with syntaxin 4, which has led to speculation that the Mlgl complex affects apical–basolateral polarity through vesicle targeting. This hypothesis is supported by the finding that a relative of the Lgl family, tomosyn, regulates Ca2+-dependent exocytosis in PC12 cells through an association with the t-SNARE syntaxin 1 (ref. 37).

To explore how mammalian Par proteins regulate cellular polarity, we have investigated proteins that interact with Par-6. Here we describe a complex comprising Mlgl, aPKC and mPar-6C and identify Mlgl as a functional target of the aPKC–mPar-6C complex.

Mlgl interacts with mPar-6C.
To identify proteins that interact with the mPar-6 complex, we transfected COS-1 cells with a construct encoding the murine mPar-6C isoform tagged at its N terminus with a Flag epitope. Lysates from COS-1 cells transfected with Flag–mPar-6C, or from untransfected cells, were incubated with M2 antibodies against Flag conjugated to agarose beads. After the captured proteins were resolved by SDS polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE), colloidal Coomassie blue and silver staining detected several proteins that co-precipitated with Flag–mPar-6C and were not present in the control (data not shown). We excised these polypeptides and prepared them for analysis by tandem mass spectrometry (Fig. 1a).

To test the specificity of the PDZ domain interaction, a GST fusion of only the PDZ domain of mPar-6C (residues 138–298) was incubated with rat brain lysate and found to precipitate endogenous Mlgl; by contrast, GST fusions of the second (457–598) and third (582–714) PDZ domains of mPar-3 did not bind Mlgl (Fig. 5d). Mlgl, aPKC and mPar-6C form a complex in vivo.
The N-terminal region of mPar-6C interacts with the aPKC isoforms PKC and PKC/ (refs 14, 18). We therefore investigated whether Mlgl, like mPar-6C, can form a complex with aPKC. Endogenous Mlgl was immunoprecipitated from COS-1 cells, and the associated proteins were detected by immunoblotting with antibodies against aPKC, which crossreact with aPKC/ . We found that aPKC co-precipitated with Mlgl (Fig. 6a) and, conversely, immunoprecipitates of endogenous aPKC contained Mlgl (data not shown), indicating that these two proteins form a physiological complex.

These immunoprecipitates were eluted with Flag peptide and then re-immunoprecipitated with antibodies against Mlgl or with pre-immune serum. aPKC directly binds and phosphorylates Mlgl.
The association of aPKC with Mlgl raised the possibility that Mlgl might be a substrate for aPKC. We therefore carried out in vitro kinase assays on Mlgl or control immunoprecipitates from 293T lysates. Mlgl was phosphorylated in the Mlgl immunoprecipitate, which also contained aPKC (Fig. 7a), but no corresponding phosphorylation was detected in the pre-immune precipitate. Similarly, a polypeptide corresponding in size to Mlgl was phosphorylated in the kinase reactions of aPKC immunoprecipitates, which were obtained by precipitating Flag-tagged wild-type aPKC (Fig. 7b). Conversely, no Mlgl was phosphorylated in the immunoprecipitates of a Flag-tagged, kinase-dead form of aPKC28, even though Mlgl was readily detected in association with the kinase-dead aPKC (Fig. 7b). These results indicate that Mlgl may be a substrate for aPKC.

Lgl contains five highly conserved and closely spaced serine residues (Ser 654, Ser 658, Ser 662, Ser 669 and Ser 672) that conform to consensus sites for PKC phosphorylation, (K/R)1–3X0–2(S/T)X0–2(K/R)1–3 (Fig. 7c, top). A peptide encompassing this region inhibits the in vitro phosphorylation of the Drosophila L(2)gl protein by an unidentified kinase38, and these serine residues are phosphorylated in vivo in Mlgl36. To investigate whether phosphorylation of these sites is mediated by aPKC, we mutated all five conserved serine residues to alanine (MlglS5A) in a Flag–Mlgl vector. Wild-type Mlgl and MlglS5A were expressed in COS-1 cells and immunoprecipitated with antibodies against Flag. Kinase assays on the immunprecipitates did not detect substantial phosphorylation of the MlglS5A mutant, although the wild-type protein was strongly phosphorylated (Fig. 7c, bottom), suggesting that the conserved serines in Mlgl are the principal sites for phosphorylation by aPKC. The absence of detectable phosphorylation could not be attributed to a loss of aPKC binding, because MlglS5A co-precipitated with both endogenous aPKC (Fig. 7c, bottom) and mPar-6C (Fig. 7d), indicating that the stable interaction of Mlgl with both aPKC and mPar-6C is independent of the serine phosphorylation sites.

To determine whether aPKC directly phosphorylates Mlgl, we carried out in vitro kinase assays with purified proteins. aPKC phosphorylated a fragment containing residues 418–1,034 of Mlgl and a smaller Mlgl polypeptide of residues 418–872 (Fig. 7e, lanes 3, 4). Both fragments contained the five conserved Mlgl serine residues, supporting the notion that these represent principal aPKC phosphorylation sites in Mlgl. Purified aPKC also bound directly to polypeptides containing residues 418–1,034 or 418–872 of Mlgl (Fig. 7f). Taken together, these data indicate that Mlgl is a substrate and binding partner of aPKC.

Mlgl phosphorylation sites are involved in polarizing migrating cells.
In a wounded monolayer of rat astrocytes, both mPar-6C and aPKC are recruited to the wound edge by activated Cdc42, and localized aPKC activity is required for inducing polarity26. To determine whether Mlgl is involved in the process of polarization, murine fibroblast migration was simulated using an in vitro scratch-wound assay39. A polarized morphology characterized by a re-orientation of Golgi, cell elongation and emergence of stress fibres perpendicular to the wound edge develops between 4 and 6 h (ref. 39), similar to that of astrocytes26, and a pronounced polarized phenotype is seen 10–12 h after wounding. Under these conditions, endogenous Mlgl localized with re-orientated Golgi complexes (Fig. 8a–d) and at the leading edge of the wounded cells (Fig. 8a, c, arrowheads).

Discussion
The Par-6–aPKC–Par-3–Cdc42 complex is known to regulate cellular polarity, but an understanding of its targets and, in particular, the relevant substrates of aPKC has been lacking. Here we have identified the mammalian orthologue of Drosophila L(2)gl as a binding partner for mPar-6C and a substrate for phosphorylation by aPKC. Mlgl interacts directly with both mPar-6C and aPKC to form a multiprotein complex, as shown by the co-precipitation of these three proteins from rodent brain, by the identification of an oligomeric Mlgl–mPar-6C–aPKC complex in transfected 293T cells and by the co-localization of the endogenous Mlgl and mPar-6 proteins to the Golgi apparatus in a cultured epithelial cell line. mPar-6C can associate with Mlgl both through its PDZ domain, and through an N-terminal segment containing the PB1 domain. The former interaction is direct and may involve the recognition of an internal sequence in the WD40 domain of Mlgl by the PDZ domain of mPar-6. Association of the N-terminal residues of mPar-6C with Mlgl may be bridged by aPKC, which binds directly both to mPar-6 through the formation of a PB1 domain heterodimer14, 18 and to Mlgl. The interactions of Mlgl, mPar-6 and aPKC are potentially subject to several stages of regulation.

Although mPar-3, mPar-6C and aPKC can associate, raising the possibility that mPar-3 is a member of the Mlgl–aPKC–mPar-6 complex, we have not detected endogenous mPar-3 in complex with Mlgl–aPKC–mPar-6 in postnatal day 10 or adult rodent brain (unpublished observations). Therefore, mPar-3–mPar-6–aPKC and Mlgl–mPar-6–aPKC may represent distinct complexes with discrete functions, although it is possible that mPar-3 associates with the Mlgl–aPKC–Par-6 complex in low quantities or under tight regulation.

Genetic analysis indicates that L(2)gl functions coordinately with two PDZ domain proteins, Dlg and Scrib, in regulating cellular polarity29, 30, 31, 32. Thus, the interaction between Mlgl and aPKC–mPar-6C provides a direct link between the Par-6–aPKC–Cdc42 complex and the Lgl–Dlg–Scrib unit. Together, these data suggest that cellular polarity and asymmetry is controlled, in part, by functional cross-talk between multiprotein complexes. In this regard, it is of interest that mPar-6 also interacts with the apically localized Pals–Crumbs polarity complex in epithelial cells40. Taken together, these data suggest that mPar-6 may serve as a hub to link the Pals–Crumbs, aPKC–mPar-3–mPar-6–Cdc42 and Lgl–Scrib–Dlg polarity units. Consistent with this hypothesis, these polarity complexes interact genetically in a hierarchical fashion during the establishment of epithelial polarity in Drosophila41, 42.

Mlgl can be phosphorylated in the Mlgl–aPKC–mPar-6 complex by a process that requires five closely spaced serine residues located in PKC consensus phosphorylation sites. The functional significance of phosphorylation of Mlgl by the aPKC–mPar-6C complex is suggested by the observation that a mutant of Mlgl lacking the five serine residues reduces the polarization of cells in response to wounding. This may reflect a function of Mlgl in the transport of proteins from the Golgi to the leading edge. Previous studies have shown that a serine to alanine mutant of Mlgl does not markedly influence the development of a polarized phenotype in MDCK cells, although the localization of the mutant protein is affected36. These differences may be due to the nature of the cell type or the polarizing stimulus. Thus, in wounded fibroblasts mPar-6C may function as a scaffold that co-localizes aPKC and Mlgl, induces Mlgl phosphorylation, and thereby regulates the polarizing function of Mlgl. L(2)gl has been shown to associate with an unidentified serine/threonine kinase38 and, together with its mammalian counterpart, to be phosphorylated on the conserved serines36, 38. Human lgl is also associated with a serine kinase activity43. Our results suggest that aPKC is a functional Mlgl kinase.

The inhibitory effect of the MlglS5A mutant on mammalian cell polarization is consistent with L(2)gl function in invertebrates. In Drosophila neuroblasts, L(2)gl directs basolateral transport of cell-fate determinants, and its localization to the neuroblast cortex through an association with Dlg is essential for this process31, 32. Similarly, in Drosophila epithelia, L(2)gl localization to septate junctions directs the recruitment of Dlg and Scrib and is imperative for its role in tumour suppression29. Taken together, these findings highlight the importance of localized invertebrate L(2)gl activity for its role in establishing cellular polarity. Similar to the role of L(2)gl in septate junction formation, the mPar-6–mPar-3–aPKC complex has been implicated in the development of tight junctions (the mammalian equivalent of septate junctions) and is recruited to early cell–cell contacts, probably through association with the junctional adhesion molecule44, 45. An interaction between Mlgl, mPar-6C and aPKC, and the consequent phosphorylation of Mlgl, may regulate Mlgl in a polarized fashion such that it can exert localized effects on protein trafficking and secretion.

The mechanisms by which Mlgl phosphorylation modifies its function remain to be established. Because Lgl family members in mammalian cells, invertebrates and yeast interact functionally and physically with myosins and t-SNARE proteins31, 32, 35, 36, 37, 43, 46, it is possible that Lgl multisite phosphorylation might control such protein–protein interactions, and thus protein/vesicle trafficking. Indeed, activation of the Drosophila kinase associated with L(2)gl results in the dissociation of myosin II heavy chain from L(2)gl and its release from the plasma membrane38. In addition, a peptide spanning residues 651–676 of L(2)gl, which corresponds to the conserved serine residues that we have defined as substrates for aPKC phosphorylation, inhibits phosphorylation and association with myosin II (ref. 38). These data are consistent with our results indicating that phosphorylation of these serine residues by aPKC–mPar-6C is important in mediating Mlgl function in mammalian cells.

We propose that the interaction between Mlgl and the asymmetrically localized mPar-6–aPKC complex may localize and phosphorylate a subpopulation of Mlgl, thereby creating a gradient of Mlgl activity in the cell. In turn, this gradient may direct the targeting of transport vesicles to specific regions of the cell, through the regulation of downstream protein–protein interactions after phosphorylation of Mlgl. This interaction between Mlgl and mPar-6–aPKC proteins, which were previously identified as components of distinct polarity complexes, suggests that cell polarity is regulated by the dynamic association of complexes that are linked into a larger network that can regulate many facets of cellular behaviour.

Methods
Cell culture
Human bladder carcinoma 5637, COS-1 and 293T cell lines were maintained in DMEM medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and antibiotics. We generated the mouse embryonic fibroblast (MEF) cell lines used in wounding assays as described39. For exogenous expression of proteins, COS-1 and 293T cell lines were transiently transfected with cDNAs using Lipofectin reagent and Opti-MEM media (Invitrogen, Burlington, ON, Canada) in accordance with the manufacturer's instructions.

Identification of interacting proteins with mass spectrometry
COS-1 cells were either transiently transfected with mPar-6C cDNA containing an N-terminal Flag epitope or left untransfected. Two days after transfection, cells were washed, lysed in PLC lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 150 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1% Triton X-100, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EGTA, 10 mM sodium pyrophosphate, 100 mM sodium fluoride, 1 mM phenyl methylsulphonyl fluoride (PMSF), 10 g ml-1 aprotinin, 10 g ml-1 leupeptin and 200 M sodium vanadate) and cleared. The lysate was incubated with Flag–M2-conjugated Sepharose beads (Sigma) at 4 °C for 1 h, washed twice with HNTG buffer (20 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 500 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 0.1% Triton-X 100, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EGTA, 10 mM sodium phosphate, 100 mM sodium fluoride, 10 g ml-1 aprotinin, 10 g ml-1 leupeptin, 200 M sodium vanadate and 1 mM PMSF) and three times with HNTG containing 150 mM NaCl. We eluted proteins from the beads with SDS sample buffer and separated them by 10% SDS–PAGE. Gels were stained with colloidal Coomassie (GelCode Blue Stain Reagent; Pierce, Rockford, IL) or with silver stain. Bands were excised, reduced, alkylated and digested in-gel with trypsin as described47. Peptides were extracted from the gel and desalted by ZipTip desalting columns (Millipore, Bedford, MA), which were equilibrated in 5% formic acid, washed with equilibration buffer and eluted with 5% formic acid, 60% methanol (v/v).

Tandem mass spectrometry analysis was done by using a nano-electrospray ionization (ESI) source (Protana A/S) coupled to a high-performance hybrid quadrapole time-of-flight API QSTARTM Pulsar mass spectrometer (MDS-Sciex, Concord, ON, Canada). Doubly or triply charged tryptic parent ion candidates were selected, and product ion spectra were generated by collision-induced dissociation (CID). We searched database by using the Mascot MS/MS search engine (Matrix Science, London, UK).

Constructs, mutagenesis, fusion proteins and antibodies
Full-length Mlgl cDNA was obtained by ligating mouse EST 35925 (IMAGE clones; ATCC, Rockville, MD) to an N-terminal fragment derived from an RT–PCR reaction of mouse testis poly(A) RNA generated using primers 5'-AAGATGATGAAGTTTCGGTTCCGGCGGCA-3' (forward) and 5'-GTCATCTAGCAGGGTCAG-3' (reverse). We cloned full-length Mlgl into pFLAG–CMV2 (Sigma), pcDNA3.1 (Invitrogen) and pECFP (BD Bioscience, Clontech, Mississauga, ON, Canada) vectors. The MlglS5A (amino acids 654, 658, 662, 669 and 672) construct was generated by the Quikchange kit (Qiagen) with primers 5'-CCACTGTCACGGGTGAAGGCCCTCAAGAAGGCACTG AGACAGGCATTCCGGCGAATCCGCAAGGCCCGTGTCGCAGG
CAAAAAACGG-3' (forward) and 5'-CCGTTTTTTGCCTG
CGACACGGGCCTTGCGGATTCG
CCGGAATGCCTGTCTCAGTGCCTTCTTGAGGGCCTTCACCCGT
GACAGTGG-3' (reverse).

Constructs encoding GST fusion proteins of the WD40 domains (residues 1–476), the C-terminal region (867–1,034), the C-terminal region including conserved serine residues (418–1,034), the serine residues alone (418–872) and the loop region between WD40 repeats 4 and 5 (266–423) were generated by subcloning fragments of Mlgl cDNA into the pGEX 4TK (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Piscataway, NJ) vector. All mPar-3 and mPar-6C cDNA constructs, unless stated otherwise, were generated as described18. We generated mPar-6C fusion proteins by amplifying regions of the mPar-6C cDNA corresponding to full-length (residues 1–346), the N-terminal (1–167); the N-terminal region PB1 and CRIB region (1–180), the CRIB and PDZ domain (117–298) and the C-terminal region (250–346) and subcloning the products in frame into pFLAG–CMV2, pEYFP or pPRO EX HTc (Life Technologies). Full-length C-terminally Flag-tagged murine aPKC and kinase-dead aPKC were a gift from C. L. Carpenter (Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Centre). HA-tagged mPar-6A and mPar-6B were a gift from I. Macara (University of Virginia).

Rabbit polyclonal antibodies against Mlgl were generated against a GST fusion protein of the Mlgl C terminus and were affinity-purified by passing the serum sequentially over CNBr-linked GST and GST-tagged Mlgl C terminus Sepharose columns. The 043 rabbit polyclonal antibody has been described18. We assessed antibody specificity by resolving 50 g of rodent brain lysate by SDS–PAGE, transferring the proteins to nitrocellulose and probing them with either the 043 antibodies or affinity-purified antibodies against Mlgl. Only one prominent band was seen for each antibody. In addition, a rabbit polyclonal antibody (5056) was generated against a human Par-6C C-terminal peptide (GWGSRIRGDGSGFSL). The 5056 antibody was purified by protein A. Mouse monoclonal antibodies against the 58K Golgi marker and M2 monoclonal antibodies against Flag were from Sigma. Polyclonal antibodies against aPKC were from Santa Cruz Biotechnology (Santa Cruz, CA) and mouse monoclonal anti-tetra His antibodies were from Qiagen.

Immunoprecipitation and immunoblotting
Cleared cell lysate was quantified using the Bio-Rad Protein Assay Kit (Bio-Rad, Mississauga, ON, Canada) and subjected to immunoprecipitation with 1 g ml-1 antibodies. After incubation with Sepharose conjugated to either Protein A or antibodies against mouse IgG, beads were washed twice with HNTG buffer containing 500 mM NaCl and three times with HNTG buffer containing 150 mM NaCl. We separated proteins by SDS–PAGE and analysed them by immunoblotting with the indicated antibodies. For double immunoprecipitation experiments, after the HNTG wash Protein A beads were resuspended in 50 mM Tris (pH 8.0) and incubated twice with 100 g ml-1 Flag (DYKDDDDK) peptide (Sigma) for 10 min to elute Flag immunocomplexes. Eluates were then subjected to another immunoprecipitation followed by SDS–PAGE and immunoblotting.

Immunofluorescence
Cells grown on glass coverslips were washed several times with PBS and fixed with ice-cold methanol for 5 min or 4% paraformaldehyde and processed as described18. Phalloidin-conjugated Texas Red (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR) was used to visualize actin stress fibres. We mounted all samples with p-phenylenediamine to retard photobleaching. Microscopy was done using a Leica DMRX microscope equipped with fluorescence and transmitted light optics or with an Olympus 1X-70 inverted microscope equipped with fluorescence optics and Deltavision Deconvolution Microscopy software (Applied Precision, Issaquah, WA).

In vitro binding experiments and kinase assays
For the pull-down assays, 100 ng of purified PKC (Calbiochem, San Diego, CA) or 500 ng of His–Par6C proteins was incubated in 1 ml of 1% Nonidet P-40 (NP-40) in PBS containing 1 mM dithiothreitol, aprotonin, leupeptin and PMSF with a tenfold molar excess of Lgl–GST fusion proteins immobilized on glutathione–Sepharose beads, for 1 h at 4 °C. Bound proteins were washed three times in 1% NP-40 in PBS buffer, resolved by SDS–PAGE and transferred to a nitrocellulose membrane. Membranes were probed with antibodies against PKC or against tetra-His.

For the kinase assays, 100 ng of purified PKC was incubated with a tenfold molar excess of Lgl–GST fusion proteins attached to glutathione sepharose beads in kinase reaction buffer (50 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 20 mM MgCl2, 4 mM MnCl2 and 10 Ci of 32P-labelled -ATP). The reactions were run at 30 °C for 6 min and then quenched with 2 SDS sample buffer containing 5 mM EDTA. We analysed the reaction products by SDS–PAGE, followed by autoradiography.

For the in vivo kinase assays, Flag–Mlgl and Flag–Mlgl5SA were immunoprecipitated with antibodies against Flag. The beads were washed twice in HNTG buffer and twice with kinase reaction buffer (50 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 25 mM MgCl2, 4mM MnCl) supplemented with 100 M orthovanadate, resuspended in kinase reaction buffer with orthovanadate and 10 Ci of 32P-labelled -ATP, and then incubated at 20 °C for 30 min. Reactions were stopped by the addition of SDS sample buffer and analysed by 10% SDS–PAGE. We dried the gels and quantified the bands by a phosphorimager (Bio-Rad).

Wounding assays and microinjection
Wounding assays were done as described39. In brief, MEFs were plated on glass coverslips and grown to 70–80% confluency. Cells were serum-starved for 24 h until 100% confluency. We generated experimental wounds by dragging a pipette tip across the coverslip. pEYFP vector alone (n = 280 total for three separate experiments), wild-type Mlgl–pFLAG–CMV2 (n = 349) and MlglS5A–pFLAG–CMV2 (n = 204) were injected into the nuclei, and the cells were returned to serum-free media and allowed to recover for 10–12 h before staining. Microinjections were done using a Leica DMIRE2 inverted microscope equipped with a heating stage and an Eppendorf 5242 microinjector. An elongated morphology, and/or microfilament bundles oriented perpendicular to the wound edge as detected by phalloidin staining, were used as criteria to define a polarized phenotype39, 48, 49, 50. We scored both polarized and non-polarized cells. To determine the extent of polarization, the number of microinjected cells expressing MlglS5A protein with polarized morphologies was divided by the total number of cells expressing MlglS5A and multiplied by 100 for each of three separate experiments. Differences were statistically analysed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA).
