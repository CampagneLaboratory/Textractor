Identification of the ubiquitin–protein ligase that recognizes oxidized IRP2

Koji Yamanaka1, 6, Haruto Ishikawa2, 3, 4, 6, Yuzuru Megumi3, Fuminori Tokunaga3, 4, Masato Kanie3, Tracey A. Rouault5, Isao Morishima2, Nagahiro Minato1, Koichiro Ishimori2, 4 & Kazuhiro Iwai1, 3, 4

The ubiquitin system is involved in several basic cellular functions1, 2, 3. Ubiquitination is carried out by a cascade of three reactions catalysed by the E1, E2 and E3 enzymes. Among these, the E3 ubiquitin–protein ligases have a pivotal role in determining the specificity of the system by recognizing the target substrates through defined targeting motifs1, 2, 3. Although RING finger proteins constitute an important family of E3 ligases4, only a few post-transcriptional modifications, including phosphorylation1, proline hydroxylation5, 6 and glycosylation7, are known to function as recognition signals for E3. Iron regulatory protein 2 (IRP2), a modulator of iron metabolism, is regulated by iron-induced ubiquitination and degradation8. Here we show that the RING finger protein HOIL-1 functions as an E3 ligase for oxidized IRP2, suggesting that oxidation is a specific recognition signal for ubiquitination. The oxidation of IRP2 is generated by haem, which binds to IRP2 in iron-rich cells, and by oxygen, indicating that the iron sensing of IRP2 depends on the synthesis and availability of haem.

The metabolism of iron must be tightly regulated owing to the toxicity of this element, and regulation is achieved in mammalian cells through iron-dependent control of IRP1 and IRP2 (refs 9,10). IRP1 and IRP2 have largely redundant roles in post-transcriptional gene regulation; however, IRP2-null mice but not IRP1-null mice develop adult-onset neurodegenerative disease11. IRP1 is a stable bi-functional protein and its activity is regulated by the assembly and disassembly of its iron–sulphur cluster9, 10. By contrast, IRP2 is regulated by iron-induced ubiquitination and degradation. A specific domain of 73 amino acids, the iron-dependent degradation (IDD) domain, is essential for targeting the protein for degradation after iron-mediated oxidative modification8, 12.

We first attempted to identify an E3 involved in the ubiquitination of oxidized IRP2 (IRP2-E3). The ubiquitination of oxidized IRP2 was inhibited by the addition of an IDD domain that had been expressed in Escherichia coli and oxidized in vitro (Fig. 1a). By contrast, the addition of an IDD domain derived from insect cells and oxidized in vitro enhanced the ubiquitination of IRP2 in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 1b). Because insect cells but not bacteria contain an active ubiquitin system, we thought that the IRP2-E3 might associate with the IDD domain from insect cells. Indeed, further analyses suggested that the IDD domain functions as the recognition site for the IRP2-E3 (data not shown).

At low oxygen pressure IRP2 is stable even in the presence of iron13. We therefore established a differential two-hybrid screening in which yeast cells were cultured in either aerobic or anaerobic conditions using the IDD domain as a bait to identify the IRP2-E3. One of the isolated clones, H3 (Fig. 1c), contained the whole coding region of a RING finger protein (Fig. 1d). We designated the protein HOIL-1 (for haem-oxidized IRP2 ubiquitin ligase-1). HOIL-1 has been cloned independently as a protein associated with the hepatitis B virus X protein (XAP3)14, protein kinase C (RBCK)15 and UbcM4 (UIP28)16.

When co-transfected into Cos cells, HOIL-1 associated with IRP2 in cells treated with ferric ammonium citrate (FAC) or haemin, but not in those treated with desferrioxamine (Df; Fig. 2a), suggesting that HOIL-1 binds to IRP2 only in iron-rich conditions. It has been shown that the amount of Myc-tagged IRP2 expressed stably in RD4 cells under the control of an inducible promoter is almost identical to that of endogenous IRP2 and that the protein is degraded in an iron-dependent manner as efficiently as its endogenous counterpart8, 17. In addition, the chimaeric protein IRP1+73, in which the IDD domain is inserted into IRP1 in an analogous position in the sequence, has been shown to undergo iron-dependent degradation8.

Full Figure and legend (90K)
We assessed the binding of HOIL-1 to IRP2 and IRP1+73 in RD4 cells. Both IRP2 and IRP1+73 were co-immunoprecipitated with HOIL-1 from lysates of cells treated simultaneously with the proteasome inhibitor MG132 and either FAC or haemin, but not Df, suggesting that endogenous HOIL-1 binds to IRP2 only in iron-rich conditions by recognizing the IDD domain (Fig. 2b). In vitro ubiquitination assays using purified components showed that oxidized IRP2 was ubiquitinated efficiently only in the presence of both E2 (UbcH5c) and HOIL-1 (Fig. 2c) and that oxidized IRP2 was ubiquitinated more efficiently by HOIL-1 than by native IRP2 (Fig. 2d). Collectively, these results suggest that HOIL-1 is an IRP2-E3.

We examined whether the ubiquitin-like (Ubl) and RING finger domains of HOIL-1 are involved in HOIL-1 binding to the IDD domain. Whereas a HOIL-1 mutant lacking the Ubl domain (HOIL-128–88) did not bind to the IDD domain in a yeast two-hybrid analysis, HOIL-1 mutants that either lacked the RING finger (HOIL-1240–283) or carried mutations in the two consensus cysteine residues in the RING finger (HOIL-1C240,243S) bound to the domain. The Ubl domain alone (residues 1–88) was sufficient for binding to the IDD domain (Fig. 3a). In iron-treated cells, HOIL-1 and HOIL-1C240,243S bound to IRP2, whereas the binding of HOIL-1240–283 to IRP2 was significantly reduced and HOIL-128–88 did not bind to IRP2 at all. An alternatively spliced form of HOIL-1, HOIL-1L, which is translated from a different initiation site but contains the Ubl domain, also bound to IRP2 even though it was expressed in much lower quantities than HOIL-1 (Fig. 3b). These results suggest that the Ubl domain of HOIL-1 is involved in substrate recognition. Involvement of the Ubl domain in substrate recognition has been also reported for the RING finger E3 Parkin, which was identified as the gene responsible for a familial form of Parkinson's disease18, 19.

We examined the role of HOIL-1 in iron-dependent degradation of IRP2 in HEK293 cells by transfecting vectors expressing IRP2 and the HOIL-1 mutants. In control transfected cells, the quantity of IRP2 was clearly lower in cells treated with FAC than in non-treated cells, suggesting that exogenous IRP2 is recognized by endogenous E3 (Fig. 3c). Iron-induced downregulation of IRP2 was reversed in cells co-transfected with HOIL-1C240,243S, but not with HOIL-128–88, suggesting that the RING-finger-mutated ligase can sequester IRP2 but does not possess E3 activity because of its inability to bind to E2 (ref. 2 and Fig. 4d). The inability of HOIL-1240–283 to stabilize IRP2 might be due to its weak binding to IRP2 in mammalian cells (Fig. 3b). Taken together, these data indicate that HOIL-1 may function as an IRP2-E3 in cells.

As haem may be involved in IRP2 degradation20, we used HOIL-1 to address directly the involvement of haem in the ubiquitination of IRP2. We first monitored the binding of haem to IRP2 in vitro. The absorption spectrum of haemin-treated IRP2 clearly showed the Soret band at about 420 nm that is typical of absorption by haem-binding proteins (Fig. 4a). Further analysis suggested that the molar ratio of haem to IRP2 was about 1 and that haem bound to the IDD domain, suggesting that the IDD domain is the haem-binding site (H. I., manuscript in preparation).

We then examined the binding of haem to IRP2 in insect cells infected with a baculovirus expressing IRP2. The absorption spectrum of IRP2 (Fig. 4b) purified from insect cells treated with haemin (unbroken line) clearly showed the Soret band even though the absorption was weaker than that of IRP2 treated with haemin in vitro. By contrast, the absorption spectrum of IRP2 purified from untreated cells (broken line) did not show the Soret band, indicating that haem binds to IRP2 in haemin-treated cells.

We tested the effect of haem on ubiquitination of IRP2 by transfecting the IRP2 expression vector into HEK293 cells (Fig. 4c). When cells were co-transfected with a control plasmid, the expression of IRP2 was not largely affected in the presence of FAC, suggesting that endogenous HOIL-1 was overwhelmed by the large quantity of exogenously expressed IRP2. In cells co-transfected with HOIL-1, however, IRP2 was downregulated in the presence of FAC or haemin, and iron-induced downregulation of IRP2 was suppressed in the presence of the proteasome-specific inhibitor lactacystin, suggesting that IRP2 is ubiquitinated efficiently by exogenous HOIL-1 and degraded by the proteasome. Treating the cells with succinyl acetone, an inhibitor of haem synthesis, inhibited the downregulation of IRP2 by FAC but not haemin, indicating that haem synthesis is essential for the ubiquitination of IRP2.

An in vitro ubiquitination assay showed that haem-loaded IRP2 was ubiquitinated by wild-type HOIL-1, but not by any HOIL-1 mutants tested or an unrelated E3 (VBC-Cul2 ligase21; Fig. 4d), confirming that HOIL-1 is indeed the IRP2-E3 and that HOIL-1 with mutations in the RING finger domain loses its E3 ligase activity. Last, we showed that haem-bound IRP2 was ubiquitinated by HOIL-1 even more efficiently than its oxidized counterpart and that the ubiquitination of haem-bound IRP2 was strongly suppressed in an anaerobic reaction (Fig. 4e).

On the basis of these results, we conclude that HOIL-1 functions as an IRP2-E3 and that both haem and oxygen are necessary for IRP2 ubiquitination. We note, however, that the existence of another IRP2-E3 cannot be excluded. Although we have observed that IRP2 that is oxidized but not bound to haem (oxidized IRP2) is ubiquitinated efficiently in vitro, haem-bound IRP2 is ubiquitinated by HOIL-1 much more efficiently than is oxidized IRP2. We therefore suspect that the small amount of haem binds to IRP2 when IRP2 is purified from non-treated insect cells and that motifs recognized by HOIL-1 might be generated by the reaction of haem and oxygen in vitro. Although clarification of precise mechanisms underlying IRP2 oxidation is needed, we can propose a tentative model of iron-induced IRP2 ubiquitination. In this model haem binds to the IDD domain in iron-rich cells, after which a superoxide radical is generated by the reaction of haem and oxygen22. On oxidation by the superoxide radical, the IDD domain is subsequently recognized by HOIL-1 and IRP2 is ubiquitinated.

Oxidation is a chief cause of protein damage. Oxidized proteins are degraded rapidly; however, little is known about the mechanisms that underlie their selective removal23, 24. Removing damaged proteins is an important component of cellular mechanisms of quality control. Notably, IRP2 oxidation functions as a specific signal for ubiquitination but the oxidized protein is not inactivated: IRP2 maintains its activity in iron-rich cells treated with proteasome inhibitors8. Thus, identification of the mechanisms involved in IRP2 oxidation and its recognition by HOIL-1 may have broader biological implications in understanding the roles of protein oxidation. We have shown that the stability of IRP2 is regulated by haem. By contrast, IRP1 is regulated by the assembly and disassembly of its iron–sulphur cluster9, 10. Our results therefore indicate that iron availability in cells may be sensed by two different systems: iron–sulphur cluster assembly and haem availability. The iron concentration required for downregulating IRP2 is identical to that for disassembling the iron–sulphur cluster of IRP1 (unpublished results). Thus, the synthesis of both haem and iron–sulphur clusters seems to be coordinately regulated by the availability of cellular iron.

Methods
Cell culture and transfection to cells
We cultured HEK293 and Cos cells in DMEM medium supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum (Sigma, St Louis, MO), 100 IU ml-1 penicillin G and 100 g ml-1 streptomycin. RD4 stable transformants expressing IRP2 or IRP1+73 under the control of a dexamethasone-inducible promoter were cultured as described8. Transient transfection to Cos cells was done by electroporation as described8. Transient transfection to HEK293 cells was done by using Fugene 6 according to the manufacturer's (Roche Diagnostics, Indianapolis, IN) instructions.

Plasmids
The Myc-tagged IRP2 expression vector (pXS–IRP2) has been described17. We subcloned a DNA fragment encoding the IDD domain amplified by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) into pT7-7–His or pGBKT7 (Clontech, Palo Alto, CA) for expression in bacteria or yeast, respectively. HOIL-1L was cloned from HEK293 messenger RNA by PCR with reverse transcription. We inserted the HOIL-1 and HOIL-1L complementary DNAs into pcDNA3.1–RGS–(His)6. HOIL-1 mutants, constructed by two-step PCR methods8, were subcloned into pGADT7 (Clontech) or pcDNA3.1–RGS–(His)6 for expression in yeast or mammalian cells, respectively. cDNAs encoding (His)6-tagged IDD domain, HOIL-1 and the various HOIL-1 mutants were subcloned into pVL1393 (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA).

Expression of recombinant proteins
E2 and ubiquitin tagged with glutathione S-transferase (GST) were expressed and purified from E. coli as described21. The (His)6-tagged IDD domain was purified by the same method as the E2 enzymes21. We generated recombinant baculovirus and infected High Five cells as described21. Recombinant E1, IRP2, the IDD domain and HOIL-1 were purified as described21. We purified VBC-Cul2 from baculovirus-infected insect cells as described21.

Generation of antiserum against HOIL-1
We amplified a cDNA encoding residues 337–385 of HOIL-1 (HOIL-1-I) by PCR and subcloned it into pGEX-2TK (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Piscataway, NJ). We expressed and purified GST–HOIL-1-I as described21 and used it to raise rabbit antiserum against GST–HOIL-1-I.

Yeast two-hybrid screening
AH109 cells were transformed first with pGBKT7–IDD by the lithium acetate method and then with a cDNA library of the human kidney cancer cell line UOK111 (a gift from R. Klausner) constructed in pGADT7 (prepared in our laboratory). We plated 1.4 106 transformants onto 150-mm dishes containing SD medium lacking histidine, tryptophan and leucine, but including 2.5 mM 3-aminotriazole and 25 M ferrous ammonium sulphate, and incubated them for 4 d. Roughly 500 His+, Trp+ and Leu+ colonies were picked up and screened further by anaerobic culture for 4 d on SD plates lacking histidine, tryptophan and leucine, but including 2.5 mM 3-aminotriazole and 40 g ml-1 ergosterol. Plasmids from colonies growing in this second screen were recovered and characterized further.

We assessed the binding of HOIL-1 mutants to the IDD domain by culturing for 3 d AH109 cells transformed with pGBKT7–IDD and pGADT7 containing the HOIL-1 mutant cDNAs on SD plates lacking histidine, tryptophan and leucine, but including 2.5 mM 3-aminotriazole and 25 M ferrous ammonium sulphate.

Immunoprecipitation and immunoblotting
RD4 stable transformants expressing IRP2 or IRP1+73 were cultured for 24 h in the presence of 20 nM dexamethasone to induce the expression of recombinant protein. After 24 h, 10 M MG132 was added for 20 min before the addition of 100 M Df, 100 g ml-1 FAC or 20 M haemin to individual plates. Three hours after adding Df, FAC or haemin, the cells were lysed.

RD4 stable transformants expressing IRP2 or IRP1+73, and Cos cells transiently transfected with the expression plasmids indicated in the figure legends were lysed with buffer containing 1% Triton X-100, 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 150 mM NaCl and 2 mM phenyl methylsulphonyl fluoride. After centrifugation, the supernatant was immunoprecipitated with rabbit antiserum against HOIL-1 (RD4 cells) or antibodies (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) against RGS–(His)6 (Cos cells) and then incubated with Sepharose beads conjugated to Protein A (Amersham). Beads were washed five times with lysis buffer and then twice with 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5). Immunoprecipitates were resolved by SDS–PAGE.

Lysates from HEK293 cells transiently transfected with the expression plasmids indicated in figure legends were resolved on SDS–PAGE and transferred to the poly(vinylidene difluoride) membranes. After blocking in a buffer containing PBS, 5% skimmed milk and 0.1% Tween 20, membranes were incubated with primary antibody followed by an appropriate horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibody (Amersham Bioscience). Membranes were visualized with the Supersignal western blotting detection system (Pierce, Rockford, IL) in accordance with the manufacturer's recommendations.

In vitro ubiquitin conjugation assay
We oxidized 0.25 g l-1 of Myc-tagged IRP2 with 5 M FeCl3 and 10 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) at 37 °C for 5 min as described12. S100 lysates of HeLa cells were prepared as described12. The conjugation reaction mixture contained (in a final volume of 20 l) 1 g of IRP2, 20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.5), 5 mM MgCl2, 2 mM DTT, 0.5 g of ubiquitin aldehyde (Ubal) and 5 g of ubiquitin or 5 g of GST–ubiquitin. We added 1 g of purified wild-type or mutant HOIL-1, and 100 ng of E1 or 50 ng of E2 as indicated in figure legends. The reaction mixtures were incubated in the presence of ATP and ATP regenerating system (0.5 mM ATP, 10 mM creatine phosphate, 10 g of creatine phosphokinase) for 60 min or 20 min, for reactions containing ubiquitin or GST–ubiquitin, respectively at 37 °C. Reactions were stopped either by adding 1% Nonidet P-40, 0.5% deoxycholate, 50 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 150 mM NaCl, 10 mM N-ethylmaleimide (NEM) and 0.1% SDS for 15 min, followed by cysteine to neutralize the NEM (all experiments except those in Fig. 4d), or by adding 4SDS sample buffer containing 200 mM Tris-HCl (pH 6.8), 8% SDS, 400 mM DTT, 40% glycerol and 0.2% bromophenol blue (experiments in Fig. 4d). IRP2 was immunoprecipitated with antibodies against Myc, resolved on 6% SDS–PAGE and analysed by immunoblot with rabbit antiserum against ubiquitin (a gift from A. Ciechanover) or antibodies against GST (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Santa Cruz, CA).
