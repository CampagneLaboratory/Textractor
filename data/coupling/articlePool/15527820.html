The influence of G protein subtype on agonist action at D2 dopamine receptors

Sarah A. Nickolls1 and Philip G. Strange,

School of Animal and Microbial Sciences, University of Reading, Whiteknights, Reading RG6 6AJ, UK

Received 23 March 2004;  Revised 28 May 2004;  accepted 18 June 2004.  Available online 1 September 2004.


Abstract

In previous studies, we have shown that agonists influence the ability of D2 dopamine receptors to couple to G proteins and here we extend this work. The human D2Short dopamine receptor and a natural polymorphism of this D2Short(Ser311Cys), have been studied by co-expressing the receptors in insect cells with G12 and either Go, Gi1, Gi2 or Gi3 G protein subunits. These preparations have been used to study the G protein coupling profiles of the two receptors and the influence of agonists. Receptor/G protein coupling was analysed in dopamine/[3H]spiperone competition binding experiments and through stimulation of [35S]GTPS binding. Although the Ser311Cys polymorphism itself had no appreciable effect on the G protein coupling specificity of the D2 receptor, agonist stimulation of [35S]GTPS binding, revealed that both dopamine and (+)-3PPP showed a clear preference for Go compared to the Gi subtypes, but quinpirole did not. These results indicate that agonists are able to stabilise different receptor conformations with different abilities to couple to G proteins.

1. Introduction

The neurotransmitter, dopamine exerts its actions by binding to G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs). Dopamine receptors are divided into two subclasses; the D1-like, which includes D1 and D5 receptors, and the D2-like, which includes D2, D3, and D4 receptors. In addition, the D2 receptor exists as two splice variants of the same gene, D2L and D2S, which differ by a 29 amino acid insert in the third intracellular loop of D2L (Grandy et al., 1989 and Giros et al., 1989). Agonist binding to D2 receptors results in the activation of several different signalling pathways including inhibition of adenylyl cyclase (Hall and Strange, 1997) and modulation of both calcium channels ( Liu et al., 1994) and potassium channels ( Liu et al., 1999). The pertussis toxin-sensitive nature of the majority of these responses leads to the conclusion that these responses are mediated by G proteins in the Gi/o family.

The concept of agonist-induced receptor trafficking (Kenakin, 1995) describes the phenomenon of different agonists being able to stabilise different conformations of the same receptor which have the ability to selectively activate one G protein over another. Evidence for this phenomenon has come from a number of systems including the 5-HT2C receptor (Berg et al., 1998), the muscarinic acetylcholine receptor ( Akam et al., 2001) and the 2 adrenoceptor (Wenzel-Seifert and Seifert, 2000), see also Kenakin (2002) and Hermans (2003) for reviews of the topic. Evidence suggesting agonist trafficking at the D2 dopamine receptor first came from the observation that the agonists (+)-3PPP and quinpirole exhibited a reversal of relative efficacy between D2 receptors in different locations (striatum and pituitary gland) (Meller et al., 1992). More recently, we have demonstrated that the rat D2L receptor preferentially couples to Go over Gi2 (Cordeaux et al., 2001), as does the human D2S receptor (Nickolls and Strange, 2003) and that the human D2L receptor preferentially couples to Go over each of the three Gi subtypes (Gazi et al., 2003). Additional reports ( Watts et al., 1998; Kilts et al., 2002 and Shapiro et al., 2003) also support the idea that D2 dopamine receptor agonists can have specific effects on signalling systems.

It has been proposed that polymorphisms in the gene encoding the D2 dopamine receptor could underlie the aetiology of a number of pathologies including alcoholism, substance dependence, and schizophrenia. Three polymorphic variants of the D2 receptor have been identified in the coding region, encoding the amino acid substitutions Val96Ala, Pro310Ser and Ser311Cys (D2Cys311) (Gejman et al., 1994). Previously, the D2Cys311 receptor has been reported to exhibit a decreased ability to mediate the inhibition of adenylyl cyclase (Cravchik et al., 1996). The amino acid substitution in the D2Cys311 receptor occurs in the third intracellular loop of the D2 receptor. As the third intracellular loop is an important region for determining the coupling of GPCRs to G proteins (Strader et al., 1987), the reduced ability of D2Cys311 to inhibit adenylyl cyclase could, therefore, be related to impaired coupling to G proteins.

In the present study, we have compared the coupling of D2S and D2SCys311 receptor variants to different G proteins by co-expression with the G protein subunits 12 and either o, i1, i2 and i3 in insect cells. The functional coupling of the receptors to these G proteins has been investigated using [35S]GTPS binding assays and dopamine/[3H]spiperone competition binding assays. This is the first time that the coupling specificity of the D2S receptor for different G proteins has been investigated thoroughly and the first time that the G protein coupling specificity of the D2SCys311 receptor has been investigated.

2. Methods

2.1. Materials

[Phenyl-4-3H]-spiperone (0.5–1.1 TBq mmol−1) was obtained from Amersham (Amersham, Bucks, UK) and [35S]-GTPS (37–55 TBq mmol−1) was obtained from NEN DuPont (Stevenage, Herts, UK) All other chemicals were from Sigma (Poole, Dorset, UK). Oligonucleotides were synthesised and desalted by Invitrogen (Carlsbad, CA, USA). The baculoviruses expressing the human G protein subunits o, i1, i2, i3, 1 and 2 were generously donated by Dr. T Kozasa (University of Illinois, Chicago, USA).

2.2. Construction of baculovirus expressing epitope tagged D2 receptors

The baculovirus encoding the D2S receptor was constructed as described in Nickolls and Strange (2003). Baculovirus encoding the D2SCys311 receptor was constructed using the following method. D2SCys311 receptor DNA was amplified using primers designed to add a NotI site, a start codon and the FLAG tag to the 5′ end of the receptor and an EcoRI site to the 3′ end. The PCR product was subcloned into pGem-T® (Promega, Southampton, UK) to produce the plasmid pGem–D2Cys311. After sequencing, the tagged receptor was excised from pGem with NotI and EcoRI and ligated into the plasmid pVL1392 (Pharmingen, San Diego, CA, USA), to produce the plasmid pV-FD2Cys311. The plasmid was then recombined with Baculogold™ DNA (Pharmingen) to produce recombinant viruses.

All viruses were purified using plaque purification and amplified according to the methods of King and Possee (1992).

2.3. Cell culture

Suspension cultures of Spodoptera frugiperda (Sf9) cells were grown in TC-100 medium containing 10% (v/v) foetal calf serum and 0.1 % (v/v) pluronic F68®. Cells were seeded in suspension at a density of between 0.3 and 0.6×106 cells ml−1, and allowed to grow until they reached a density of 1×106 cells ml−1 (log phase). The cells were then infected with either a multiplicity of infection (moi) of three for receptor virus or 3:6:6:6 for receptor::: viruses and harvested 48 h after infection. Additional D2S/o and D2S/i2 preparations were made by varying the moi of both R and G to allow the effects of varying receptor levels to be ascertained.

2.4. Membrane preparation

Cells were collected by centrifugation for 10 min at 1700×g. For every 50 ml of cell suspension used, 5 ml of ice-cold buffer (20 mM HEPES, 6 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, pH 7.4) was used to resuspend the cell pellet, which was transferred to a Dounce glass:glass homogeniser. The cells were homogenised at 4 °C by 40 strokes of the Dounce homogeniser. The homogenate was centrifuged for 10 min at 1700×g, the supernatant was collected and centrifuged at 48000×g (4 °C) for 1 h. The resulting pellet was resuspended in buffer and aliquots of 250 l were stored at −80 °C. The method of Lowry et al. (1951) was used to determine the protein content of membrane preparations, using bovine serum albumin as standard.

2.5. Saturation binding assays using [3H]-spiperone

Cell membranes (25 g protein) were incubated in triplicate with [3H]-spiperone (0.001–2 nM) in a total volume of 1 ml buffer (20 mM HEPES, 6 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, pH 7.4). Non-specific binding was determined by the inclusion of 3 M (+)-butaclamol. The reaction was initiated by the addition of membranes and the tubes were incubated at 25 °C for 3 h. The reaction was terminated by rapid filtration using a Brandel cell harvester with four 3 ml washes of ice-cold PBS (140 mM NaCl, 10 mM KCl, 1.5 mM KH2PO4, 8 mM Na2HPO4). The filters were soaked in 2 ml scintillation fluid overnight before the amount of radioactivity present was determined by liquid scintillation spectrometry.

2.6. Dopamine competition versus [3H]-spiperone binding

Cell membranes (25 g protein) were incubated with 0.3 nM [3H]-spiperone and various concentrations of dopamine, in triplicate, in a final volume of 1 ml buffer (20 mM HEPES, 6 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA, 1 mM DTT, pH 7.4). Non-specific binding was determined by the inclusion of 3 M (+)-butaclamol. The reactions were initiated, incubated and terminated as described above. The effect of guanine nucleotides on dopamine binding was assessed by the addition of 100 mM NaCl and 100 M GTP to the buffer. The addition of DTT to the assays as an antioxidant did not affect the level of [3H]spiperone binding observed.

2.7. [35S]-GTPS binding assays

Cell membranes (5 g) were incubated in 80 l buffer (20 mM HEPES, 100 mM NaCl, 6 mM MgCl2, 1 mM DTT, pH 7.4) containing 1 M GDP and various concentrations of agonist. All experiments were performed in duplicate. The reaction was initiated by the addition of cell membranes and incubated at 30 °C for 30 min. A 20 l volume of [35S]-GTPS (0.2 nM final concentration) was then added and the incubation continued for a further 30 min. The reaction was terminated by rapid filtration with a Brandel cell harvester and four 1.5 ml washes with ice-cold PBS. Radioactivity was determined as described for saturation analysis.

2.8. Isotopic dilution GTPS saturation binding

The number of G proteins in infected Sf9 cells was determined by [35S]-GTPS isotopic dilution saturation binding. This method is based on the fact that if insect cells are infected at the same time, one flask with G protein subunits (D2x12) and one without (D212), the increase in [35S]-GTPS binding in the first compared to the second is due to expression of the G subunits. Cell membranes (25 g) were incubated for 30 min at 30 °C, in triplicate in a final volume of 1 ml of buffer (20 mM HEPES, 100 mM NaCl, 6 mM MgCl2, pH 7.4) containing 0.1 nM [35S]-GTPS. Binding of [35S]-GTPS was inhibited with unlabelled GTPS (0.05–50 nM). Reactions were initiated by the addition of membranes. Termination and determination of radioactivity was as for [3H]-spiperone saturation analysis. The number of G proteins was calculated by the following equation:


[35S]-GTPSbound is the specific binding of [35S]-GTPS determined by subtraction as above, GTPStotal is the total concentration of radioactive and non-radioactive GTPS combined and [35S]-GTPSconc is the concentration of [35S]-GTPS. Hyperbolic saturation curves were then generated to determine the apparent Bmax of functional G protein in the preparations containing G subunits.

2.9. Data analysis

Data were analysed using PRISM (GraphPAD Software Inc., San Diego, CA) and statistical significance determined using either Student’s t-test or ANOVA followed by the Tukey post hoc analysis as appropriate. P<0.05 determined statistical significance. Statistical analyses of binding affinities were performed on the pKi values as these are normally distributed.

3. Results

In order to examine the ability of D2S and D2SCys311 to activate the G protein subunits, o, i1, i2 and i3, each of the receptors were (a) expressed alone, (b) co-expressed with 12 G protein subunits and (c) co-expressed with 12 G protein subunits and one of the G subunits, to produce six different membrane preparations for each receptor variant. Data on preparations D2S/o(preparation 1) and D2S/i2 (preparation 1) have been published previously in Nickolls and Strange (2003), but are included to allow comparison with the data for the mutant receptor. Additional D2S/o and D2S/i2 preparations were made by varying the moi of R::: used in the infections to allow the effect of differing receptor expression levels and R:G ratios to be ascertained (D2S/o (preparation 2) and D2S/i2 (preparation 2)). Receptor levels in the co-expressing preparations were quantified using [3H]-spiperone saturation analysis (Table 1). Saturation analysis of [3H]-spiperone binding to D2 receptors expressed in insect cells gave a Kd of 89 pM (pKd 10.05±0.12, mean±S.E.M., n=3) for D2S receptors and 60 pM (pKd 10.22±0.07, mean±S.E.M., n=3) for D2SCys311 receptors. These values were unaffected by the co-expression of G proteins (Table 1). The [3H]-spiperone saturation binding data revealed that in preparations containing the same G subunit, the expression levels of the D2S receptor were higher than those of the D2Cys311 receptor, although this was not significant for the o preparations.

Table 1. Expression levels of D2 receptors and G proteins

D2 receptor expression levels were measured using saturation binding with [3H]-spiperone as described in Methods section and G protein levels were measured using isotopic dilution GTPS saturation studies as described in Methods section. Data from both assays were fitted best by a one-binding site model (P<0.05) from which Kd (nM) and Bmax (pmol mg−1) values were determined. Values are expressed as mean±S.E.M. of three to four independent experiments.

G protein levels were measured by isotopic dilution GTPS saturation analysis. This method has been used before (Nickolls and Strange, 2003) and shown to give similar results to western blot densitometry analysis. Time-course experiments showed that the t1/2 for association of 100 pM [35S]GTPS to Go was 0.98±0.42 min and for Gi2 was 3.63±1.78 min (n=3). It was, therefore, considered that a 30 min incubation time was adequate for saturation experiments to reach equilibrium. In these studies, there were no appreciable differences between the affinities of the different G subunits for GTPS (Table 1). Furthermore, although there was some variation in the expression levels of the G proteins, there was no significant difference between individual G protein levels between D2S and D2Scys311 preparations (P>0.05, Student’s t-test).

In the preparations lacking co-expressed G protein subunits, analysis of dopamine/[3H]-spiperone competition binding data revealed that dopamine was able to bind to the D2 receptor isoforms with both high and low affinity (Kh and Kl) (Table 2, Fig. 1). Analysis of dopamine/[3H]-spiperone competition binding to membranes from cells expressing D2 receptors co-expressed with G protein subunits revealed an increase in the percentage of high affinity sites labelled by dopamine, compared to preparations expressing the receptor alone (P>0.05, ANOVA). This is consistent with interaction between the exogenously expressed D2 receptor and G proteins. Neither the identity of the D2 receptor variant nor the G subunit had any significant influence on the percentage of sites exhibiting high affinity for dopamine (P>0.05, ANOVA), or the affinity of dopamine for the low affinity state of the receptors (Kl) (P>0.05, ANOVA). However, co-expression of Gi3 with the D2S receptor led to a decrease in the affinity of dopamine for the high affinity state of the receptor (Kh), compared to when this receptor was expressed alone or with Go or Gi2 (P<0.05, ANOVA followed by the Tukey post hoc analysis).

Table 2. Analysis of dopamine binding using competition versus [3H]-spiperone in membranes expressing either D2S, or D2SCys311 receptors

Dopamine/[3H]-spiperone competition experiments were performed as described in Methods section. Competition data in the absence of GTP and sodium ions were fitted best by a two-binding site model (P<0.05) and values for the high affinity state (Kh), the low affinity state (Kl) and the percentage of receptors exhibiting high affinity for dopamine (%Rh) are given. In the presence of GTP (100 M) and sodium ions (100 mM), data fitted best to a one-binding site model (P<0.05) and the values for the single affinity state (KiGTP) are given. Values are expressed as mean±S.E.M. of four independent experiments. There was no significant difference in values for Kh, Kl, %Rh or KiGTP between D2S and D2Scys311 receptors (P>0.05, Student’s t-test). ANOVA analysis revealed no significant influence of G protein on either Kl or KiGTP values and Kl and KiGTP values did not differ significantly (P<0.05, ANOVA). ANOVA analysis also revealed that there was no significant influence of the G proteins on the Kh of dopamine for the D2sCys311 receptor (P>0.05) but that G proteins did influence the Kh of dopamine in the D2s preparations with preparations containing Go and Gi2 exhibiting higher affinity for dopamine than the Gi3 preparation (P<0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis) (denoted by * in the table).

In order to ascertain the effect of guanine nucleotides on agonist binding to the D2 dopamine receptors, dopamine/[3H]spiperone competition studies were performed in the presence of GTP and sodium ions, as it has been reported that the inclusion of sodium ions is necessary for the complete conversion of brain D2 receptors from the high to the low affinity states in the presence of GTP (Grigoriadis and Seeman, 1985). In the presence of sodium ions and GTP, dopamine binding to all preparations was fitted best by assuming a single receptor state (KiGTP). In all preparations KiGTP was not significantly different to Kl (P>0.05, ANOVA). This indicates that the binding of dopamine to the lower affinity state is unaffected by addition of sodium ions to the buffers and also shows that high affinity agonist binding observed in the receptor-alone preparations was due to the receptor coupling to endogenous insect cell G proteins.

However, despite the presence of high affinity GTP-sensitive agonist binding to the receptors, in the absence of co-expressed G protein subunits, dopamine was unable to stimulate [35S]-GTPS binding to endogenous G subunits through either D2S or D2SCys311 receptors (Fig. 2). It was, therefore, concluded that there was only a weak unproductive interaction between exogenous D2 receptors and endogenous insect cell G proteins.

The structurally dissimilar dopaminergic agonists dopamine, quinpirole and (+)-3-PPP (Fig. 3) were all able to stimulate [35S]-GTPS binding in a concentration dependent manner in all membrane preparations co-expressing G subunits (Fig. 2 and Fig. 4), consistent with interaction between exogenously expressed D2 receptors and G proteins. The maximal effect of dopamine was higher in preparations with high receptor expression (compare D2S/o preparation 1 with 2 and D2S/i2 preparation 1 with 2), but conversely, the potency of dopamine was not appreciably affected by the change in receptor expression level or R:G ratio (Table 3). Furthermore, there was no appreciable difference in the relative efficacy or potency of either (+)-3-PPP or quinpirole between the different preparations that expressed the D2S receptor with the same G subunit. This phenomenon was also true for other D2S preparations made, which exhibited R:G ratios from 1:2.7 to 1:9.2 (data not shown).

Investigation of the influence of the G subunit on agonist efficacy and potency revealed that whichever D2 receptor subtype was expressed the potencies of dopamine and (+)-3PPP were significantly affected by the identity of the G subunit (P<0.05, ANOVA), but the potency of quinpirole was not (P>0.05, ANOVA). Analysis of the D2S receptor data revealed that dopamine, (+)-3PPP and quinprole exhibited similar rank orders of potency with Go>Gi2=Gi1>Gi3. Dopamine potency at D2S/Go was significantly higher (P<0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis) than at the D2S/Gi preparations. The potency of (+)-3PPP at D2S/Go was significantly higher (P<0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis) than at D2S/Gi1 and D2S/Gi3. However, the difference in the potency of quinpirole at D2S/Go compared to D2S/Gi3, was only 3.4-fold (not significant, P>0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis) contrasting with a 15.5- and 13.2-fold difference in selectivity demonstrated by dopamine and (+)-3PPP, respectively.

Statistical analysis revealed that there was no difference in agonist potency or relative efficacy of (+)-3-PPP and quinpirole between D2S and D2Scys311 preparations containing the same G protein (P>0.05 Student’s t-test). The efficacy of dopamine was decreased in the D2Scys311 preparations compared to the D2S preparation, but this was probably related to receptor expression level. Nevertheless the D2Scys311 receptor appeared to discriminate less well between the G proteins compared to the D2S receptor. At the D2SCys311 receptor, the rank order of dopamine potency was Go>Gi1>Gi2=Gi3. However, for this receptor, although the identity of the G protein affected signalling, the difference in potency for dopamine between individual G protein subtypes was not significant (P>0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis), even though there was a 3.9-fold difference between the potency at D2SCys311/Go compared to D2SCys311/Gi3. (+)-3PPP exhibited a rank order of potency Go>Gi1=Gi2>Gi3, and the potency at D2SCys311/Go was significantly higher than at the Gi subtypes, with a 9.6-fold difference (P<0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis). Again the potency of quinpirole was not significantly influenced by the co-expression of different G proteins (P>0.05, ANOVA), only exhibiting a 2.1-fold difference between Go and Gi3 and a 3.0-fold difference between Gi1 and Gi3.

The relative efficacy of the agonists (+)-3PPP and quinpirole was also influenced by the identity of the co-expressed G proteins (P<0.05, ANOVA). The Tukey post hoc analysis showed that in the D2S preparations, (+)-3PPP exhibited higher relative efficacy in preparations expressing Go compared to preparations expressing the three Gi subtypes (P<0.05). Also, the relative efficacy of quinpirole varied significantly between membrane preparations containing D2SCys311/Gi1 and D2SCys311/Gi2 (P<0.05, Tukey post hoc analysis).

4. Discussion

We have recently analysed the ability of the rat D2L dopamine receptor (Cordeaux et al., 2001) and the human D2S dopamine receptor (Nickolls and Strange, 2003) to couple to o and i2 G protein subunits and the ability of the human D2L dopamine receptor to couple to o, i1, i2 and i3 G protein subtypes (Gazi et al., 2003). In this study, this work is extended to compare the coupling profile of the human D2S receptor with Go, Gi1, Gi2 and Gi3 proteins and to investigate the ability of a natural polymorphism of the human D2S receptor (D2SCys311) to couple to these G proteins. Three agonists have been selected for this study. The natural ligand dopamine was used. The agonists quinpirole and (+)-3PPP were also chosen, firstly because they differ structurally (Fig. 3), and secondly because they exhibit a reversal of efficacy between D2 receptors in different locations (striatum and pituitary gland) (Meller et al., 1992).

Preparations of D2S and D2SCys311 receptors with the four different G proteins were made and, for some R/G combinations, preparations with different receptor levels and R:G ratios were made. The receptor–G protein (R:G) ratio has been reported to influence both agonist efficacy and potency (Newman-Tancredi et al., 1997 and Gazi et al., 1999). In the present study, the receptor expression level did seem to influence the maximal [35S]GTPS response but the R:G ratio did not affect either the relative efficacy of agonists or their potency. Similar observations have been reported by Gazi et al. (2003). This may be due to compartmentalisation of receptor and G proteins in to microdomains, leading to a different R:G stoichiometry in the microdomain as compared to measurements in the membrane as a whole. Further evidence for this is suggested by the fact that not all the D2 receptors can bind agonist with high affinity, even though in all cases G protein levels are in excess of receptor levels. The receptor levels reported in this study are similar to other reports of D2 receptor expression in insect cells (Ng et al., 1997 and Grunewald et al., 1996) and the G protein levels are similar to those reported in both recombinant and native cell systems ( Neubig et al., 1985; Kim et al., 1994 and Ransnas and Insel, 1988)

The binding of dopamine to D2S and D2SCys311 receptors expressed without exogenous G protein subunits was guanine nucleotide sensitive, exhibiting two affinity states in the absence and a single affinity state in the presence of GTP and sodium ions. These observations are consistent with the ternary complex model of R–G interaction (De Lean et al., 1982 and Wreggett and De Lean, 1984) and indicate that the receptor is able to couple to endogenous insect cell G proteins. However, dopamine was unable to stimulate [35S]-GTPS binding to endogenous insect cell G protein subunits, so that interaction of R and G apparent in ligand binding does not result in function. These properties render the system very useful for functional studies of receptor–G protein reconstitution.

The results of this study show that the agonists dopamine, (+)-3PPP and quinpirole are able to select particular receptor states, a phenomenon described as agonist trafficking (Kenakin, 1995). Dopamine and (+)-3PPP clearly couple the receptor variants preferentially to Go over Gi subtypes, based on agonist potency, but quinpirole does not. (+)-3PPP exhibits a consistent potency difference for signalling via Go and Gi3 ranging from 9- (D2Scys311) to 27-fold (D2S). Similarly, the fold difference of potency for dopamine between these two G proteins is 15.5-fold for the D2S receptor and 3.9-fold for the D2SCys311 receptor. However, the type of G protein present does not significantly alter the potency of quinpirole, with the potency values between the G proteins only varying 2.1- (D2Scys311) to 3.9-fold (D2S). Structurally both dopamine and (+)-3PPP contain phenolic hydroxyl groups whereas quinpirole does not. These hydroxyl groups are proposed to interact with serine residues in TM5 of the dopamine receptor (Woodward et al., 1996) and this could explain why dopamine and (+)-3PPP have a similar pattern of G protein activation, which differs from that of the structurally very different agonist quinpirole.

This study adds to the growing consensus that GPCRs are able to activate different pathways dependent on the agonist bound. This concept began with initial studies on the Drosophila tyramine receptor (Robb et al., 1994), the PACAP receptor ( Rampelbergh et al., 1996), the 5-HT1A serotonin receptor (Millan et al., 1993) and the muscarinic acetylcholine receptor ( Gurwitz et al., 1994) and since then many other receptors including 2-adrenergic (Brink et al., 2000), calcitonin ( Watson et al., 2000), 2-adrenergic (Wenzel-Seifert and Seifert, 2000), D2 dopamine (Lawler et al., 1999), 5-HT2A/2C receptors (Berg et al., 1998) and neuropeptide receptors ( Jarpe et al., 1998) have been reported to exhibit agonist-directed receptor signalling. However, the mechanisms behind this are poorly understood, in particular, the question of whether agonists stabilise pre-existing states or whether agonist binding causes conformational selection of receptor states remains. Our previous work ( Nickolls and Strange, 2003) provided evidence that D2S precouples to Go, but not Gi2, in the absence of agonist. It seems that the receptor can exist in different conformations, dependent on the G protein, in the absence of agonist and binding of the agonist is able to stabilise further receptor states.

The affinity of dopamine for the high affinity state of the D2S receptor when it is co-expressed with Gi3 is lower than when it is co-expressed with any of the other G proteins, and this translates into a reduction in dopamine functional potency, albeit not efficacy. Conversely, the differences in the potency of dopamine when the D2 receptor is co-expressed with Go or Gi1/2 are not reflected in the affinity of dopamine for the high affinity state of the receptor, so that for these G proteins, the ability of dopamine to differentially activate the G proteins must be due to events beyond agonist binding.

It has been suggested in a previous report (Yang and Lanier, 1999) that agonists exhibit higher efficacy and/or potency at Go over Gi subtypes due to the lower affinity of Go for GDP (Ferguson et al., 1986), leading to Go being "primed for action". This could lead to differences in maximal responses for full agonists signalling via different G proteins and differences in relative efficacies/potencies for agonists signalling via different G proteins. Other examples in the literature, however, have reported agonists which display higher efficacy and/or potency at Gi subtypes compared to Go (Lorenzen et al., 1998), suggesting that the affinity of a G protein for GDP does not necessarily affect the ability of a receptor to activate it.

Natural polymorphisms of the dopamine receptors have been implicated in the aetiology of a number of disease states. However, reports implicating the Ser311Cys polymorphism of the D2 receptor in diseases have so far been inconclusive and contradictory. Serretti et al. (1998) and Arinami et al. (1994) have reported an association between the Ser311Cys polymorphism and schizophrenia. A study by Spurlock et al. (1998) did not, however, replicate these findings. Due to the low frequency of the D2Cys311 allele in the population, finding people homozygous for the mutation is unlikely. Therefore, conclusive data on the role, if any, of this mutation, in the aetiology of diseases are difficult to obtain. Thus, investigation of the pharmacological properties of this receptor may reveal whether it is likely to have a significant effect on the human dopaminergic system in vivo.

Previously, Cravchik et al. (1996) have reported that dopamine acting through the D2SCys311 receptor exhibited a reduced ability to inhibit forskolin-stimulated adenylate cyclase, but little difference in functional potency. In the present study, the D2S receptor exhibited a greater maximal stimulation of [35S]-GTPS binding by dopamine, in the preparations expressing Gi1 and Gi3, compared to the D2Scys311 receptor. This phenomenon was also observed when comparing D2Cys311 preparations with the D2S/Go (1) and D2S/Gi2 (1) preparations, but not in the D2S/Go (2) and D2S/Gi2 (2) which express the D2 receptor at a lower level. Therefore, it is considered that the lower expression level is the causative factor in the reduction in dopamine maximal effect at the D2SCys311 receptor. Although, in this investigation, we have found no significant differences in the potencies of dopamine, (+)-3PPP or quinpirole at D2S receptors compared to D2SCys311 receptors, the selectivity of dopamine and (+)-3PPP for Go over Gi3 is much more marked at the D2S receptor compared to D2Cys311 receptor and this difference was not attenuated by reducing the expression level of the D2S receptor, suggesting that there is some effect of the Ser311Cys mutation on the G protein coupling of the D2 receptor.

In conclusion, we have demonstrated that the naturally occurring dopamine D2 receptor mutation D2 Ser311Cys does not have any appreciable effect on the G protein coupling profile of the receptor. However, we have demonstrated that structurally different agonists acting through the D2 receptor are able to select different receptor states.
